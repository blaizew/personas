### Module 16: Negotiating Over Email, Text, or Written Communication

Written communication strips away 93% of your total signal (tone and body language), leaving only words. Voss treats this as a constraint that demands deliberate technique adaptation -- not a reason to avoid negotiating in writing, but a reason to be more surgical about what you send.

#### Chess by Text -- One Move Per Message [4/16] [SIGNATURE]

**Core principle:** In written communication, make exactly one point per message. Never bundle multiple moves together.

**Exact quote:** "If you were playing chess by text, would you put seven moves in your text? No, you'd only put one move in."
*Source: huberman*

**What it means in practice:** People instinctively over-bundle in email and text. They include the greeting, the context, the ask, the justification, two follow-up questions, and a closing -- all in one message. This comes across as cold, overwhelming, and impossible to respond to without a significant time investment. The recipient's cognitive load skyrockets and response likelihood plummets.

Instead, treat each message like a chess move: make one point, then wait for the response before making the next. This creates a conversational rhythm even in asynchronous communication.

**Why it works:** Written communication lacks the natural turn-taking cadence of voice conversation. When you bundle seven points, the reader has no way to signal which point landed, which confused them, and which they want to push back on. One move per message restores that signal.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User says "I sent them a long email and never heard back"
- User is drafting a multi-paragraph message with several asks
- User is negotiating terms back and forth via email and hitting impasses
- User asks how to handle a counterpart who "went dark" after a detailed proposal

**Practical application:** When you need to cover multiple topics, break them into separate messages spaced 5-10 minutes apart. Each message gets its own response, creating momentum. The alternative -- one massive email covering everything -- creates decision paralysis and often gets deferred to "when I have time to think about all of this."

#### No-Oriented Questions in Writing [6/16]

**Core principle:** No-oriented questions are even more powerful in text than in voice because they don't require tone to carry the message -- the structure itself does the work.

**Exact quote:** "Have you given up on [specific thing]?"
*Source: black-swan-newsletter*

**Additional quote:** "Is it a ridiculous idea..." works in cold email.
*Source: lewis-howes*

**What it means in practice:** In voice conversation, your FM DJ tone helps no-oriented questions land softly. In writing, you don't have that cushion -- but you also don't need it. The written form of "Have you given up on...?" triggers loss aversion just as powerfully through structure alone. The recipient reads those words and their brain fires the same "I'm not a quitter" response regardless of delivery.

**Key no-oriented patterns for written communication:**
- "Have you given up on [project/deal/relationship]?" -- for ghosters
- "Is it a ridiculous idea to [proposal]?" -- for cold outreach
- "Would it be horrible if [request]?" -- for permission asks
- "Is now a bad time to talk?" -- to initiate a phone call from text

**Why this works better in writing:** In person, yes-oriented questions ("Would you like to...?") can ride on warmth and relationship to succeed. In writing, without that warmth, yes-oriented questions feel transactional and triggering. No-oriented questions feel safe in any medium because the safety comes from the structure, not the tone.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is writing cold outreach emails
- User needs to reengage someone who stopped responding
- User is asking for something via email and wants it to land well
- User is following up after a proposal with no response

#### The "Is Now a Bad Time" Text [4/16] [SIGNATURE]

**Core principle:** A two-line text can set up a phone conversation that resolves complex issues in under 10 minutes.

**Exact quote:** "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear."
*Source: huberman*

**Full context from the Nick Nanton story:** Voss needed to reopen a settled agreement with filmmaker Nick Nanton about a documentary. He sent a two-line text on a Sunday: "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear." Nick immediately responded he was on a Zoom call, would call in 30 minutes. "He already knows he ain't going to like what he's going to hear. I'm prepping him for bad news."

**What it means in practice:** This is a text-to-phone bridge. You're not negotiating in text -- you're using text to set up the optimal conditions for a phone call. The two lines accomplish three things simultaneously:
1. "Is now a bad time" gives them permission to say no (no-oriented), producing either immediate attention or a scheduled callback
2. "Something you don't want to hear" inoculates them against the negative -- their imagination conjures something worse than reality
3. The brevity signals respect for their time while creating urgency

**When NOT to use:** Don't use this for trivial asks or good news. It's specifically for when you need to reopen something, deliver difficult information, or have a conversation the other person won't want to have. Overuse dilutes the power.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User needs to reopen a settled agreement
- User needs to deliver bad news and wants to do it efficiently
- User wants to move a stalled email thread to a phone call
- User is avoiding a difficult conversation and needs a structured opening

#### Under-Promise Time ("I Need 13 Minutes") [3/16]

**Core principle:** Ask for an odd, specific amount of time. Finish under it. They associate you with giving time back.

**Exact quote:** "I call you on the phone, I say, 'Louis, I need 13 minutes.' You give me 15 because you ain't got 13 on your calendar. I get done at 9... you just gave me back time in my day. Everybody else is taking it away. Now you love me."
*Source: lewis-howes*

**What it means in practice:** This technique is primarily used in written communication to request calls or meetings. The odd number (13, not 15; 7, not 10) signals precision -- you've actually thought about how long this will take rather than rounding to the nearest standard block. Then finishing early creates a positive association: you're the person who gives time back rather than stealing it.

**Why odd numbers work:** Round numbers (15 min, 30 min, 1 hour) feel like generic time blocks anyone would request. Precise numbers (13 minutes, 22 minutes) feel calculated and honest -- like you've actually mapped what the conversation requires. It builds credibility before you even start talking.

**Application in email/text:** When requesting a call, never write "Do you have time to chat?" or "Can we schedule a call?" Instead: "I need 13 minutes. When works for you?" This communicates: I won't waste your time, I know exactly what I need, and I'll be out of your hair fast.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User wants to schedule a call with someone busy
- User is following up on an email and wants to move to phone
- User's meetings/calls consistently run over and damage relationships
- User wants to be the person people pick up the phone for

#### Labels Work in Text [3/16]

**Core principle:** "It seems like..." and "It sounds like..." work in written form. Written labels are actually safer because you can craft them perfectly.

**Exact quote from the newsletter:** Labels are described as "cognitive tools. When you put words to an unspoken emotion, especially a negative one, you create a release valve."
*Source: black-swan-newsletter*

**What it means in practice:** Many people assume labels only work face-to-face because they seem to require warmth and tone. But written labels have a distinct advantage: you can revise them until they're precise. In conversation, you get one shot. In email, you can craft "It seems like this project has been frustrating for your team" with surgical accuracy.

The recipient still feels understood. The neurochemical response (cortisol reduction, oxytocin release) still occurs when someone reads words that accurately describe their emotional state.

**How to use labels in email:**
- Open with one: "It seems like you've been dealing with a lot of competing priorities this quarter."
- Use mid-message to acknowledge the difficulty of your ask: "I know this probably feels like one more thing on your plate."
- Close with one: "It sounds like finding the right timing matters more to you than the specifics."

**Caution:** In text, avoid labels that could read as passive-aggressive without tone. "It seems like you don't care about this" reads very differently in text than it sounds in a warm voice. Stick to labels that demonstrate understanding rather than accusations disguised as labels.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is emailing someone they've never met and wants to build rapport
- User's emails feel transactional and they want more connection
- User needs to acknowledge a difficult situation before making an ask
- User wants to write a follow-up that shows they were actually listening

#### The 7-38-55 Problem in Written Communication [5/16]

**Core principle:** Text removes 93% of your communication. You must compensate deliberately.

**Exact quote (from the Elon Musk story):** "They're on a phone, and Elon goes, 'you're fired!' And then everybody in the room with him can see that he's joking. But the person on the other side can't, and they all go to where, wait, wait, wait, wait, they can't see your look on your face right now, you gotta stop."
*Source: lex-fridman*

**What it means in practice:** The 7-38-55 rule (7% words, 38% tone, 55% body language) means written communication carries only the 7%. Humor, sarcasm, gentleness, and playfulness all travel through tone and body -- stripped away in text, they often land as confusion or hostility.

**Compensating strategies:**
1. Use explicit labels to convey what your tone would normally communicate: "I'm not trying to put pressure here -- I'm genuinely curious..."
2. Over-acknowledge: where you'd normally smile to signal goodwill, write it out instead
3. Keep messages short and single-purpose (chess by text) to reduce the surface area for misinterpretation
4. When in doubt about how something reads, move to phone: "Is now a bad time to talk?"

**The practical upshot:** Never write anything in text that depends on tone to not be offensive. If it would need a smile, a gentle voice, or a wink to land correctly, it doesn't belong in text. Say it on the phone instead.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User's text messages or emails are creating friction or being misread
- User wants to be funny/playful in writing and it keeps backfiring
- User is in a text exchange that's escalating and needs to de-escalate
- User asks whether to handle something over text vs. phone

#### Stories

**Nick Nanton Two-Line Sunday Text**
> Voss screened his company's documentary "Tactical Empathy" in Vegas and told filmmaker Nick Nanton he loved it. Two days later, he realizes there's a major omission -- his team member Derek isn't in the film. He needs to reopen a closed conversation. Strategy: "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear." Two-line Sunday text. Nick immediately responds he's on a Zoom call, will call in 30 minutes. "He already knows he ain't going to like what he's going to hear. I'm prepping him for bad news." On the call: "Look, I know what I said. We got a problem." Done in under ten minutes. Nick calls back the next day with a favor to ask -- "You got it. Doesn't matter what it is."
*Teaching application:* The complete structure for text-initiated renegotiation -- (1) two-line text sets context, (2) flag bad news coming, (3) get straight to the problem, (4) be in problem-solving mode immediately. Never do the "how are you? how are the kids?" preamble before dropping bad news in text.
*Source:* huberman

**The Ghost Reengagement Sequence**
> From the Black Swan newsletter: "Have you given up on [specific thing]?" ... "After they respond (and they will respond quickly -- be ready), you must do a Summary of their perspective to get a 'that's right.'" The sequence: (1) Send: "Have you given up on [specific thing]?" (2) Wait -- they will respond quickly because "nobody wants to be a quitter. The word 'surrender' is baked into our DNA as something to resist." (3) Deliver a Summary of their perspective (4) Get "that's right" (5) Proceed.
*Teaching application:* "Have you given up on X?" is optimized for email/text because the loss-aversion trigger doesn't need tone to carry it. The structure alone does the work. Use when someone has ghosted after a proposal, stopped responding mid-negotiation, or dropped off communication.
*Source:* black-swan-newsletter

**The Elon Musk "You're Fired" Conference Call**
> Voss's contacts among SpaceX/Tesla senior executives told him: "They're on this conference call, just words, and a guy on the other end of the line says something that was wrong but wasn't bad. And so they said, they're on a phone, and Elon goes, 'you're fired!' And then everybody in the room with him can see that he's joking. But the person on the other side can't, and they all go to where, wait, wait, wait, wait, they can't see your look on your face right now, you gotta stop, you gotta stop, because the guy on the other side is dying right now."
*Teaching application:* Illustrates the 7-38-55 rule catastrophically. Words that are clearly a joke with visual context become genuinely traumatic without it. Deploy when discussing remote communication, the limits of text/phone, and why humor/sarcasm should never be attempted in writing.
*Source:* lex-fridman
