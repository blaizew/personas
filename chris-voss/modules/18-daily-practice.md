### Module 18: Daily Practice and Maintaining Negotiation Skills

Negotiation is not a knowledge problem -- it's a skill problem. You don't maintain it by reading about it. You maintain it through daily repetitions in low-stakes interactions that keep the neural pathways greased for when the high-stakes moment arrives. Even Voss, with 24+ years of experience, has to actively practice or his skills decay without him knowing.

#### Negotiation Is a Perishable Skill [12/16] [EMPHATIC]

**Core principle:** Skills decay without daily use. You won't know they've decayed until a high-stakes moment exposes the rust.

**Exact quote:** "It ain't riding a bike."
*Source: lewis-howes*

**Additional quote:** "Negotiation's a perishable skill."
*Source: diary-of-a-ceo*

**Additional quote:** "A year later, my supervisor said 'that was horrible' -- I didn't know my skills had decayed."
*Source: knowledge-project (suicide hotline performance review story)*

**What it means in practice:** This is Voss's most emphatic and frequently repeated point about skill maintenance. After completing his initial training and performing well on the hotline, a year passed. He answered a call that seemed successful -- the caller even congratulated him. His supervisor Jim listened in on the call for the annual review. Voss walked back feeling great. Jim said: "That was horrible." Voss was shocked: "Did you just hear that guy? He congratulated me!" Jim was right at every step.

The terrifying insight: **you don't know when your skills have decayed.** The degradation is invisible to you because you're using your deteriorated skills as the measuring instrument. Only external feedback or a failed high-stakes moment reveals the gap.

Voss describes literally standing outside a hotel psyching himself up because he's out of practice: "I have to actively maintain my skills. He describes literally standing outside a hotel psyching himself up because he's out of practice." [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY exact wording from lewis-howes]

#### Small-Stakes Practice for High-Stakes Results [12/16] [EMPHATIC]

**Core principle:** Every human interaction is a practice rep. Use Lyft drivers, TSA agents, baristas, and hotel clerks as your training ground.

**Exact quote:** "I practice on Lyft drivers, TSA agents, baristas, hotel clerks."
*Source: lex-fridman*

**Additional quote:** "There's never a single conversation where you can't gain an edge."
*Source: jordan-678*

**What it means in practice:** The gap between "knowing the techniques" and "deploying them under pressure" can only be bridged through repetition. But you don't need high-stakes situations to practice -- every single human interaction qualifies:

- The barista: practice your "what do you love about working here?" state-change question
- The hotel check-in: practice the accusations audit before asking for an upgrade
- The TSA agent: practice a label ("tough day?" or "just another day, huh?")
- The Lyft driver: practice mirroring and genuine curiosity
- The customer service call: practice predicting their reality before stating yours

These aren't just exercises -- they produce real results. The Macy's 30% discount, the TSA agent who cut Voss to the front of the line, the lost luggage clerk who walked into the bowels of the airport -- all happened during "practice" interactions.

**The key mindset shift:** Don't think of these as "wasting" techniques on unimportant interactions. Think of them as the only way to ensure the techniques will be available when something important happens. "The Blue Angels build habits on the ground so they don't crash in the sky."

**Coaching triggers:**
- User asks where to practice negotiation skills
- User says they don't have enough "real" negotiations to stay sharp
- User distinguishes between "practice" and "real" situations (this distinction is wrong)
- User wants to build confidence before a big negotiation

#### Shu-Ha-Ri Mastery Progression [5/16]

**Core principle:** Skills develop in three stages: rigid execution, adaptive feel, and transcendent improvisation. You must progress through all three -- no shortcuts.

**Exact quote:** "Shu is, do it exactly as the master's telling you... Ha is when you've done the repetitions enough times, you're getting a feel for it... Re level, you're still in the discipline, but you're making up your own rules."
*Source: lex-fridman*

**Applied to tactical empathy progression:**
- **Shu (beginner):** "You sound angry." -- You observe the emotion and name it using the exact formula taught. It feels mechanical. It works anyway.
- **Ha (intermediate):** "This is probably gonna make you angry." -- You're getting a feel for the timing and the situation. You can preemptively label based on context. You adapt the formula while staying within the discipline.
- **Ri (master):** "I know you're angry." -- You don't need to observe it. You know from the circumstances alone. You make your own rules within the framework. You may even create new formulations: a coached client (Michael) spontaneously generated "Gather data with your eyes" -- attributed it to Voss, but Voss had never said it.

**What it means in practice:** At Shu level, follow the script exactly: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "How am I supposed to do that?" Don't customize, don't add flair, don't improvise. Just do it right. Only after 63+ repetitions of correct execution should you start adapting.

The mistake most people make: they want to skip Shu and go directly to Ri. They've read the book, they "understand" the principles, so they try to improvise. This always fails because understanding is not skill. The neural pathway hasn't been myelinated through repetition.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User says the techniques "feel unnatural" or "sound scripted"
- User wants to put the techniques "in their own words" before practicing them as taught
- User has been practicing and is starting to feel the rhythm (transitioning from Shu to Ha)
- User has significant experience and is asking about when to break the rules

#### 63-64 Repetitions to Groove a Habit [3/16]

**Core principle:** Each repetition builds myelin insulation around the neural pathway. You can go excruciatingly slow in practice as long as you do it right.

**Exact quote:** "63 to 64 repetitions to put it in your brain... The Blue Angels build their habits before they get up in the sky. Otherwise the jets crash."
*Source: lewis-howes (citing John Foley, Blue Angel pilot)*

**What it means in practice:** This provides a concrete, specific answer to "how much do I need to practice?" Approximately 64 correct repetitions of a skill groove it into automatic execution. That's:
- 64 mirrors with baristas and colleagues
- 64 labels deployed on Lyft drivers and customer service reps
- 64 "How am I supposed to do that?" delivered in low-stakes pushbacks
- 64 "Is now a bad time to talk?" phone call openers

The critical word is "correct." Sixty-four sloppy repetitions groove sloppy execution. This is why Voss emphasizes that you can go excruciatingly slow -- speed doesn't matter, accuracy does. Each correct rep adds myelin insulation, making the neural pathway fire faster and more automatically.

**The Blue Angels metaphor:** Fighter pilots don't practice formation flying at Mach 2. They practice on the ground, walking through maneuvers at human speed, until the patterns are automatic. Only then do they add speed. Same principle: practice at the speed of accuracy, not the speed of conversation.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User wants to know "how long until this becomes natural"
- User is rushing through practice and not getting results
- User asks if they need to do formal drills or if casual practice counts
- User has been practicing but still freezes in high-stakes moments (likely under 64 reps)

#### "You Fall to Your Highest Level of Preparation" [9/16] [EMPHATIC]

**Core principle:** Under stress, you don't rise to the occasion -- you default to whatever skill level you've actually built. The preparation IS the actual work; the conversation is just execution.

**Exact quote:** "You fall to your highest level of preparation."
*Source: diary-of-a-ceo*

**Additional quote (from the book):** "When pressure hits, you fall to your level of preparation, not rise to occasion."
*Source: book*

**What it means in practice:** This principle demolishes the fantasy of "rising to the occasion." Under pressure -- heart rate elevated, cortisol flooding, amygdala firing -- you do not gain superhuman focus. You lose access to everything you haven't drilled into automaticity. Whatever lives at your level of preparation is what comes out.

Voss was ready for his first bank robbery (Chase Manhattan) because he'd spent 1.5 years working the suicide hotline, keeping his skills perishable-proof through daily reps. He didn't "rise to the occasion" -- he fell to the level he'd prepared for, which was very high.

**The implications for daily practice:** If your preparation level is "I read the book and understand the concepts," that's what you'll fall to under pressure -- intellectual understanding without executable skill. If your preparation level is "I've mirrored 64 baristas and labeled 64 Lyft drivers," that's what you'll fall to -- automatic, fluid execution that doesn't require conscious thought.

**The preparation IS the work:** Don't think of practice as preparation for the "real" thing. The practice is the real thing. The high-stakes conversation is just the place where your practice level gets revealed. If you've done the work, the conversation is easy. If you haven't, no amount of last-minute cramming will save you.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User has a big negotiation coming up and asks how to prepare
- User "knows" the techniques but chokes under pressure
- User asks whether reading more will help them improve (it won't -- practice will)
- User had a high-stakes interaction go badly and wants to understand why

#### The Recovery Mindset [5/16]

**Core principle:** Even experts miss. The key is recognizing the miss, recovering quickly, and treating every failed rep as data -- not as evidence of incompetence.

**Exact quote (LAX airline counter):** "He felt seen, but I missed, and I'm practicing, and I'm trying to stay sharp."
*Source: lex-fridman*

**What it means in practice:** At LAX, Voss tried "Tough day?" on a TSA agent. The body language said no -- it wasn't a tough day. Quick recovery: "Just another day, huh?" -- "Yeah, just another day." Voss reflects: he missed. The man felt seen, but the label wasn't precise. And that's fine -- because the purpose was practice, not perfection.

This mindset is critical: if you treat every missed label or failed mirror as a personal failure, you'll stop practicing. If you treat it as data ("that label didn't land -- what would have been better?"), you compound skill through iteration.

**The success rate reality:** "If it works 9 times out of 10, sometimes you don't get anything." Even at peak performance, some interactions won't produce results. This is normal. The goal is high batting average, not perfection.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User tried a technique and it "didn't work" -- they want to quit
- User is afraid to practice because they might do it wrong
- User asks how to handle it when a label misses or a mirror falls flat
- User needs permission to be imperfect while building skill

#### Awkwardness Is the Signal of Learning [3/16]

**Core principle:** If a technique feels uncomfortable, that discomfort signals you're growing -- not that you're doing it wrong.

**Exact quote:** "Awkwardness is an indicator of accelerated learning. Embrace the feeling of awkwardness you have right now as you read these words and imagine all the ways this won't fit your circumstances. Hug the cactus. Find out. Liberate yourself from your amygdala."
*Source: black-swan-newsletter*

**What it means in practice:** The amygdala -- the brain's threat-detection system -- registers unfamiliarity as danger. When you first try to mirror someone or deploy an accusations audit, every cell in your body screams "this is weird, stop, they'll notice, it won't work." That feeling is not diagnostic of anything except novelty. It's your primitive brain resisting growth.

Push through the awkwardness with small-stakes practice. You'll never know if it works until you try it. And you can't try it if you let your amygdala's fear response prevent action.

**The cactus metaphor:** "Hug the cactus" means embrace the pain of awkwardness rather than avoiding it. The momentary sting of feeling weird is the price of a permanent skill upgrade.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User says "I can't say that -- it would feel weird"
- User is avoiding practicing because the techniques feel unnatural
- User tried once, felt awkward, and concluded "it's not for me"
- User needs reframing: awkwardness = growth signal, not failure signal

#### Stories

**LAX Airline Counter + TSA Practice**
> At LAX, waiting in line, the woman behind the counter "is clearly making a point to not meet my eyes so that I don't approach." Voss approaches and says "Tough day?" -- "She kinda snaps around and she goes, no, no, no, how can I help you? And goes out of her way to help me." Then at TSA, he looks for another practice target. A guy watching bags come out of the x-ray machine with an indifferent look. "Tough day?" The body language says no. Quick recovery: "Just another day, huh?" -- "Yeah, just another day." Voss reflects: "He felt seen, but I missed, and I'm practicing, and I'm trying to stay sharp."
*Teaching application:* Even experts miss, and the key is continuous practice in low-stakes environments. Recovery from a missed label is itself practice. The airline counter woman went out of her way to help -- practice that produces real results. The TSA interaction was a miss that still connected -- practice that builds diagnostic skill.
*Source:* lex-fridman

**Macy's 30% Discount**
> Voss is in a department store; a woman he's with finds a thread out of place on a jacket and says "Watch me get 10% off." Voss responds: "It's the right place. I can get 30% by being nice." He jokes around with the salesman, eventually says playfully "Give me the employee discount." The guy walks away, approaches the manager (who shakes his head), then another employee whispers in his ear -- "his eyes light up" -- and he comes back and keys in 30% off. Voss's analysis: by being playful rather than demanding, he avoided the aggravation tax and the clerk actively searched for ways to help.
*Teaching application:* Playfulness in retail interactions is practice AND produces real results. The 20% gap between demanding 10% and playfully receiving 30% represents the aggravation tax. The salesman didn't just give a discount -- he actively problem-solved to find the biggest one available.
*Source:* lewis-howes

**TSA "Bless Me Father"**
> Voss always has a water bottle in his bag. At TSA in New Jersey, they find it. "Now they could almost walk me back out to the curb because the line is so long." He says: "Bless me, Father, for I have sinned." The TSA agent looks at him and says: "How long since your last confession, my son?" Voss: "You know what, an hour ago. I'm screwing up all the time." The agent poured out the water (which he's not supposed to do), walked Voss back, cut him in front of everyone in line, put him through personally saying "I'm taking care of this guy, it's okay."
*Teaching application:* Humor + self-deprecation transformed an enforcement interaction into an alliance. The TSA agent went from enforcer to personal advocate in 3 seconds. This is practice for turning adversarial dynamics into collaborative ones -- a skill that transfers directly to business negotiations where you've made a mistake.
*Source:* lewis-howes

**Lost Luggage "Wave a Magic Wand"**
> Voss arrives home after a flight, in a great mood, and discovers his luggage is lost. He walks into the lost luggage office where "these people are battered children. They know that you expect them to wave a magic wand and poof, your luggage is going to be there." A young woman asks how she can help. Voss says, "I need you to wave a magic wand." She laughs. Then she does something unprecedented -- walks him to the carousel, climbs up on it, walks down the ramp into the bowels of the airport, disappears for almost ten minutes, and the carousel starts up with his bag emerging. On his way out, she high-fives him and says, "How's that for waving a magic wand?"
*Teaching application:* Genuine playfulness (not technique, not performance) produced an "astonishing" outcome impossible through rigid demands. Being in a genuinely good mood and making people laugh triggers extraordinary discretionary effort. The key was that Voss was genuinely playful -- the mood was real, not manufactured.
*Source:* huberman

**Suicide Hotline Performance Review**
> After completing training on a suicide hotline, Voss was sharp. A year later, his supervisor Jim listened in on a call for the annual review. The caller congratulated Voss for doing a good job. Voss walked back to debrief feeling great. Jim said "that was horrible." Voss was shocked: "Did you just hear that guy? He congratulated me!" Jim was right at every step. "It was at that point that I kind of took... I rededicated to the deeper dive."
*Teaching application:* You don't know skills are decaying. Getting complimented by the subject can be a red flag, not validation. External feedback catches what self-assessment misses. This story is the origin of Voss's conviction that negotiation is perishable -- a year of non-deliberate practice eroded his performance invisibly.
*Source:* knowledge-project
