### Module 10: Detecting Deception, Manipulation, or Bad Faith

When someone suspects the other party is lying, manipulating, or not negotiating genuinely, this module provides Voss's frameworks for detecting deception and surfacing the truth -- without confrontation, which never works.

#### Truth-Telling Baseline Detection [7/16]

The foundational deception-detection principle. Voss's approach rejects the idea that you can spot lies through any single cue. Instead, you establish how someone communicates when they are being truthful, then watch for departures from that pattern.

> "People lie 20 ways. They tell the truth one way."
> -- huberman

> "We all have one way we tell the truth. One way. We might lie five to seven different ways, but you've got one way you tell the truth."
> -- ted-talk

> "It doesn't matter which of the many ways you might come out of your truth-telling -- the only thing that matters is that you came out of the truth-telling baseline."
> -- ted-talk

**Practical meaning:** You cannot detect lies by looking for specific tells (avoiding eye contact, fidgeting). What you can detect is deviation from someone's established baseline of truth-telling. This requires patience -- you must first spend enough time in verifiable, low-stakes conversation to learn what their "truth voice" sounds like, then notice when it changes.

**How to establish baseline:** Start conversations with easy, verifiable questions. Where are you from? What do you do? How did you get into this line of work? Pay attention to pace, word density, pronoun usage, and emotional register. Then introduce harder questions and watch for shifts.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User suspects a vendor, partner, or counterpart is lying
- User needs to vet someone over multiple conversations
- User is preparing for a meeting where honesty is critical
- User asks "how do I know if they're telling the truth?"

#### The Pinocchio Effect / Deception Indicators [6/16]

A set of linguistic tells that emerge when people are being deceptive, drawn from hostage negotiation experience and supported by academic research. Voss names this the "Pinocchio Effect" because "liars use more words, more third-person pronouns, and more complex sentences. They over-explain to sound convincing and distance themselves from the lie. Truth-tellers are direct, simple, and comfortable with brief answers." [book, Ch. 8]

> "Liars use more words than truth-tellers and more third-person pronouns (him, her, it, they). They employ complex sentences to persuade suspicious listeners."
> -- book

**Practical meaning:** Deception manifests not in what people say but in how they say it. The four key indicators:

1. **More words than necessary.** Truth is simple and direct. Lies require supporting architecture -- qualifications, explanations, justifications. When a simple question produces a paragraph, pay attention. The liar over-explains because they need the fabrication to sound convincing, which paradoxically makes it less convincing to a trained listener.

2. **Third-person pronouns increase.** "Liars use more third-person pronouns (him, her, it, they, one)" [book, Ch. 8] to distance themselves from their own statements. "I did it" becomes "It was done" or "They decided." Heavy use of "we," "they," "them" where "I" would be natural signals distancing -- the liar is psychologically separating themselves from the lie.

3. **Sentence complexity rises.** "More complex sentences" [book, Ch. 8] are required to maintain internal consistency of a fabrication. Simple truths get stated simply. When sentence structure becomes elaborate and multi-claused in response to a straightforward question, the person is constructing rather than reporting.

4. **First-person pronoun avoidance.** The inverse of the third-person signal -- when someone can't or won't say "I will do X" with a first-person pronoun, they may be avoiding personal ownership of a commitment. This connects to the pronoun-as-power indicator: "Heavy 'I,' 'me,' 'my' usage = the person is less important in the decision chain" while "difficulty accessing first-person pronouns / using 'we,' 'they,' 'the team' = the person holds real decision-making power." [book, Ch. 8] In the deception context, the shift is driven by avoidance of ownership, not authority.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User notices a counterpart becoming unusually verbose
- User senses evasiveness but can't pinpoint why
- User is evaluating written communication (email, proposal) for authenticity

#### The "Win-Win" Red Flag [6/16]

One of Voss's most reliable early-warning indicators for manipulative intent. When someone declares their collaborative intentions rather than demonstrating them, it is almost always a setup.

> "If someone opens a negotiation with me and they say right off the bat, look, I want to do a win-win deal with you, that correlates extremely highly with someone who's trying to pick my pocket."
> -- huberman

> "The sooner win-win comes out of somebody's mouth across the table, that -- well, that's an automatic mark of somebody's trying to -- to rip me off."
> -- lewis-howes

> "Most of the time when people call [win-win] out, they're trying to get you to give in."
> -- future-organization

**Practical meaning:** Authentic collaborators demonstrate value through their behavior -- they ask questions, listen, show flexibility, and invest time in understanding your position. Manipulators declare intentions verbally ("let's do a win-win deal") as a preemptive framing device designed to make you feel guilty for protecting your interests. The earlier "win-win" appears in a conversation, the stronger the correlation with manipulative intent.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User reports that a counterpart led with "win-win" language
- User feels pressure to be accommodating based on stated (not demonstrated) collaborative intent
- User is evaluating cold outreach or proposals that lead with mutual benefit language

#### The Specificity Test for Threats [4/16]

A threat assessment framework drawn directly from hostage negotiation that distinguishes posturing from genuine danger. The diagnostic is simple: specificity.

> "It's specificity. You're looking for it... if they say, look, if we don't get this by this specific deadline, if we don't get these specific things met by this specific time, that's pretty specific."
> -- huberman

The Philippines kidnapping illustrates this perfectly. When the captors said "he's going to lose an egg" (a euphemism for killing the hostage), Voss assessed:

> "They didn't say when it was going to happen. They didn't say how it was going to happen. They didn't say who was going to do it. The basic specificity of who, what, when and where. Like, they left themselves an out here."
> -- huberman

He told the team they had time to maneuver. Later, the captors called back with a different message: "If we don't get paid tomorrow, your son dies." Voss's read: "All right, now that's specific. And these guys sound like they mean it." The family was advised to pay.

**Practical meaning:** Check every threat (legal, business, personal) against four criteria: Who will act? What exactly will they do? When will it happen? Where? Vague threats ("something bad will happen," "there will be consequences") are posturing -- the person is leaving themselves room to back down. Specific threats ("I'm filing the lawsuit Monday with Judge Henderson in the Eastern District") demand immediate attention.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User has received a threatening communication (legal, business, personal)
- User needs to assess whether an ultimatum is real
- User is deciding whether to escalate or wait

#### Three Types of Yes (Commitment, Confirmation, Counterfeit) [5/16]

Most agreements are not what they appear to be. Voss identifies three distinct types of "yes," and the majority of yeses encountered in negotiation are counterfeit.

> "About 80% of the time I want to know what your deal terms are so I can shop them."
> -- knowledge-project [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY]

> "I can give you fake yeses all day long and you're never gonna get what you wanted."
> -- knowledge-project

The three types (from Chapter 4 of the book, "Beware 'Yes' -- Master 'No'"):

1. **Counterfeit yes** -- "said to end the conversation, avoid conflict, or extract your terms for comparison shopping." [book, Ch. 4] This is an escape route, not agreement. The person wants out of the conversation and "yes" is the fastest exit. This is the most common type, and the most dangerous because it creates the illusion of progress. The "Daryl" crisis hotline call is the signature example: Voss persuaded an anxious caller that everything was manageable, got a "yes," felt victorious -- then a colleague called it one of the worst calls ever made. The "yes" was hollow because Daryl hadn't been changed, just managed. He agreed to get off the phone.

2. **Confirmation yes** -- a reflexive affirmation with no commitment behind it. "Yes, I understand" does not mean "yes, I agree." "Yes, I hear you" does not mean "yes, I'll act." This is the autopilot response -- acknowledging receipt of words, not endorsing their content.

3. **Commitment yes** -- genuine intent to follow through. Rare and valuable. This is the only "yes" that leads to behavioral change and action. It comes from the counterpart arriving at their own conclusion, not being talked into someone else's. "The goal is behavioral change, not intellectual submission. Let the other person lead themselves to conclusions." [book, Ch. 4]

**Practical meaning:** When you hear "yes," your work is not done -- it's just beginning. Test every yes with "how" questions about implementation. A counterfeit yes collapses under implementation questioning because the person never intended to follow through and cannot describe what execution looks like. This is why Voss says "'No' often opens the discussion up" -- because "No" gives the counterpart safety and control, producing honesty rather than performative agreement. "People need to feel autonomous before they can cooperate." [book, Ch. 4]

**Coaching triggers:**
- User got a "yes" that felt too easy
- User suspects agreement is performative
- User has experienced repeated agreements that never led to action
- User did all the talking and the other person just agreed

#### Draw Out, Don't Confront [5/16]

When you've detected deception, the instinct is to confront it. Voss is absolute that this is counterproductive.

> "Flat-out asking people if they're liars will never help you."
> -- thought-economics

> "You have to draw people out of the position of fear to really understand them."
> -- thought-economics

> "Most of the time-- it's because they're scared and don't know how to tell the truth."
> -- thought-economics

**Practical meaning:** Most deception is fear-driven, not malicious. The person lying to you is almost always afraid -- of consequences, of your reaction, of losing face, of losing the deal. Confrontation amplifies their fear and hardens their deception. Instead, create safety. Use labels ("It seems like there's something making this complicated for you") and silence. When people feel safe enough, truth emerges voluntarily. Your goal is not to prove they lied -- it's to get the real information.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User has caught someone in a lie and wants to confront them
- User senses evasiveness from a direct report, partner, or counterpart
- User is frustrated that someone won't "just be honest"

#### Pronoun Usage as Power and Deception Indicator [5/16]

A diagnostic tool for identifying who actually holds power in a negotiation, and who is performing powerlessness as a manipulation strategy.

> "The most powerful negotiators in business will always use plural pronouns because they're trying to hide their influence on their side."
> -- jordan-678

The Chase Manhattan bank robbery is the definitive illustration. The robber used plural pronouns constantly -- "these other guys," "they're dangerous," "I don't know what they're going to do" -- positioning himself as a helpless intermediary. In reality, he was the mastermind who had orchestrated the entire operation. Some of his accomplices didn't even know they were robbing a bank.

> "He was a master manipulator and he did that by... making himself look deferential and not in charge."
> -- jordan-678

**Practical meaning:** When someone uses heavy "I/me/my" language, they are often lower in the decision chain and projecting power they don't actually have. When someone consistently uses "we/they/them" and defers to people not in the room, they are often the actual decision-maker hiding their authority to avoid being cornered. The more someone makes themselves appear powerless, the more closely you should examine their actual influence.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is trying to identify the real decision-maker
- User suspects someone is feigning helplessness to avoid commitment
- User is in a multi-party negotiation and can't tell who has authority

#### The 7-38-55 Rule (Incongruence Detection) [2/16]

A communication research framework that Voss applies directly to deception detection. Based on Albert Mehrabian's research, referenced in Chapter 8 of the book.

> "Superstar negotiators -- real rainmakers -- know that a negotiation is a playing field beneath the words, where really getting to a good deal involves detecting and manipulating subtle, nonobvious signals beneath the surface." [book, Ch. 8]

The distribution: **7% of communication is the words themselves. 38% is tone of voice. 55% is body language and facial expressions.** [book, Ch. 8]

**Practical meaning for deception detection:** When words, tone, and body language are aligned, you're likely hearing truth. When they diverge -- when someone says "yes" but their tone drops, when they agree but their body pulls back, when they promise commitment but their voice tightens -- the nonverbal channels are overriding the verbal one. The 93% of communication that is NOT words will betray a lie that the 7% is trying to sell.

The operational tool: "When tone or body language misaligns with words, use labels to surface the incongruence: 'I heard you say yes, but it seemed like there was some hesitation in your voice.'" [book, Ch. 8] You're not accusing them of lying -- you're noticing the discrepancy and creating space for truth. This is particularly powerful in team settings: "In meetings, this is how you detect hidden disagreement that would otherwise torpedo execution later." [book, Ch. 8]

**Coaching triggers:**
- User senses something "off" about a conversation but can't name it
- User reports agreement that doesn't feel genuine
- User is trying to verify commitment in a meeting or negotiation
- User needs to distinguish between what someone says and what they actually believe

#### Stories

**Khalid Sheikh Mohammed Identification**
> Two FBI agents had access to a badly wounded terrorist in a secret location outside the United States, months after 9/11. Their strategy was patience and baseline establishment: "We just talked to this guy long enough till he knows we know what he looks like when he tells the truth, and then we lay the picture on him, see what he says." After extended conversation establishing his truth-telling pattern, they showed him a photograph. "Who is this?" He responded: "You know who it is." They pressed. He said: "It's Ramsey's uncle." Ramzi Yousef's uncle was Khalid Sheikh Mohammed, the organizer of the 9/11 attacks. "The interesting part about this: at the time, most of the US government doesn't think Khalid Sheikh Mohammed is involved with Al Qaeda at all, let alone organizing the attack."
*Teaching application:* When you need to verify critical information, establish the truth-telling baseline first through extended low-stakes conversation. Then introduce the high-stakes question. The departure from baseline tells you everything. Patience and observation outperform coercion or technology.
*Source:* ted-talk

**The Text Scam (Strippers, Dog, Clown, and Pony)**
> A friend's phone number was spoofed by scammers. Voss received texts from "his friend" asking for urgent money. To verify, Voss planted false memories: "That was such a crazy night, and I still owe you money from then. So that night when we were gambling, I still owe you money." (There was no gambling -- it was breakfast.) The scammer accepted the false memory: "Yeah, don't worry about it." Now confirmed as a con, Voss escalated: "And when we were with those strippers and that dog and the clown and the pony, I'll never get over that." The scammer became confused, then hostile, and stopped texting. Voss sent the exchange to the real friend, who replied: "By the way, my mom does think you're attractive."
*Teaching application:* Verify identity by planting claims only the real person could confirm or deny. When someone accepts a false memory, you have confirmed they are not who they claim to be. Once confirmed as deception, disengage -- or have fun.
*Source:* huberman

**Chase Manhattan Bank Robber (Plural Pronouns)**
> During a bank robbery in Brooklyn -- Voss's first major case -- the robber got on the phone and was "as calm and rational as you could possibly be." He masterfully used plural pronouns: "There are all these other people with me and I don't know what they're going to do. These other guys they're so much more dangerous than I am." He'd put negotiators on hold saying "they're going to overhear what I'm saying." Post-incident, they discovered he had "lined up the whole bank robbery and manipulated everybody." Some accomplices "thought that they just went out to rob a cash machine that day."
*Teaching application:* The person who appears least threatening and most deferential may be the actual architect. When someone consistently uses plural pronouns and defers to absent parties, investigate whether they are the real power -- not the intermediary they claim to be.
*Source:* jordan-678, jordan-165, diary-of-a-ceo

**The Philippines "Lose an Egg" Threat Assessment**
> A 17-year-old boy was kidnapped. The captors called and said: "You tell his father he's going to lose an egg." The team panicked. Voss assessed: they didn't specify when, how, or who would carry it out. "They left themselves an out here." He told the team: "We got plenty of time to play with this." Later, after a bungled ransom delivery, a different call came: "If we don't get paid tomorrow, your son dies." Voss: "All right, now that's specific. And these guys sound like they mean it." The family paid. The son came out alive.
*Teaching application:* Apply the specificity test (who, what, when, where) to every threat. Vague threats leave room for retreat and signal posturing. Specific threats with concrete details demand immediate action.
*Source:* huberman

**Procurement Leverage Inversion**
> At a supply chain conference, a procurement professional told Voss privately: "When you're in procurement you got to be really careful that you're not your supplier's biggest customer or only customer because you'll end up putting them out of business." Voss realized: "Procurement does not waste their time on suppliers they have no interest in, ever. So if procurement gets in a game with you, buckle up, let them call you names, don't get upset, calm down. They're only talking to you because they really want you."
*Teaching application:* When facing aggressive, intimidating behavior from a counterpart, ask: would they be investing this effort if they had better options? Their aggression IS the buy signal. Don't fold under pressure that is itself evidence of your leverage.
*Source:* jordan-165
