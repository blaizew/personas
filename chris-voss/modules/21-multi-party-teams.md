### Module 21: Negotiating in Multi-Party or Team Situations

When multiple stakeholders are involved -- a hidden team behind the negotiator, internal alignment issues, or a visible group with competing interests -- the dynamics multiply in complexity. Voss's core insight is that you are NEVER negotiating with just one person. The person at the table is a representative; the real decision happens elsewhere. Your job is to influence the internal conversation you cannot see.

---

#### There's Always a Team on the Other Side [8/16] [EMPHATIC]

The single most important principle for multi-party negotiation: never treat an interaction as two individuals. Behind every negotiator is a team, a boss, a committee, a board, or a family. Fifty percent of deals die not because of the person across from you but because of invisible stakeholders who never came to the table.

**Quote:** "There's always a team on the other side. Always, always, always." (spycast)

**Quote:** "Fully 50 percent of their deals that get killed, get killed internally." (jordan-678, referencing a multinational telecom)

**Practical meaning:** The person you're negotiating with may genuinely agree with you and still cannot deliver. Their boss, their legal department, their procurement team, their board -- any of these can kill a deal after agreement is reached. This is why "Yes is nothing without How" -- because "how" questions force the visible negotiator to think about (and reveal) their internal approval process. If they cannot describe implementation, it means they haven't secured alignment from the people who actually decide.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user gets enthusiastic agreement from a counterpart but suspects non-delivery, when a deal stalls after verbal commitment, when the user is preparing for any B2B negotiation. Always ask: "Who else needs to agree for this to happen?"

---

#### "How" Questions Force Internal Coordination [6/16] [SIGNATURE]

The eureka moment in Voss's methodology for multi-party situations: asking "how" questions doesn't just shift burden to the counterpart -- it forces the counterpart's entire hidden team to coordinate around your concern. This was discovered in a real kidnapping when a simple question created unprecedented internal collaboration within the captor group.

**Quote:** "The how question causes a unification on the other side." (jordan-678)

**Quote:** "We'd never forced the other side into an internal collaboration that favored us ever before." (spycast)

**The Ecuador Discovery (detailed mechanism):** In the Pepe Escobar kidnapping, Voss deployed "How do we know he's alive?" instead of traditional security questions. The kidnapper's negotiator "kept coming back out to the jungle and saying, 'You know, these guys were asking me this question. I don't know how to deal with it.'" This forced "an entire series of meetings of the kidnappers to get behind one strategy that they never would have had, ever." The internal collaboration changed the sequencing of their priorities from "How do we get money?" to "How do we prove he's okay so we can get money?" -- which required better treatment of the hostage.

**Practical meaning:** When you ask "how" questions repeatedly (4-5 times), the visible negotiator must go back to their team. Each return trip forces the team to coordinate -- which means (a) you learn about their internal structure through the delays and changes, (b) their team expends cognitive resources solving your problem, and (c) the process of coordination often shifts their internal dynamics in your favor because the person advocating for you gains status within their group.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user suspects the person they're talking to cannot deliver alone, when a deal requires buy-in from multiple internal stakeholders, when you want to diagnose the other side's internal structure without asking directly.

---

#### Watch Their Wingmen, Not Their Speaker [3/16]

In team-based negotiation, the person speaking is guarded -- they know they are performing. The real diagnostic information comes from the people NOT talking, who believe no one is watching them.

**Quote:** "The speaker is guarded; their team members think no one is watching them." (jordan-165)

[PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY] Voss describes that in meetings, people who are not the primary speaker will physically react to things they disagree with -- "they will almost flip around in their seats" -- because they do not realize anyone is watching their body language.

**Practical meaning:** When negotiating with a team, your primary intelligence target is not the speaker. The speaker has prepared their face, their words, their tone. The others have not. Watch for micro-reactions: head shakes, eye rolls, crossed arms, posture shifts, glances at the speaker when something is said that the team member disagrees with. These reactions are involuntary and unguarded. They tell you what the internal disagreements are, who has real authority, and where the pressure points live.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is in a meeting with multiple people from the other side, when they want to identify the real decision-maker, when they suspect internal disagreement they could exploit or address.

---

#### Team-Based Listening (Up to 7 People) [4/16]

Voss's operational insight from FBI hostage negotiation: one person literally cannot capture all the information in a conversation. The human bandwidth math proves it. Speech delivers 140-160 words/minute; tone carries 5x the information of words alone; total information flow exceeds any single listener's capacity. The solution is team listening with specific assignments.

**Quote:** "Give me seven people. If you give me seven people to listen to everything, we'll all pick a different part... and we will come up with an encyclopedia of information, ideas and clues in a 10 minute conversation." (jordan-165)

**Operational structure:** In FBI negotiations, up to 7 listeners had specific assignments: one tracked time patterns, one tracked negative statements, one tracked positive statements, others tracked tone shifts, pronoun usage, profanity levels, and break patterns. After each call, the team synthesized findings. The person on the phone could be the least technically skilled negotiator because they had 7 people feeding them intelligence in real time.

**Practical meaning:** The BEST negotiator runs the team, not the phone. This inverts the common assumption that the most skilled person should be the one talking. Instead, the most skilled person should be listening, analyzing, and directing. In business applications: bring a colleague to important meetings specifically to listen and observe while you talk. Debrief afterward. A "note-taker" is really an intelligence analyst.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is preparing for a high-stakes meeting, when they can bring a colleague, when they want to maximize information extraction from a critical conversation. Advise: "Never negotiate alone if you can avoid it. Someone who isn't speaking can hear things you structurally cannot."

---

#### The Proof of Life / Boss in the Jungle Pattern [4/16]

In multi-party negotiations, you must identify whether you're dealing with the actual decision-maker or a representative. The same pattern appears in guerrilla kidnapping and corporate deals: a representative comes to town, negotiates, then returns to "the jungle" (or the C-suite) for thumbs-up or thumbs-down.

**Quote:** "You are negotiating with a rep. The rep takes it back to the boss." (spycast)

**Practical meaning:** The "boss in the jungle" pattern means: (1) Your counterpart's authority must be verified before you invest resources. (2) "How" questions surface who else needs to agree: "How does your team handle decisions like this?" (3) Implementation details are the diagnostic -- if they cannot describe next steps, they may not have the authority to deliver. (4) Getting verbal commitment from a representative is worth less than understanding the representative's relationship to the actual decision-maker. The Verizon statistic -- "fully 50% of deals that Verizon cuts never get implemented because they get killed in terms and conditions" -- proves this pattern is ubiquitous.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user suspects they are not dealing with the actual decision-maker, when a counterpart keeps deferring to absent authority ("I'll have to check with my boss"), when a deal keeps getting modified after apparent agreement.

---

#### Pronoun Usage Reveals the Real Decision-Maker [5/16]

A specific diagnostic tool for multi-party situations: listen to pronoun patterns. The real decision-maker hides behind collective pronouns; the powerless person overuses first-person pronouns.

**Quote:** "The most powerful negotiators in business will always use plural pronouns because they're trying to hide their influence on their side." (jordan-678)

**Quote:** "Heavy 'I/me/my' usage = lower importance in decision chain. Difficulty accessing first-person pronouns = higher importance/decision-making power." (book)

**The Chase Manhattan Bank Robbery demonstration:** The bank robber was "a master manipulator" who used plural pronouns constantly: "There are all these other people with me and I don't know what they're going to do. These other guys they're so much more dangerous than I am." He'd say "Let me put you on hold because they're going to overhear what I'm saying." Post-incident they learned he had "lined up the whole bank robbery and manipulated everybody." Some accomplices "thought that they just went out to rob a cash machine that day."

**Practical meaning:** "The harder they work to make themselves seem powerless, the more influential they are." When someone constantly deflects to committees, bosses, or unnamed others -- that is often the actual decision-maker using deniability as a power tool. Conversely, someone who leads with "I" and "me" may be posturing authority they don't actually have. In meetings: the person who speaks least and defers most may be the one everyone is actually trying to satisfy.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is trying to identify who actually decides, when someone keeps saying "I'll have to check," when a negotiation involves multiple people with unclear authority. Listen for pronouns -- they reveal power structure involuntarily.

---

#### Allied Dysfunction Is the Real Threat [3/16]

Sometimes the greatest danger in a multi-party negotiation comes not from the adversary but from dysfunction within your own side. Misaligned allies, non-communicating partners, and organizations that refuse to share information can destroy outcomes more reliably than any opponent.

**Quote (from the Burnham-Sobero case):** "The US government was not highly collaborative. The Philippine government was not highly collaborative. Everybody wanted to get their pound of flesh out of the other side." (huberman)

**Critical operational detail:** During the Burnham case, the local negotiator assigned to work with the FBI "will not tell us anything until after he's told them. So he's having conversations with the bad guys, and we're actually hearing about him secondhand." Kidnappers announced they'd kill a hostage on a Philippine national holiday -- "we showed up at Philippine National Police Headquarters, in Manila, and it was closed." Nobody told the FBI it was a holiday.

**Practical meaning:** Never assume your allies are sharing critical information. The Burnham disaster taught Voss: "I had assumed at that point in time that people would tell us the stuff we need to know, we didn't need to ask. And after that, I got like, look, there ain't nothing here that I don't need to know." In business: when working with partners, advisors, lawyers, or internal teams -- verify alignment constantly. The ally who withholds information or operates independently is more dangerous than the counterpart across the table.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is working with partners or colleagues in a joint negotiation, when they have lawyers or advisors involved, when there are multiple internal stakeholders who might not be aligned. Always ask: "Is your own side fully coordinated?"

#### Stories

**Ecuador Kidnapping (Internal Collaboration)**
> Voss's first major test of the "how" proof-of-life question. The wife kept asking: "How do I know my husband's even alive? How am I supposed to pay if I don't know he's alive?" The kidnapper's negotiator "kept coming back out to the jungle and saying, 'You know, these guys were asking me this question. I don't know how to deal with it.'" This forced "an entire series of meetings of the kidnappers to get behind one strategy that they never would have had, ever." The internal collaboration changed the hostage's treatment -- captors started calling him "Don Pepe" (a sign of respect), relaxed his security, and he eventually escaped on his own.

*Teaching application:* "How" questions don't just stall or shift burden -- they force the other side's internal team to coordinate in ways that favor you. The question creates a need for internal consensus that didn't exist before. Each time the representative returns to the team with your question, the team must collaborate to answer it -- and that collaboration shifts their collective stance. In business: persistent "how" questions force procurement teams, legal departments, and executive committees to actively problem-solve your concerns rather than simply rejecting them.
*Source:* spycast, jordan-678

**Chase Manhattan (The Mastermind Behind Plural Pronouns)**
> The bank robber was "one shrewd guy" -- kept saying the other guys were dangerous, he had no power. "He was extremely calm. At first we just thought we were dealing with an inadequate personality." After 12 hours, they discovered he was the mastermind: his accomplices "didn't even know they were going to rob the bank. They thought they were going to burglarize a cash machine." He'd hidden hundreds of thousands in the bank's construction walls, burned a pile of money so it looked like nothing was taken. His constant use of "we" and "they" and "them" was not weakness -- it was the most powerful person in the room hiding behind plural pronouns.

*Teaching application:* The diagnostic lesson for multi-party situations: the person who appears least powerful, who constantly defers to others, who claims inability to decide -- may be the actual architect of the entire situation. In corporate negotiation, anyone who habitually says "I'll need to check with my team" or "the committee decides" might be the committee. Watch for the person whose apparent helplessness others defer to implicitly.
*Source:* jordan-678, jordan-165, diary-of-a-ceo

**Burnham-Sobero (Allied Dysfunction)**
> The hostage case that changed Voss's career: 13 months, multiple hostages executed, catastrophic failure from allied dysfunction. The Philippine government wouldn't share information. The local negotiator operated independently. The FBI showed up to headquarters on a national holiday with no warning. The final rescue was "friendly fire" -- Philippine Scout Rangers accidentally fired on the encampment not knowing hostages were there, killing two of three remaining hostages.

> Voss's takeaway: "I had assumed at that point in time that people would tell us the stuff we need to know, we didn't need to ask. And after that, I got like, look, there ain't nothing here that I don't need to know." The failure sent him to Harvard: "If we did everything we know how to do and it wasn't enough, that means we're not smart enough, we got to get better."

*Teaching application:* The biggest threats often come from dysfunction on your own side, not from the adversary. Multi-party negotiation means managing your own team's coordination as rigorously as you manage the external negotiation. Never assume shared awareness. Never assume aligned priorities. Verify everything with your own allies as aggressively as you probe the other side.
*Source:* huberman, spycast, diary-of-a-ceo

**The 50% Implementation Failure (Telecom)**
> Voss's company was competing for a negotiation contract with a major telecommunications company. They discovered that "fully 50% of the deals they sign never get implemented. Half." Their salespeople were getting agreement at the table, but the internal approval chain -- legal, terms and conditions, compliance -- was killing deals after signatures. The company was "destroying their profit over bad implementation or even not even implementing at all."

*Teaching application:* The team behind your counterpart doesn't just influence the deal -- they can entirely invalidate it after the fact. "Yes" from the person across from you is meaningless if their internal team vetoes during implementation. This is why "How will your team handle this?" and "What does implementation look like on your end?" are essential questions. They force the representative to think about (and potentially secure) internal alignment before committing.
*Source:* knowledge-project, jordan-678, spycast
