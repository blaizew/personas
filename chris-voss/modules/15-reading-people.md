### Reading People and Adapting Your Style

Most communication breakdowns are not personality conflicts or bad faith -- they are type mismatches. Voss's framework divides all humans into three negotiator types, each with different relationships to time, silence, and trust. Recognizing the mismatch is usually the key to breaking any impasse.

---

#### Three Negotiator Types: Assertive, Analyst, Accommodator [12/16] [SIGNATURE]

**Quote:** "We believe, my company, that you're born one of three types. Fight, flight, or make friends. It's a descendant from the caveman days... the world splits pretty evenly into thirds." (jordan-165)

**Quote:** "When you have miscommunications and mismatches and communications or barriers, impasse is almost always a type problem, a type mismatch problem." (jordan-165)

**The three types:**

**Assertive (Fight):** Values time above all. Wants to feel heard. Direct, fast, aggressive. Has not accomplished anything until they've had their say. Their relationship to silence: it means aggression or rejection -- they'll fill it immediately with more talking.

**Analyst (Flight):** Values data and accuracy. Needs thinking time. Appears cold, distant, methodical. Hates being rushed or surprised. Their relationship to silence: it means "thank you for giving me space to think" -- silence is a gift, not a threat.

**Accommodator (Make Friends):** Values relationship. Warm, agreeable, prioritizes connection. Will agree to things they don't mean to preserve the relationship. Their relationship to silence: it means fury -- "the worst thing an accommodator can do to you is stop working on a relationship because that's the thing they value most."

**Quote:** "A caveman, walking down the jungle path, way back when he encountered something alive, he had one of three thoughts -- can I kill it and eat it? Is it going to kill me? Can I make it with it?" (jordan-678)

**Practical meaning:** Each type projects its own values onto others, causing catastrophic miscommunication. An assertive talks fast and thinks the analyst is slow/passive. The analyst goes silent and thinks the assertive is reckless/stupid. The accommodator agrees and neither of the other two realizes the agreement is hollow.

**Critical diagnostic:** The same behavior (silence) means three completely different things depending on the type. If you misread silence, you will misread the entire negotiation.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User's approach keeps failing with a specific person
- User is confused by someone's behavior that seems irrational
- User interprets silence as anger, agreement, or rejection without checking
- User is at an impasse and doesn't know why

#### Labels Work on Everyone; Adapt the Secondary Tool [6/16]

**Quote:** "Any question puts people on guard to some degree... If in that moment I want you to stop and think and take a step back, I'll ask you what question. If instead I want to trigger a straight stream of consciousness, seems like, sounds like... hits your brain in a completely different way." (high-performance)

**Practical meaning:** Labels ("It seems like...", "It sounds like...") work universally across all three types -- they are the one tool that transcends type differences. But secondary tools must be adapted:

- **With Analysts:** Use labels, not questions. An analyst asked a question will say "let me get back to you in a week." Given a label, they'll "blurt it right out" because labels "remove a barrier" and "tap directly into thought processes." Labels trigger stream-of-consciousness disclosure from analysts.

- **With Assertives:** Let them talk first so they feel heard. Don't try to out-talk them. Mirror and label until they exhaust themselves. Only then will they listen.

- **With Accommodators:** Be careful that their warmth doesn't mask disagreement. Their "yes" may be relationship-preserving rather than commitment-making. Probe with "How" questions to test whether agreement is genuine.

**Quote:** (On labels vs. questions with analysts) "A student coined the phrase 'unlocking the floodgates of truth-telling'... questions make people stop, think, and formulate. Labels remove a barrier and tap directly into thought processes." (knowledge-project)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User has been asking questions and getting evasive "let me think about it" responses (dealing with an analyst)
- User can't get a word in edgewise (dealing with an assertive)
- User keeps getting agreement that never becomes action (dealing with an accommodator)

#### The 7-38-55 Rule [5/16]

**Quote:** "7% of message is words, 38% is voice tone, 55% is body language. When tone/body language misaligns with words, use labels to discover incongruence." (book)

**Practical meaning:** Most communication is not verbal. When you detect a mismatch between someone's words and their tone or body language, the non-verbal channel is telling the truth. The words are the performance.

The operational response to incongruence: label it. "I heard you say yes, but there was hesitation in your voice." This is not confrontational -- it's observational. It gives the person permission to voice what their body was already communicating.

**Application to text/email:** Written communication removes 93% of the signal (tone + body). This means text negotiations are flying blind on 7% of available information. Compensate by using label language ("It seems like...") rather than direct questions, and by moving to voice or in-person whenever stakes are high.

**The Elon Musk story (illustrating the rule):** On a conference call, Musk said "you're fired!" as a joke. Everyone in the room could see his face -- clearly joking. But the person on the phone, receiving only the 7% words + 38% tone, thought he was terminated. "They're on a phone, and Elon goes, 'you're fired!' And then everybody in the room with him can see that he's joking. But the person on the other side can't." (lex-fridman)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User detected a mismatch between someone's words and behavior
- User is negotiating primarily via text/email and feels something is off
- User wants to know how to address "I sense they don't really agree"

#### "What Got You Here Won't Get You There" [4/16]

**Quote:** "There's the old saying, 'What got you here, won't get you there.' We try to counsel people that there are elements of the analysts that are really good and you want to know what's good. And you want to know what the companion skills are from the accommodator that you want to add to your skill set." (jordan-678)

**Practical meaning:** Don't try to change your default type. Keep your core strengths -- but consciously add complementary skills from the other two types. An assertive who learns accommodator warmth becomes unstoppable. An analyst who adds assertive decisiveness becomes a closer. An accommodator who develops analyst rigor stops getting steamrolled.

**Quote (on Voss's daughter-in-law):** "Everybody that knows her thinks she's an accommodator... She's just so darn smart, she learned that she gets a lot more information out of the situation the more pleasant she is. She is sucking every bit of information that you have." (high-performance)

The most effective operators blend types: analytical processing underneath, relational warmth on the surface. This is learnable -- not a personality change but a skill addition.

**Quote:** "Are you being a chameleon? Or are you simply learning and getting better? Are you discarding behaviors that you've learned that don't serve you well, in favor of behaviors that you can learn that serve you better?" (future-organization)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is an assertive whose directness keeps alienating people
- User is an analyst who can't connect or close
- User is an accommodator who keeps getting taken advantage of
- User asks "should I change my personality?"

#### Gut vs. Amygdala [4/16]

**Quote:** "We're teaching people, learn the difference between your gut and your amygdala, for lack of a better term, your fear centers, and know which one is which. And listen to your gut. Your gut is ridiculously accurate." (huberman)

**Practical meaning:** The gut reads truth via signals you can't consciously identify -- micro-expressions, tonal micro-shifts, possibly even pheromones. The amygdala generates fear based on past trauma and projection. They feel similar in the body but produce opposite quality of information.

**The distinction:**
- Gut signal: calm certainty that something is off, without being able to explain why. Often described as "I just knew" or "something didn't feel right."
- Amygdala signal: anxiety, catastrophizing, avoidance. Driven by past experience being projected onto the present. "The last person who talked like this burned me."

**Quote:** "Some of the great investigators I knew would say, I can just smell it. I can smell it." (huberman)

**Quote:** "Our subconscious, where our radar is, is literally 20 million times faster than our conscious." (jordan-165)

**Practical application for reading people:** When you get a gut read on someone during a negotiation -- trust it, but verify through labels rather than acting on it directly. "Seems like something just crossed your mind" checks an intuition without committing to an interpretation.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User has a "bad feeling" about someone but can't articulate why
- User is second-guessing intuition because the facts look fine
- User is paralyzed by anxiety that may be amygdala, not gut

#### Stories

**Voss at Harvard -- "Every Negotiator Should Be Assertive"**
> When first exposed to the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument at Harvard's executive negotiation course, Voss assumed all his negotiators should be assertive types because "we got people's lives on a line." He later discovered his best team included all three types -- analysts, relationship-oriented people, and assertives -- because each brings something the others cannot. "As it turns out, each type has stuff they bring to the table that are necessary. No one type has the market cornered."
*Teaching application:* Projection bias leads even experts to assume their type is the "correct" one. Voss -- a natural assertive -- had to learn that his own type was not inherently superior, just different. Diversity of type is a force multiplier, not a weakness.
*Source:* jordan-165

**Charlie Beaudoin "Smile, They Can Feel It"**
> Charlie Beaudoin was an FBI hostage negotiator and natural accommodator. "We'd walk into a bar that we'd never been in before... Charlie would walk around and literally introduce himself to everybody in the bar one at a time. He'd just walk up and say 'hi I'm Charlie' and he'd smile at people." During hostage negotiations, Charlie wrote a note on the board: "Smile -- they can feel it in your voice." It was "very powerful... Charlie broke down more barriers just by smiling at people."
*Teaching application:* The accommodator's superpower is warmth, and it works even over the phone. Smiling changes vocal quality -- the other person can literally hear it. This is a learnable hack: even non-accommodators can adopt this one behavior and gain its benefits. Also demonstrates that the accommodator type is not weak -- Charlie was breaking down hostage-taker barriers.
*Source:* knowledge-project

**The Analyst-Who-Smiles (Daughter-in-Law)**
> Voss's daughter-in-law is the director of marketing at Black Swan Group. "Everybody that knows her thinks she's an accommodator... She's just so darn smart, she learned that she gets a lot more information out of the situation the more pleasant she is. She is sucking every bit of information that you have."
*Teaching application:* The most effective operators combine types: analytical processing underneath, warmth on the surface. She doesn't reveal her analytical nature because warmth produces better data -- people open up to pleasant people. This is the aspiration: keep your core type but add the tools that work from other types.
*Source:* high-performance

**Plane Seatmate Deep Read**
> On a flight into LA, Voss used labels (it seems like, it sounds like, it looks like) and found out more about the stranger next to him "than he's told anybody in 20 years." In one flight he learned: the man has an adopted daughter from China, his mother had bipolar and committed suicide when he was 17, he was raised by grandparents, his grandfather owned 11 banks that went bankrupt in the Depression, he's now a successful LA contractor married to his first wife. Voss's assessment: "I know this guy's incredibly loyal. He's very practical. He's very hardworking... I know that we could do business together."
*Teaching application:* Labels create such deep connection that strangers reveal their entire life history in a single interaction. This isn't manipulation -- people WANT to be understood. The business application: in one interaction, you can assess character, predict behavior under stress, and build the trust foundation for future collaboration.
*Source:* lewis-howes

**The Three-Second Type Assessment**
> "The first thing is just be willing to take a step back first and a step back, usually it only takes three seconds. There's interesting data that actually indicates that a moment if you will, is three-second long. And in three seconds, you can get a good feel for the type." Listen to tone: Analyst (calm, measured, cold/distant), Assertive (fast, direct, time-pressured), Accommodator (warm, friendly, relationship-focused). Then adjust immediately.
*Teaching application:* You don't need extended observation to identify type. Three seconds of listening -- before you engage -- gives you enough to calibrate your approach. The pause itself is the technique. Most people rush into talking without this diagnostic moment.
*Source:* jordan-678
