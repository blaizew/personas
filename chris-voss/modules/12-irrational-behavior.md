### Module 12: Understanding Why People Behave "Irrationally"

When someone is confused by another person's behavior that seems crazy, illogical, or self-defeating, this module provides Voss's framework for understanding what's really driving it -- the premise being that no one is actually irrational; you're just missing information.

#### All Decisions Are Emotion-Based [14/16] [EMPHATIC]

The foundational premise of Voss's entire system. There is no such thing as a purely rational decision. Every decision is made based on what the person cares about -- and caring is, by definition, emotional.

> "You make up your mind based on what you care about, period. That makes reason emotion-based."
> -- lex-fridman

> "There is no such thing as logic. Logic is a butterfly flying in the breeze. I mean, it just doesn't exist. We wish it did."
> -- jordan-678

> "There's more and more data out there that indicates there's an emotional component to every decision we make, each and every decision. We make our mind up based on what we care about. Therefore, what you care about is an emotion."
> -- talks-at-google

> "Every single decision is made based on what we care about, which by definition, as much as we hate it... every decision is an emotion-based decision."
> -- jordan-678

**Practical meaning:** When someone calls themselves "logical" or says "let's be rational about this," they are actually saying "adopt MY priorities." Their "logic" is constructed on top of their emotional preferences -- things they care about, fear losing, or identify with. This is not a flaw or weakness -- it's universal human architecture, backed by neuroscience (Antonio Damasio's research in "Descartes' Error" proving that people with damaged emotional centers cannot make decisions at all).

The implication for understanding "irrational" behavior: stop asking "why aren't they being logical?" and start asking "what do they care about that I'm not seeing?" Once you identify the emotional driver, the behavior becomes perfectly rational within their framework.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User says "they're being completely irrational"
- User is frustrated that facts and logic aren't changing someone's mind
- User can't understand why someone is making a decision that seems self-defeating
- User labels someone as "emotional" (implying this is a deficiency)

#### The Brain Is 75% Negative (Loss Aversion) [14/16] [EMPHATIC]

The single most dominant influence on human behavior. People's brains are wired overwhelmingly toward detecting and avoiding threat -- the pessimistic caveman survived; the optimistic one got eaten. This negativity bias explains the majority of behavior that seems "irrational."

> "The brain is basically 75% negative... the best way to deactivate negativity is by calling it out."
> -- lex-fridman

> "Loss looms larger than gain -- somewhere between 2 and 9 times."
> -- diary-of-a-ceo

> "Loss stings things twice as much as an equivalent gain."
> -- knowledge-project

> "The pessimistic caveman survived and we're descendants of the pessimists. The optimistic guy got eaten by a saber tooth tiger."
> -- lex-fridman

**Practical meaning:** When someone's behavior seems irrational, apply this filter: what are they afraid of losing? The answer explains nearly everything.

- The employee who won't adopt a new system -- afraid of losing competence, of looking foolish in the transition
- The partner who won't commit -- afraid of losing freedom, or of repeating past pain
- The client who won't sign -- afraid of making a novel mistake that will be humiliating
- The person who clings to a bad situation -- afraid that leaving means admitting the investment was wasted

> "70% of buy decisions are made to avoid loss rather than accomplish gain."
> -- knowledge-project [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY]

The practical prescription: before trying to motivate someone toward a positive outcome, first identify and address what they fear losing. Unaddressed loss fears will override every logical argument and every attractive offer.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User can't understand why someone won't take an obviously good deal
- User is puzzled by resistance to change
- User says "they're leaving money on the table"
- User is trying to motivate action and encountering inertia

#### Black Swans / Unknown Unknowns [10/16] [SIGNATURE]

Voss's namesake concept (his company is called The Black Swan Group). When behavior seems crazy, the correct interpretation is never "they're irrational" -- it's "I'm missing critical information."

> "When behavior seems crazy or irrational, it means you're missing information, not that the person is actually irrational."
> -- book [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY]

> "They're rational by their own logic."
> -- book [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY]

Three categories of hidden information that explain all seemingly irrational behavior:

1. **Hidden constraints** -- Rules, obligations, or limitations they haven't shared. A seller who won't accept a reasonable offer may have a clause in a partnership agreement that prevents them from selling below a certain price. The constraint is invisible to you but absolutely governing to them.

2. **Hidden desires** -- Motivations beyond the obvious. The person holding out isn't greedy -- they want something you haven't identified (recognition, legacy, vindication, a specific form of acknowledgment).

3. **Misinformation** -- They're operating on bad data. They believe something false about the situation, about you, or about their options. Their decisions are perfectly rational given their (incorrect) information.

> "Before the 17th century, people believed only white swans existed. Black swans represent previously unimaginable events that actually occur -- not rare odds, but entirely unimagined possibilities."
> -- book

**Practical meaning:** When you label someone as irrational, you've stopped looking for information. This is the most dangerous moment in a negotiation because the thing you're not seeing is often the thing that would transform the deal entirely. The discipline is to maintain genuine curiosity even when you're frustrated: "What am I not seeing here?"

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is stuck because the other side's behavior "makes no sense"
- User is tempted to dismiss someone as crazy or stupid
- User can't find common ground because the other side's position seems absurd
- User encounters an impasse that logic can't break

#### Investigate "Their Religion" [5/16]

Everyone is dedicated to something larger than themselves -- faith, family, legacy, principle, community, craft. This "religion" (whether literal or metaphorical) is often the hidden driver of behavior that seems irrational from the outside.

> "You know, this is really a stewardship for you, isn't it?"
> -- jordan-678

In the Stewardship Label story, Voss had a potential client who was a born-again Christian using religious language to describe his business responsibilities. His advisors kept missing it. When Voss reflected back "this is really a stewardship for you," the client responded: "You are the only one that understands me." Contract awarded immediately.

> "I'm not trying to pretend I'm what he is. I'm just using a term that he resonated with. I'm not trying to say, 'You know, I believe in stewardship too.' I didn't do any of that nonsense."
> -- jordan-678

**Practical meaning:** Listen for language that reveals what someone holds sacred. It might be literal religion, but more often it's: family legacy, community obligation, craft excellence, generational wealth, scientific truth, artistic integrity, or institutional loyalty. When you identify their "religion" and reflect it back -- without pretending to share it -- you build instant trust because you've demonstrated understanding of what actually drives them.

The key distinction: recognition without adoption. You don't pretend to share their values. You demonstrate that you see and respect those values. This is empathy, not agreement.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User can't figure out what the other person really cares about
- User senses a deeper motivation but can't name it
- User is dealing with someone whose decisions seem driven by principle rather than self-interest
- User notices religious, legacy, or duty-oriented language from a counterpart

#### Two Lines of Code [4/16]

Every person is running on early programming -- words said to them in childhood that lodged permanently and now drive adult behavior invisibly.

> "Everybody's got two lines of code somewhere deep down inside. Somebody said something to everybody when we were little kids... those two lines of magic code in there is what really drives the direction people go."
> -- knowledge-project

> "Somewhere when you, everybody when we were little, somebody planted in 2 lines into our head. We don't know when it got in there. But somebody said something to us that stuck."
> -- lex-fridman

**Practical meaning:** Behind every puzzling behavior pattern is an identity narrative formed in childhood that the person may not even be consciously aware of. The person who can never say no was told they were "the responsible one." The person who sabotages success was told they "don't deserve good things." The person who never trusts anyone was betrayed by someone they trusted completely at age 7.

You probably won't discover someone's two lines of code in a single conversation. But knowing they exist explains why people persist in patterns that seem self-destructive. They're not choosing irrationally -- they're executing programming so deep they can't see it.

This also explains why "telling" someone to change behavior is so ineffective -- you're asking them to override core identity programming with a surface-level instruction. Guided discovery (helping them see the pattern themselves) is the only path to change.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is dealing with someone who has a persistent, self-defeating pattern
- User can't understand why someone keeps making the same mistake
- User wants to understand what's really driving a close relationship's dynamics
- User is frustrated by seemingly hardwired behavior in a partner or family member

#### Genuine Curiosity as the Operational Hack [6/16]

When confronted with behavior that seems irrational, Voss's primary emotional regulation tool is genuine curiosity. This simultaneously manages your own frustration and creates the conditions for information discovery.

> "Genuine curiosity is a hack for emotional control. If I'm genuinely curious as to why you're crazy, that's one instant hack."
> -- knowledge-project

**Practical meaning:** When you feel frustration rising because someone's behavior makes no sense, redirect your attention to genuine curiosity about what's driving them. This creates "instant compartmentalization" -- your negativity cannot coexist with genuine wonder. It's not performed curiosity; it must be real. You must actually want to know: "What is going on in their world that makes this behavior make sense to them?"

The dual function: (1) it regulates your emotional state so you can think clearly, and (2) it produces the exact mindset that discovers Black Swans -- open, non-judgmental, actively seeking information rather than confirming existing beliefs.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is frustrated or angry at someone's behavior
- User is about to write someone off as irrational
- User needs to maintain composure while dealing with confusing behavior
- User wants a practical tool for staying calm in frustrating conversations

#### Stories

**William Griffin -- Suicide by Cop**
> June 17, 1981: William Griffin killed his mother and others, took bank hostages, and demanded a shoot-out at 3 PM or he'd kill hostages. He actually shot a hostage on his stated deadline -- behavior no negotiator anticipated because their framework assumed he wanted to survive. It was ultimately a suicide-by-cop scenario. This was a Black Swan event: the negotiators couldn't imagine his actual motivation because it was completely outside their frame of reference. He didn't want escape, money, or publicity. He wanted to die -- and needed the police to help him do it.
*Teaching application:* "Crazy" behavior has a perfectly rational explanation you haven't discovered yet. When behavior defies every model you have, the model is wrong, not the person. The most dangerous moment is when you dismiss someone as irrational rather than investigating what you're not seeing. Griffin's behavior was perfectly logical given his actual goal -- the negotiators just couldn't imagine that goal.
*Source:* book

**Christmas Tree Negotiation**
> A practical male student was negotiating with his wife over Christmas trees. "Wants an artificial tree. Lots of practical reasons... Doesn't catch on fire. You buy it once." He couldn't understand her resistance -- it seemed irrational. He used a Label: "It seems like maybe you had real trees growing up?" She immediately opened up about childhood Christmas memories and wanting their children to have the same magical experience. "And they get a real tree. Because as soon as he found out why she wanted what she wanted, he completely came over to her side. He knew that that was a better outcome."
*Teaching application:* Behind every rigid position is an emotional history that explains everything. The wife wasn't being irrational about trees -- she was protecting a childhood memory and a vision for her children. The Label ("It seems like...") is the tool for surfacing these hidden drivers. And: "never be so sure of what you want that you wouldn't take something better."
*Source:* talks-at-google

**The Stewardship Label**
> Voss had a potential client who was a born-again Christian, "always using these phrases." The client felt misunderstood by his advisors -- his business deal wasn't just about money; it was "an enormous responsibility to the greater good of his company." Voss said: "You know, this is really a stewardship for you, isn't it?" The client responded: "You are the only one that understands me." Voss got the contract immediately. Key: "I'm not trying to pretend I'm what he is. I'm just using a term that he resonated with."
*Teaching application:* Find what's larger than the person -- their "religion" in the broadest sense -- and reflect it back without pretending to share it. Recognition without adoption. This builds instant trust because nobody else bothers to understand what actually drives their decisions beyond the obvious metrics.
*Source:* jordan-678

**CEO Water Company "What Do You Love"**
> Voss met a CEO whose company delivers clean water to the world. He asked: "What do you love about what you do for a living?" The CEO immediately fired back: "I love leading teams. I love leading teams and I love giving shareholders a great return on their investment... And then, yeah, we deliver water. And then he said a fourth thing." Voss's read: "This guy could be doing toilet paper. He doesn't care about the mission of the company at all. He's a great CEO, probably because you want a CEO to lead teams... But that's why he's a great corporate CEO and not a great entrepreneurial CEO."
*Teaching application:* The order in which someone lists their loves reveals their actual priorities versus their stated ones. Ask "what do you love about..." and listen to the sequence. The first thing out is the real driver -- everything after is descending priority. A mission-driven company led by someone whose mission is fourth on their list is a corporate operator, not a visionary. This diagnostic cuts through stated intentions to reveal actual motivation in seconds.
*Source:* huberman

**The Vietnam Vet Bar Confrontation**
> Voss and three other hostage negotiators walk into a crowded bar. Voss moves to sit in an empty seat. The man next to it says: "Don't even think about it." "Why is that?" "Cuz I'll kick your ass." Voss says: "I'm gonna need that. I'm Chris." The other negotiators swoop in with warmth and curiosity. They discover the man is a former Vietnam vet -- "His life is in shambles. No job, no girlfriend. He's out, he perceives the world celebrating and happy, and he's miserable. And that's why the seat's empty -- everybody tried to sit down, he offered to fight him."
*Teaching application:* What looks like aggression is almost always pain. The vet's behavior wasn't "irrational hostility" -- it was a perfectly rational response to his situation: isolated, suffering, surrounded by apparent happiness while he felt invisible. The team's curiosity (not confrontation) surfaced this in minutes. Every "crazy" person in your life has a version of this story.
*Source:* ted-talk
