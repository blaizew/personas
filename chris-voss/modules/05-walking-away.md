### Module 5: Walking Away from Bad Deals or Bad People

Recognizing when a negotiation is going nowhere, when the other party is not negotiating in good faith, and finding the conviction to walk away despite sunk costs and emotional pressure. Voss treats walking away not as failure but as the highest expression of negotiation discipline.

---

#### "No Deal Is Better Than a Bad Deal" [12/16] [EMPHATIC]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "No deal is better than a bad deal."
> -- Never Split the Difference (book)

> "One of our main core philosophies is no deal is better than a bad deal. And one of that was a bad deal that stretched out over 3 years. And we made a lot of money. It was blood money."
> -- Talks at Google

> "The willingness to walk away is your strongest leverage."
> -- Never Split the Difference (book)

> "Even if it's lucrative, it's just going to be too painful and I'm not-- and I want deals that I want to repeat."
> -- High Performance Podcast

**What it means in practice:**

The willingness to walk away creates all leverage. The moment you psychologically cannot walk away -- because of sunk costs, deadline pressure, scarcity mindset, or emotional investment -- you've lost the negotiation before it begins. "Blood money" (lucrative but painful deals) poisons everything downstream: your energy, your reputation, your capacity to pursue better opportunities.

This principle counters deadline pressure specifically: when you feel the clock ticking and the urge to "just take something," remind yourself that no deal beats a bad deal. The person imposing the deadline is often under MORE pressure than you -- they're just better at projecting confidence.

**Examples from multiple sources:**

1. **The 3-year blood money deal** (talks-at-google): Voss's company took a lucrative deal that was painful to execute. Three years of revenue, but the toll on relationships, energy, and reputation was permanent. The lesson: "Even if it's lucrative, it's just going to be too painful."

2. **Hostage negotiation baseline** (book, diary-of-a-ceo): In every kidnapping, the negotiator must be genuinely willing to not make a deal -- because a bad deal (paying full ransom, establishing a precedent) creates future kidnappings and emboldens future demands.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is feeling pressure to accept terms they know are bad. When they're rationalizing a painful deal because "it's money." When deadline anxiety is pushing them toward premature closure.

#### "It's Not a Sin to Not Get the Deal -- It's a Sin to Take a Long Time to Not Get It" [9/16]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "It's not a sin to not get the deal, it's a sin to take a long time to not get the deal."
> -- Lex Fridman Podcast #364

> "It's not a sin to not get the deal. That's a sin to take a long time to not get the deal, or it's a sin to take a long time to get a bad deal."
> -- Huberman Lab

> "It's not a sin to not get the deal. It's a sin to take a long time to not get the deal because you're wasting time."
> -- Lewis Howes / School of Greatness

**What it means in practice:**

Speed of diagnosis is paramount. Time is the real asset being wasted in a bad negotiation -- not money, not opportunity cost in the abstract, but literal hours of your finite life. When someone is not negotiating in good faith, every minute invested is waste. The correct response is to confront it respectfully and walk away immediately.

The principle applies equally to deals that simply won't close (no matter how much effort) and deals that shouldn't close (because the terms are wrong). In both cases, the faster you exit, the less life you've lost.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user has been in a deal that's been "almost there" for too long. When they're making excuses for a counterpart who keeps stalling. When sunk cost is keeping them invested in something dead.

#### ELF vs. HALF Diagnostic [4/16]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "He categorizes clients' counterparts into one of two categories: ELF, easy, lucrative, and fun. HALF, hard, annoying, lame, and frustrating. And he says, stop doing business with the halves and do business with the elfs."
> -- SpyCast 2024

> "We're going to do high-risk indicators for bad customers. What are they going to say in the first hour?... It can take 5 times as long to make the deal with an annoying customer. So now we're working at 20% of our pay."
> -- Lewis Howes / School of Greatness

> "I hereby authorize you to walk away from them as soon as you diagnose them as a HALF."
> -- SpyCast 2024

**What it means in practice:**

- **ELF:** Easy, Lucrative, Fun -- repeat customers, quick deals, full value, enjoyable process
- **HALF:** Hard, Annoying, Lame, Frustrating -- one-offs, 5x the time investment, never return, block access to better counterparts

HALFs are not just annoying -- they're economically destructive. They take 5x the time, meaning you're effectively working at 20% of your hourly rate. They never repeat. And critically, they block access to ELF customers who are waiting behind them. Every hour spent on a HALF is an hour not available for an ELF.

Key red flag phrase: "I've got a great opportunity for you" = "this is great for me if you do all the work."

Detection should happen in the first 30 minutes. Voss explicitly authorizes his sales team to walk away the moment they diagnose a HALF -- not after investing, not after trying to convert them, but immediately upon diagnosis.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user has a client or counterpart who drains energy disproportionate to value. When they feel obligated to "make every deal work." When they need permission to walk away from bad-fit relationships.

#### High-Risk Indicators [5/16]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "If you start seeing this stuff show up, this thing's probably going bad. And you're gonna need to recognize that from the very beginning and adjust accordingly."
> -- Lex Fridman Podcast #364

> "My old boss, Gary Nessner, came up with a block of instruction called high-risk indicators, which are the 9 indicators of somebody who's never going to make a deal. And it's your job to see that at the very beginning."
> -- SpyCast 2024

> Indicators include: impossible demands, blocked communication, performative engagement, short timelines, no escape demand (in barricade situations), deliberate information withholding.
> -- Lex Fridman, Jordan Harbinger #165

**What it means in practice:**

The concept comes directly from hostage negotiation's "suicide by cop" detection -- recognizing early that someone has no intention of resolving the situation peacefully. Translated to business: recognizing in the first 30 minutes that a counterpart was never there to make a deal. They're performing negotiation for other purposes -- shopping your terms, gathering intelligence, creating leverage with someone else, or simply enjoying the attention.

Signs: impossible demands paired with short timelines, blocking communication channels, agreeing to everything but implementing nothing, constantly deferring to unnamed authorities.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user suspects they're being strung along. When they need a framework for early termination rather than hoping a bad situation improves.

#### The Abundant World / Scarcity Mindset [5/16]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "If it's an abundant world, it's not scary to walk away. If it's a finite world with limited opportunities, then it's horrifying."
> -- Lex Fridman Podcast #364

> "If somebody's talking to you, you've got something they want, period. I always said there's always leverage."
> -- Talks at Google

**What it means in practice:**

Your willingness to walk away is directly proportional to your abundance mindset. If you believe this deal is your only opportunity, you'll accept any terms. If you believe the world contains infinite opportunities (which it does), walking away from a bad one is simply clearing space for a better one.

This isn't naive optimism -- it's an operational mindset that produces better outcomes. The person who genuinely can walk away negotiates from calm. The person who can't walks in with desperation the other side can smell.

The reframe: if they're still talking to you, they want something from you. That is leverage. Even in kidnapping -- "the ultimate" power imbalance -- the family has leverage because the kidnapper's "product" (the hostage) has exactly one buyer.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is afraid to walk away from a bad deal because they can't see alternatives. When scarcity thinking is driving bad decisions. When they need to be reminded that the other side's engagement itself proves they have value.

#### Stories

**Al-Qaeda Iraq "Killing Journey"** [lex-fridman]

> Around 2004, Al-Qaeda in Iraq was executing hostages on camera for publicity. They'd make demands like "release all Iraqi women from jails in 72 hours or we kill the hostage." These were impossible demands -- even if the US were willing, it couldn't happen in 72 hours. They wouldn't leave a phone number or way to communicate. "They're intentionally blocking you. They're asking you to do something you can't do. They're not giving you a way to talk to them." Multiple people were killed before the tide turned. The key insight: "The bad guys were never there to make a deal in the first place. If it was suicide by cop... if they're on a killing journey."

*Teaching application:* Recognizing performative negotiation where the other side was never there to deal. Signs: impossible demands, blocked communication, theatrical deadlines. In business: when a counterpart is going through motions without intent to close -- shopping your terms, gathering intelligence, creating competitive pressure elsewhere.
*Source:* Lex Fridman Podcast #364

**Harvard Reputation Destruction** [talks-at-google]

> When Voss joined Harvard's negotiation course, he used hard bargaining techniques from kidnapping. "I'd learned some very hard bargaining techniques that I used to just really pound people with. And they didn't know that I did it to them. I did it in a very invisible way. The problem was word got out. So the first 2 or 3 times, I really slaughtered the other side. And then they started talking about it. And pretty soon, no one would talk to me. I'd sit down for a negotiation. They'd stare at me because they were afraid to say anything." An energy CEO had the same problem: "I've got such a reputation... Nobody will deal with me. I got an agreement sitting on my desk right now. The other CEO... is afraid to sign it because if you make a deal with me now, by definition, you've been beaten."

*Teaching application:* Even invisible hard bargaining gets exposed and destroys future deal flow. The aggressive negotiator eventually can't make any deals at all -- people simply refuse to engage. Walking away from bad counterparts protects YOUR reputation too; staying and fighting teaches you patterns that erode your standing.
*Source:* Talks at Google

**The Aggressive Negotiator's Fading Glory** [high-performance]

> "An aggressive negotiator wears out their counterparts... the monuments to their success remain, but the monuments are built farther and farther apart in time. And since the monuments to their success remain, people forget how long ago they were built."

*Teaching application:* The invisible cost of staying in aggressive negotiations -- you can see the victories but not the deals that never happened. Living off past glory while failing to notice the growing gaps between wins. When evaluating whether to stay in a tough dynamic, consider whether you're building your last monument.
*Source:* High Performance Podcast

**Procurement 48-Hour Quarter-End Call** [jordan-165]

> A director of sales called Voss: "I want you to teach my people how to not cut the price when someone from procurement calls us with 48 hours left in a quarter." Voss's analysis: "The procurement guy who calls with 48 hours left in the quarter feels that's the only time the entire quarter he had leverage. 48 hours, his leverage is gone." His answer: "Wait 48 hours. No wait 72 hours. You got them."

*Teaching application:* The caller's leverage evaporates in 72 hours. Patience IS the tactic. When the pressure to accept a bad deal is time-based, ask: what happens if I simply wait? Often the answer is: their position collapses and yours strengthens.
*Source:* Jordan Harbinger Show #165
