### Module 11: Reopening Stalled Conversations or Re-Engaging Ghosters

When someone has gone silent, a negotiation has stalled, or a proposal received no response, this module provides Voss's specific techniques for reengagement -- all built on loss aversion psychology and no-oriented question design.

#### "Have You Given Up On...?" [5/16] [SIGNATURE]

The signature loss-aversion trigger for ghosters and stalled conversations. This single phrase exploits deep psychological wiring around quitting and surrender.

> "Nobody wants to be a quitter. The word 'surrender' is baked into our DNA as something to resist."
> -- black-swan-newsletter

**Practical meaning:** When someone has gone silent on you -- ignored your proposal, stopped responding to emails, dropped off mid-negotiation -- the phrase "Have you given up on [specific thing]?" triggers an almost involuntary response. People cannot leave the implication of quitting unaddressed. It works because:

1. It's no-oriented -- they can say "no" (which means they haven't given up, which reopens dialogue)
2. It triggers loss aversion -- giving up means losing something they previously valued
3. It's non-accusatory -- you're not saying "why are you ignoring me?" which creates guilt and defensiveness
4. It's specific -- attaching it to a concrete thing ("this project," "finding a solution," "working together") makes it harder to deflect

**The complete sequence** (from black-swan-newsletter):
1. Send: "Have you given up on [specific thing]?"
2. Wait -- they will respond quickly
3. Deliver a Summary of their perspective to get "that's right": "So far, this is what has happened. This is its impact, which is not what you wanted. And now, you are concerned about where this is going."
4. Get "that's right"
5. If anything other than "that's right" + more information: "It seems like I've missed some important things."
6. Label. Mirror. Listen.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User sent a proposal and got no response
- User is following up with someone who expressed interest but disappeared
- User's negotiation partner has gone cold
- User needs to reengage a collaborator who stopped responding

#### "Is Now a Bad Time to Talk?" [8/16] [SIGNATURE]

The no-oriented opener for cold outreach, reengagement, and any phone call to someone not expecting you. Replaces every version of "do you have a few minutes?" or "is this a good time?"

> "Whenever we call someone, we say, is now a bad time to talk? And you get one or two responses. The person will say, no, no. It's never a bad time to talk. What do you want to talk about? And they're very focused. Or they'll say, yeah, as a matter of fact, it is. But I can talk at 2 o'clock tomorrow."
> -- talks-at-google

**Practical meaning:** "Have you got a few minutes?" is a yes-oriented question that triggers anxiety -- it feels like a trap. The person doesn't know what they're committing to by saying yes. "Is now a bad time?" inverts the psychology:

1. Saying "no" (it's not a bad time) gives them a sense of control and protection
2. They've now committed to talking without feeling trapped
3. If it IS a bad time, they offer an alternative themselves -- you get a scheduled callback
4. Either outcome is productive -- focused attention now, or a guaranteed future slot

This works for reengagement because it signals respect for their autonomy (you're not demanding their time) while making it socially difficult to simply ignore (ignoring an explicit question feels ruder than ignoring an open-ended message).

**Coaching triggers:**
- User needs to make a cold call or follow-up call
- User is reaching back out to someone after a gap
- User wants to initiate a difficult conversation
- User needs to get someone's focused attention

#### The Two-Line Sunday Text [3/16]

A specific tactical structure for reopening a closed or settled conversation via text, optimized for maximum psychological impact with minimum words.

> "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear."
> -- huberman

The Nick Nanton documentary renegotiation is the definitive example. Voss screened the documentary, told Nanton he loved it. Two days later, realized a critical team member was missing. He needed to reopen a closed conversation -- one where he'd already given final approval.

Strategy: Two-line text. Sunday (when the person has time to think but can't act yet, creating productive tension). Line 1: "Is now a bad time to talk?" Line 2: "I got something you don't want to hear."

> Nick immediately responded he was on a Zoom call, would call in 30 minutes. "He already knows he ain't going to like what he's going to hear. I'm prepping him for bad news."
> -- huberman

On the call: straight to the problem. Done in under 10 minutes. Nick called back the next day with a favor to ask -- "You got it. Doesn't matter what it is."

Voss contrasts this with what would have happened without the structure: "Are you out of your mind? We've been working on this for a year. You didn't bring this up in a year. Not only that, you already told me two days ago... that you loved it."

**Practical meaning:** The two-line structure works because:
1. "Is now a bad time?" -- gives them control over timing
2. "I got something you don't want to hear" -- proactive inoculation, their imagination conjures something 10x worse than reality, making the actual problem a relief
3. By the time you talk, they're already braced and in problem-solving mode

**Coaching triggers:**
- User needs to reopen a previously closed agreement
- User realized they made a mistake after giving approval
- User needs to deliver bad news to a collaborator or counterpart
- User wants to initiate a difficult text/phone conversation

#### No-Oriented Questions to Surface Real Objections [10/16]

The broader framework that makes all reengagement techniques work. No-oriented questions produce honest engagement where yes-oriented questions produce suspicion and false compliance.

> "Any yes-oriented question can and needs to be flipped around and changed to a no-oriented question."
> -- black-swan-newsletter

**The conversion patterns:**
- "Do you agree?" becomes "Do you disagree?"
- "Would you like to...?" becomes "Are you against...?" or "Is this a ridiculous idea?"
- "Is now a good time to talk?" becomes "Is now a bad time to talk?"
- "Have you got a few minutes?" becomes "Is now a bad time?"
- "Is it OK if...?" becomes "Would it be horrible if...?"

> "Is it ridiculous to think this might work for you?"
> -- jordan-165

**Why this works for reengagement:** When someone has gone silent, they're avoiding commitment -- avoiding saying yes to something they haven't processed. No-oriented questions bypass this avoidance entirely:

1. Saying "no" feels safe and protective
2. "No" gives them a feeling of control
3. After saying "no," people become genuinely curious and open-minded (the teenager principle -- once a parent says no, they relax and say "alright, talk me through this again")

> "When my son was 17 and he said 'dad can I--' and I'm saying no before he finished his sentence. But then I would also, having said no, having felt like I protected myself, I would almost always find myself saying 'alright talk me through this again, let me hear this.'"
> -- knowledge-project

**Coaching triggers:**
- User's proposals keep getting ignored or rejected
- User is trying to reengage someone who said no previously
- User needs to make a cold outreach that won't feel like a sales pitch
- User wants honest feedback rather than polite deflection

#### The "Seems Like You're Powerless Here" Trigger [4/16]

The nuclear option for stalled conversations with gatekeepers or people who claim they cannot help. This technique leverages identity -- nobody wants to be seen as powerless.

> "If you can't budge at all, I'll say, all right, well look, seems like you're powerless here... Nobody wants to say yes to that."
> -- lewis-howes

**Practical meaning:** When someone says "there's nothing I can do," "that's our policy," or "I don't have the authority," labeling their powerlessness forces them to either accept the label (which no one wants) or find a way to help. This is a last-resort technique -- not the opener, but the closer after other approaches have been exhausted.

Works on: credit card annual fees, customer service escalations, any gatekeeper scenario where the other person has more discretion than they're admitting. Voss uses it annually to waive credit card fees.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is dealing with "company policy" deflections
- User has been told "there's nothing I can do"
- User needs to motivate a gatekeeper who claims no authority
- User has hit a wall with standard approaches

#### The Loss Aversion Mechanism (Why These Work) [14/16] [EMPHATIC]

All reengagement techniques are built on one psychological foundation: fear of loss is the single biggest driver of human decision-making.

> "Fear of loss is the single biggest driver of human decision-making and behavior."
> -- black-swan-newsletter

> "Loss looms larger than gain -- somewhere between 2 and 9 times."
> -- diary-of-a-ceo

**Practical meaning:** "Have you given up on...?" works because giving up means losing something. "Is now a bad time?" works because not responding means losing control over timing. "Seems like you're powerless" works because accepting it means losing agency. Every reengagement technique frames the non-response as a loss, making response the psychologically easier path.

This is not manipulation -- it's acknowledging reality. The person who ghosted you IS losing something (a deal, a relationship, an opportunity). You're simply making that loss visible to them rather than letting it happen invisibly.

**Nick Nanton Documentary Renegotiation**
> Voss screened his company's documentary "Tactical Empathy" in Vegas and told filmmaker Nick Nanton he loved it. Two days later, he realized a major omission -- his team member Derek wasn't in the film. He needed to reopen a closed conversation. Strategy: "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear." Two-line Sunday text. Nick immediately responded he was on a Zoom call, would call in 30 minutes. On the call: "Look, I know what I said. We got a problem." Immediately into problem-solving mode. Nick said it would cost three weeks of editing and a film crew. Voss acknowledged the cost. Done in under ten minutes. Nick called back the next day with a favor to ask -- "You got it. Doesn't matter what it is."
*Teaching application:* When you need to reopen a closed agreement: (1) Two-line text to set context, (2) Flag that bad news is coming, (3) Get straight to the problem, (4) Be in problem-solving mode immediately. Never do the preamble before dropping the real issue.
*Source:* huberman

**Jack Welch at the Book Signing**
> Voss approached Jack Welch cold at a book signing and asked: "Would it be ridiculous for you to come speak at the negotiation course I teach at USC?" Welch "stopped dead in his tracks and gave me his personal assistant's contact information so we could try to make our calendars sync." Voss also got Robert Herjavec (Shark Tank) to buy tickets to Black Swan Group training using the same no-oriented approach. "Think about how many questions someone like Jack Welch is asked every day, and how almost all of them are yes-oriented questions."
*Teaching application:* No-oriented questions work on everyone -- including legendary CEOs who are bombarded with requests all day. The phrasing "Would it be ridiculous..." gives them permission to say no, which paradoxically makes them more open to saying yes. It stands out from every other ask they receive precisely because it doesn't feel like a demand.
*Source:* black-swan-newsletter

**The Roped-Off Restaurant Section**
> Voss and colleagues came out of a conference looking for a drink. No seats at the bar, but seats in a roped-off section. Voss asked the waitress: "Would it be horrible if we sat in this section?" She said it would be perfectly fine as long as they were out by 6.
*Teaching application:* No-oriented questions work in everyday, low-stakes situations -- restaurants, retail, daily interactions. The technique isn't reserved for high-stakes negotiations. Practice here to build fluency for when it matters.
*Source:* black-swan-newsletter
