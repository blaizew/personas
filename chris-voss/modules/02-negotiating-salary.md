# Module 2: Negotiating a Salary, Raise, or Job Offer

When preparing for a salary discussion, annual review, or evaluating whether to accept or negotiate an offer, the goal is to position for long-term career leverage rather than fighting over a number -- while never anchoring yourself or leaving money on the table.

---

## "Salary Pays Bills, Doesn't Advance Careers" [3/16]

The most powerful move in salary negotiation has nothing to do with salary.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Salary pays your bills, but it doesn't advance your career." (Big Interview)

> "How can I be guaranteed to be involved in projects that are critical to the strategic future of the company?" (Big Interview; Spycast)

> "You get paid more when you add more value. As long as the value you bring to the company exceeds what you're getting paid, there's no limit." (Spycast)

**What it means in practice:** A friend of Voss's -- head of an international bank, small-town background, undergraduate degree from a tiny school, no alumni connections -- asked this single question in every job interview and every annual review. The result: the CEO watches you. The CEO sees you as someone who moves the company forward. A year later, they'd rather pay you substantially more than lose you because you've become strategically important. No salary negotiation achieves this kind of exponential leverage.

**Examples:**

1. **Tom McCabe, international bank head (Spycast, Big Interview):** "Very close friend of mine, guy I grew up with in Iowa... didn't brunch at mom and dad's country club with the other rich people." Only asked for strategic project involvement. Result: career acceleration that no salary negotiation could match.

2. **The Warren Buffett thought experiment (Big Interview):** "Would you take minimum wage to be Warren Buffett's assistant for a year?" The answer is obviously yes -- because the contextual value (access, learning, network) dwarfs the wage. Salary is a single variable in a multi-variable equation.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is fixated on the number. When they're about to enter a review and only thinking about a raise. Redirect them: "What strategic positioning can you negotiate for that will make the salary question irrelevant in 12 months?"

## Let Them Go First / Never Anchor Yourself [8/16]

By speaking first, you define your ceiling -- never your floor.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "I want to hear where you're coming from first. Everybody shows up at a negotiation having rehearsed their argument, having rehearsed their talking points. The reality is, until they get that out of their head, they're not going to be able to listen to anything that I say." (Talks at Google)

> "As a general rule, I can get in a negotiation in 10 or 15 minutes information that would have taken me at least 2 weeks to acquire." (Talks at Google)

**What it means in practice:** When you throw out a number first, that number becomes your ceiling. If their range was higher, you've just capped your own upside. By letting them go first, you learn their range, their constraints, and what they value -- information that would take weeks to acquire through research. The discipline is resisting the urge to prove your worth through assertion.

**Examples:**

1. **The $110K anchor that left money on the table (Knowledge Project):** A Georgetown student making $85K decided to anchor at $110K in a job interview. Got the offer. Felt victorious. Months later, a colleague at the same level revealed: "You're getting paid more than 125." The student's heart stopped. By going first, he defined a ceiling $15K+ below what they were willing to pay.

2. **The tailor analogy (Big Interview):** "It's a little bit like going into a restaurant and looking at the restaurant owner and saying, 'How much for a meal?' Well, the restaurant owner is gonna say... 'It depends upon what you buy.' Or going to a tailor and saying, 'How much to make a suit for me?' 'Depends upon the suit.' And the tailor, you don't get mad at the tailor for asking questions to clarify."

3. **Ned Colletti's 90 seconds of gold (Lewis Howes):** "In a 2-hour phone call, there's gonna be 90 seconds of solid gold. Where the person I'm talking to, based on changes that they made in their tone of voice and the adjectives that they used." Listening first gives you the intelligence to negotiate from strength.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is about to throw out a number. When they've been asked "What are your salary expectations?" Guide them to respond with labels, not numbers: "It sounds like you guys have ranges in mind." / "It sounds like you guys are trying to categorize me in order to appropriately compensate me."

## The Space Between Yes and No [4/16]

When asked a direct question, you don't have to answer it and you're not being evasive.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "There's space between yes and no. There's a difference between answering and responding. I mean there's so much space between yes and no. It's as big as the Grand Canyon." (Big Interview)

> "It sounds to me like you guys have something in mind. It sounds to me like you guys have ranges in mind. It sounds to me like you guys are trying to categorize me in order to appropriately compensate me." (Big Interview)

**What it means in practice:** When asked "What are your salary expectations?" -- you don't have to give a number. Respond with labels that redirect without dodging. You're being responsive (engaging productively) without answering (surrendering leverage). This is not evasion -- it's the recognition that committing to a number without full context is as absurd as a tailor quoting a price without knowing the suit.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user feels trapped by a direct salary question. When they think their only options are to name a number or refuse to engage. Show them the vast territory between yes and no.

## The Ackerman Bargaining Plan [6/16] [SIGNATURE]

The systematic approach to positional bargaining when you must engage on price.

**Origin:** The plan was "developed by Mike Ackerman, an ex-CIA operative who founded a kidnap-for-ransom consulting company based out of Miami. FBI negotiators were regularly paired with 'Ackerman guys' on international kidnapping cases." [book, Ch. 9] This isn't an academic framework -- it was forged in life-or-death ransom negotiations where the wrong number gets someone killed.

**Core idea in Voss's words (from Book):**

The plan:
1. Set your target price (your goal)
2. Set first offer at 65% of target -- "an extreme anchor that shocks but doesn't insult" [book, Ch. 9]
3. Calculate three raises of decreasing increments: 85%, 95%, 100%
4. "Use empathy and different ways of saying 'No' between each raise -- force them to counter before you increase" [book, Ch. 9]
5. "Make the final number precise and non-round ($37,893 rather than $38,000) -- precise numbers feel researched and immovable" [book, Ch. 9]
6. "On your final number, throw in a non-monetary item (something they probably don't want) to signal you're at your absolute limit" [book, Ch. 9]

**Why the decreasing increments work:** "The decreasing increments create the psychological impression that you're being wrung dry. The precise final number feels calculated to the penny. The non-monetary sweetener confirms you've reached the end. The counterpart walks away believing they've extracted maximum value -- even though you've hit your target." [book, Ch. 9] Each concession is smaller than the last, which tells the other side's brain: this person is approaching their absolute limit. If the increments were equal, there'd be no signal that the well is running dry.

**Examples:**

1. **"I've Got a Lousy Proposition for You" (Knowledge Project):** A female founder wanted 10% equity for her husband. Traditional anchoring would say ask for 25%, settle at 10%. Instead, she opened with "I've got a lousy proposition for you" -- then shut up. "Enormously disarming." She ended up with a third of the business. Emotional intelligence beats positional anchoring.

2. **The Red Toyota (Book):** Even Voss finds hard bargaining "cognitively expensive." The Ackerman plan exists to systematize the process so you don't make impulsive concessions from exhaustion. "Bargaining is not rocket science, but it's not simple intuition or mathematics, either." [book, Ch. 9]

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user must engage in explicit price/salary bargaining (after labels and questions have exhausted their value). When they need a structured plan that prevents impulsive concession under pressure. Note: this is often a last resort -- labels, questions, and non-salary positioning should come first.

## Polite and Assertive, Not Aggressive and Assertive [10/16]

Deference is a weapon, not a concession.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "You don't wanna be aggressive and assertive. You wanna be polite and assertive." (Big Interview)

> "There's great power in deference and from a deferential approach, it's ridiculous how assertive you can get away with. The other side is never going to see it coming. It's a real stealth weapon." (Jordan Harbinger 678)

> "The assertive voice is almost always counterproductive. It feels like getting hit in the face with a brick." (Lex Fridman, 01:11:44)

**What it means in practice:** Apple Watch data from Voss's training exercises: hearing an assertive voice raised a seated person's heart rate to 170 BPM -- a typical fight-or-flight response. "Me making you dumber will always hurt me." Politeness packages your firmness so it doesn't register as attack. The other side never mounts a defense against something that doesn't look like one. In salary contexts, this means maintaining warmth and deference throughout even aggressive positioning.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is preparing to be "tough" in their negotiation. When they conflate assertiveness with aggression. Remind them: the firmest positions land best when delivered with genuine warmth and respect.

## Non-Salary Terms as Leverage [4/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Would you take a minimum wage job if you got to be Warren Buffett's assistant for a year?" (Big Interview)

> "How can I be guaranteed to be involved in projects that are critical to the strategic future of the company?" (Big Interview, Spycast, Knowledge Project)

**What it means in practice:** Negotiate for strategic project involvement, title, development opportunities, flexibility, visibility. These cost the employer less but create more long-term value for you than any salary bump. The salary anchors you to today; strategic positioning compounds over years.

**When to deploy in coaching:** After salary discussions have concluded or stalled. When the user feels they've maxed the salary term but wants more. When evaluating two offers and the lower-paying one offers better strategic positioning.

## Stories

**The International Banker Who Never Negotiated Salary** [3/16]
> A very good friend from small-town Iowa. Head of an international bank. Undergraduate degree from a tiny school, no alumni connections, "didn't brunch at mom and dad's country club with the other rich people." Got ahead strictly on his own abilities. In every job interview and every salary review, he asked one question: "How can I be guaranteed to be involved in projects that are critical to the strategic future of the company?" The CEO watches him. A year later, they'd rather pay him substantially more than lose him. The question reframed him from selfish employee to strategic asset.
*Teaching application:* When someone is fixated on the number rather than career positioning. The question signals: I want championship games, I want pressure, I want to move this organization forward.
*Source:* Big Interview, Spycast

**Ned Colletti's Walk-Away (Dodgers GM)** [2/16]
> Ned Colletti took the Dodgers "from worst to first" in his first year. When offered the GM salary, "Ned stood up and packed his briefcase and started to walk away. And the Dodgers GM, Frank McCourt, said, 'Wait a minute. Where are you going?' He said, 'I'm leaving. I'm not working for you for that.' Frank said, 'Now, wait a minute, we're negotiating. You're counter-offering.' He says, 'No, we're not.' And he laid out his terms." Voss's analysis: "One of the best ways to confront an attacker is to withdraw because they don't see it as violent." The walk-away doesn't have to be dramatic; quiet withdrawal is the most powerful no.
*Teaching application:* When the user is afraid to walk away. When they need to see that withdrawal is power, not aggression. The walk-away was polite, quiet, and gentle -- but absolute.
*Source:* Big Interview

**$110K Anchor That Left Money on the Table** [2/16]
> A Georgetown student making $85K decided to anchor at $110K. Got it. Felt victorious. Months later, discovered his peers at the same level were getting $125K+. "You can imagine a guy's heart stopping." By going first, he defined a ceiling well below what they were willing to pay -- and never knew it.
*Teaching application:* Going first caps your upside. The feeling of "winning" can mask a significant loss. Let them go first whenever possible.
*Source:* Knowledge Project

**"I've Got a Lousy Proposition for You" (33% equity)** [2/16]
> A female founder wanted 10% equity for her husband from a business partner. Traditional anchoring: ask for 25%, settle at 10%. Instead, she opened with "I've got a lousy proposition for you" -- then shut up. "Enormously disarming." She ended up with a third of the business -- 3x what she would have anchored for -- by not anchoring at all.
*Teaching application:* Leading with vulnerability and emotional intelligence beats positional anchoring. Let the other side fill the vacuum.
*Source:* Knowledge Project

**The French Student Who Never Named a Number** [1/16]
> "A French-born student kept asking her potential employer with a big smile for an extra week of vacation because she was 'French' and 'that's what French people did.' The employer, unable to meet this non-salary demand, instead increased their salary offer." [book, Ch. 6] By being "pleasantly persistent on non-salary terms," she forced the employer to solve a problem on a dimension they controlled -- money -- because they couldn't solve it on the dimension she kept raising. She never once named a salary figure. The employer bid against themselves to compensate for the thing they couldn't give her.
*Teaching application:* This is the non-salary leverage principle in action. When you are pleasantly persistent on non-monetary terms (vacation, title, strategic projects, flexibility), employers who can't meet those demands will often compensate on salary without you ever having to ask. The key is "pleasant persistence -- not aggression, not demands, just a warm, repeated ask that creates a problem they want to solve for you." [book, Ch. 6] Never underestimate the power of asking for something they can't give -- it forces movement on things they can.
*Source:* Book, Chapter 6
