### Module 4: Saying No Without Damaging the Relationship

Declining requests, pushing back on unreasonable demands, and setting boundaries without creating hostility or burning bridges. The Voss system treats "no" not as a single word but as a graduated process that preserves relationships while holding firm positions.

---

#### "How Am I Supposed to Do That?" [14/16] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "The ultimate way to say no in the entire world is just kind of kindly and calmly with the late-night FM DJ voice, just to say, 'How am I supposed to do that?'"
> -- Jordan Harbinger Show #678

> "This calibrated question, delivered with genuine curiosity and the right tone of voice, is one of the most effective tools in a negotiator's arsenal. It's not confrontational, yet it stops aggressive demands in their tracks."
> -- Black Swan Newsletter

**What it means in practice:**

This is not a question seeking information. It is a method of saying no without the word "no" ever leaving your mouth. Delivered calmly with the late-night FM DJ voice, it shifts the entire problem-solving burden onto the person making the demand. They must now confront the logistics of their own request -- and often realize it's unreasonable without you having to say so.

The technique requires persistence. Repeat it 4-5 times. Each repetition forces deeper reflection. The signal that you've maxed a particular term: the other side says "if you want it, you have to do it." At that point, move on to the next issue.

**Examples from multiple sources:**

1. **Harvard / Bob Mnookin** (jordan-678): Voss stopped Mnookin, head of Harvard's negotiation program, and handed him his FBI business card. Mnookin posed a test: "We got your son. We need a million dollars. We'll kill him in the morning." Voss replied with the FM DJ voice: "How am I supposed to do that?" Mnookin "just blanked, he didn't know how to react."

2. **Hollywood Hills $20K Rental** (lewis-howes): A student negotiating a luxury rental for a wealthy client used "How am I supposed to do that?" repeatedly. The realtor shifted terms, cut the price. After more negotiation: "How's my client supposed to pay that?" The realtor responded: "If your client wants a house, he's got to pay it." Voss: "Bang, you got a deal. When the other side says, if you want it, you have to do it... you've maxed that term. Now you move on to something else."

3. **Live Hostage Roleplay with Steven Bartlett** (diary-of-a-ceo): Bartlett plays a hostage-taker demanding a car in 60 seconds. Voss deploys "How am I supposed to do that?" combined with chaos-painting. After ~2 minutes, Bartlett asks "How long do you need?" -- extending his own deadline without Voss ever saying no. Bartlett's internal monologue: "All the questions were making me ponder... making me abandon my focus."

4. **Kevin O'Leary on Shark Tank** (lewis-howes): O'Leary delivers his classic "take it or leave it, yes or no." An entrepreneur responds: "How am I supposed to do that?" followed by "How am I supposed to agree to those terms and pay you back your money?" O'Leary starts thinking instead of demanding. Barbara Corcoran, seeing the opening, jumps in with a better deal.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user needs to decline an unreasonable demand (from a boss, client, partner) without creating hostility. When they're facing an ultimatum and feel trapped between acceptance and confrontation. When they need to test how firm a position really is without escalating.

#### The Five-Stage "No" Ladder [3/16]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

From *Never Split the Difference* (book), the graduated rejection system:

1. "How am I supposed to do that?"
2. "Your offer is very generous, but that doesn't work for me"
3. "I'm afraid I just can't do that"
4. "I'm sorry, no" (delivered gently)
5. Direct "No" with downward inflection and tone of regard

> Most counterparts adjust by step 2-3.
> -- Never Split the Difference (book)

**What it means in practice:**

Most people never need to reach step 5. The ladder provides a systematic path from soft deflection to firm refusal, each step slightly more explicit than the last. The beauty is that steps 1-3 never contain the word "no" -- they communicate refusal through impossibility framing, gracious decline, and regretful inability.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user needs a rehearsed sequence for a known confrontation. When they tend to jump straight to "no" (burning bridges) or never say no at all (getting walked over). Give them the full ladder and let them practice each rung.

#### Deference as Stealth Assertion [10/16]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "There's great power in deference and from a deferential approach, it's ridiculous how assertive you can get away with. The other side is never going to see it coming. It's a real stealth weapon."
> -- Jordan Harbinger Show #678

> "Deference works on everybody... I do not want the negotiation approach that I used to be restricted."
> -- Knowledge Project #27

**What it means in practice:**

Politeness packages refusal so it doesn't register as an attack. The other side cannot mount a defense against something that doesn't look like aggression. This works at all levels of the power hierarchy: superiors feel entitled to deference (satisfied), peers feel respected (appreciative), subordinates feel nurtured (loyal). It's a 360-degree tool with no downside.

The deferential billionaire (knowledge-project): A billionaire in New York -- older, wealthier, more socially established than Trump -- uses deference when speaking to Trump. He calls him "president" and is deferential. He doesn't need anything from Trump. "His life is on a higher-functioning level across the board." Yet he uses deference "because people love when somebody doesn't have to be deferential but is." Result: more influence over Trump than almost anyone else.

**Examples from multiple sources:**

1. **The bank robber who used it for evil** (jordan-678, diary-of-a-ceo): The Chase Manhattan bank robber manipulated everyone by appearing helpless and deferential -- "there are all these other people with me and I don't know what they're going to do." He was the mastermind but appeared to be the least threatening person in the room.

2. **Tom Girardi** (lewis-howes): Top trial attorney in California, "an immovable, unrelenting opponent, and that's why he's so successful, and he's ridiculously nice about it." When you argue with him, he immediately starts talking about "how you and I are going to be successful together 10 years from now."

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user needs to push back on someone who outranks them. When they confuse politeness with weakness. When their natural assertiveness is getting them labeled as difficult.

#### "I Don't Think It's Gonna Happen, But I'll Check" [3/16]

**Core idea in Voss's own words:**

> "I basically said, I don't think it's gonna happen. But I'll check, because I'm trying to shift us out of an adversarial into a collaborative conversation... I don't want to mislead you."
> -- Diary of a CEO

Voss contrasts this explicitly with "I'll try" -- which he calls "the classic American lie" or "lie in the English language." Everyone knows "I'll try" means no. But "I don't think it's gonna happen, but I'll check" is honest about the unlikelihood while offering genuine advocacy.

**What it means in practice:**

This converts the dynamic from opponent vs. opponent into collaborator vs. shared obstacle. You're being transparent about the low probability while positioning yourself as an advocate rather than an adversary. The other side hears honesty (which builds trust) and effort (which generates goodwill) simultaneously.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user needs to decline without closing the door permanently. When they want to preserve a relationship while being realistic. When "no" feels too final but "yes" would be dishonest.

**Hollywood Hills $20K Rental** [lewis-howes]

> A student negotiating a $20K/month luxury rental -- the other side assumes his wealthy client has unlimited money. The student says "How am I supposed to do that?" and they shift terms, cut price. After more negotiation: "How's my client supposed to pay that?" The realtor responds: "If your client wants a house, he's got to pay it." Voss's analysis: "Bang, you got a deal. When the other side says, if you want it, you have to do it... you've maxed that term. Now you move on to something else." The deal closed below $20K with better terms. "In a deferential way, they still feel in control... if you don't make the deal at that point, what they say after the fact, they say, 'I didn't make a deal but I'd deal with them again.'"

*Teaching application:* Repeat until you hear "if you want it, you have to do it" -- that's the signal you've maxed the term. Preserves the relationship whether or not you close.
*Source:* Lewis Howes / School of Greatness
