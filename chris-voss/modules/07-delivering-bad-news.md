### Module 7: Delivering Bad News or Having Difficult Conversations

When you need to fire someone, break up with someone, deliver disappointing results, end a partnership, or tell someone something they don't want to hear -- the temptation is to soften, stall, or rehearse how to make it "gentle." Voss rejects all of that. Speed is mercy, warning is a gift, and the last thing you say matters more than anything before it.

---

#### "There's No Gentle Way to Cut Somebody's Head Off" [4/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "He looked at me and he... There's no gentle way to cut somebody's head off."
> *Source: huberman*

This comes from Arthur Caliandro, Norman Vincent Peale's protege and "one of the best human beings I've ever met," whom Voss consulted when struggling over whether to fire a nonprofit executive director. Voss expected counsel toward a gentler path. Instead, Caliandro delivered this line -- which Voss has taught ever since.

**What it means in practice:** When delivering definitively bad news (firing, breakup, ending a relationship), speed is mercy. Don't draw it out trying to save yourself. The agonizing over "how" is often self-serving -- it protects the deliverer from discomfort, not the receiver from pain.

> "Seeking a gentle way to deliver definitively bad news is often about saving yourself, not the other person."
> *Source: huberman*

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Arthur Caliandro / Nonprofit Firing (huberman):** Voss was involved in a nonprofit affiliated with a church and was agonizing over whether to fire the executive director. He went to the minister expecting gentle counsel. Instead: the fastest, most direct path IS the kindest path.

2. **The general principle applied to breakups (huberman):** Voss extends this to personal relationships -- "When delivering terminal bad news, don't draw it out trying to save yourself. Warn them, pause no more than 3 seconds, deliver."

3. **Business context (knowledge-project, future-organization):** In professional contexts, leaders who agonize for weeks over a firing are torturing themselves AND the organization. Everyone else already knows the person should go -- they're watching to see if leadership will act.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is agonizing over HOW to deliver bad news -- spending days rehearsing scripts, asking "how do I say this nicely?" The answer is: you don't make it nice. You make it fast. Warn them, deliver, be compassionate in the aftermath.

#### Warn Them Bad News Is Coming [8/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "People are ridiculously resilient to pain. If warned and then that, you lower the boom. You're not going to like what I have to say. It's going to be heartbreaking. You're going to hate me. Hesitate no more than 3 seconds."
> *Source: huberman*

> "You're not going to like what I'm getting ready to say. It's going to sound really harsh... You will imagine something 10 times worse than what I have to say and what I have to say will actually be a relief."
> *Source: high-performance*

**What it means in practice:** Never ambush with bad news. But also never cushion with irrelevant positives (the "mud sandwich" or "compliment sandwich"). The technique is simple: say bad news is coming, pause no more than 3 seconds, deliver. Humans brace incredibly well when given even a moment to prepare. Their imagination conjures something 10x worse than reality, making your actual news a relief by comparison.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **The Nick Nanton Documentary Text (huberman):** Voss texts a filmmaker on Sunday: "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear." Two lines. Nick immediately knows bad news is coming and is mentally prepared before the conversation even starts. Resolution in under 10 minutes.

2. **The Restaurant Table (high-performance):** On a first date at a steakhouse, Voss tells the hostess: "I am going to be the worst customer that has ever walked into this restaurant." Pauses three breaths. Her survival wiring imagines something horrible. Then: "We walked right by a table that I'd really love to sit at." She's so relieved she seats them immediately.

3. **The Inoculation Principle (lex-fridman, diary-of-a-ceo):** Voss says "What I'm getting ready to say is probably gonna offend you" before lectures. The audience imagines something terrible. Whatever follows is a relief.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Before any difficult conversation -- delivering a rejection, sharing disappointing results, firing someone, breaking up. The script is: "You're not going to like what I have to say." Pause. Deliver. Do NOT fill the pause with pleasantries, context-setting, or "how was your weekend?"

#### Straight Shooter vs. Blunt [5/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "It's what I would call the difference between being blunt and being a straight shooter. A straight shooter tells you the truth. They just tell it in a way that lands softly."
> *Source: huberman*

**What it means in practice:** Directness and gentleness are not opposites. You can be completely honest while being considerate of how truth lands. The distinction is tone and framing, not content. A blunt person says "You're getting fired because you're incompetent." A straight shooter says "You're not going to like what I have to say. We're letting you go. Here's what happens next."

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Huberman interview:** Voss draws this distinction explicitly when discussing how to deliver bad news. The content is identical -- the delivery makes one version humane and the other cruel.

2. **Knowledge-project:** Voss's advice to his younger self: "Don't change any of your positions, don't go in any other directions -- just be nicer about it. You get a lot farther." Same assertion, warmer delivery.

3. **Future-organization:** Voss reframes vulnerability as "just being honest" -- the behavior looks the same, but the internal frame (honesty vs. deliberate vulnerability) determines whether it feels natural.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone confuses being honest with being harsh. When they say "I'm just being direct" as an excuse for cruelty. The coaching is: keep the honesty, change the packaging. Warm tone + direct content = respected straight shooter.

#### Fire on Monday, Not Friday [2/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Fire them on a Monday, not on a Friday."
> *Source: huberman*

**What it means in practice:** On Friday, the fired person can't take action -- they can't look for a job on Saturday, can't call contacts, can't do anything productive. Two days of helpless misery. On Monday, they can immediately begin moving forward -- make calls, apply, meet people. The humane timing is the one that enables action.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is scheduling difficult conversations and choosing timing. The principle extends beyond firing: deliver bad news when the person can DO something about it, not when they're trapped with it.

#### Last Impression Is the Lasting Impression [7/16] [EMPHATIC]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "The last impression is a lasting impression. It doesn't -- you know, we have gravity, we can't explain why gravity works, but you're still not going to step off the balcony because gravity's there."
> *Source: lewis-howes*

> "People remember two parts of an interaction: the most intense moment and how you made them feel at the end."
> *Source: black-swan-newsletter*

**What it means in practice:** Structure difficult conversations so the final moments leave a positive trajectory. It's fine -- even preferable -- to start tough, because ending high creates an upward emotional arc. The person will carry the feeling of the ending far longer than the content of the middle.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Oprah's Private Confrontations (high-performance):** Oprah has "taken some highly volatile people to the woodshed over their misbehavior." She delivers ultimatums ("it's my way or the highway"). But her consistent closer: "No matter what you decide, you have to understand that I will always love you and I will always support you." This transforms confrontation into care.

2. **The Entertainment Industry Contrast (high-performance):** "The entertainment industry is infamous for in a limo, out in a taxi. With Oprah, you're in a limo, out in a limo." Consistent respect from first to last contact is her signature.

3. **Long-term relationship context (lewis-howes):** "When ending a business relationship, the last two sentences will ring in their ears permanently." Structure accordingly.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Any time someone is about to have a difficult conversation. Coach them to plan the ending as carefully as the content. Even after firing someone: "I respect what you've done here, and I'll be a reference for you." Even after a breakup: close with genuine regard. The ending IS the memory.

#### Stories

**Arthur Caliandro / Nonprofit Firing**

> Voss was involved in a nonprofit affiliated with a church and was struggling with whether to fire the executive director. He went to the minister, Arthur Caliandro -- Norman Vincent Peale's protege, "one of the best human beings I've ever met." Voss expected counseling toward a gentler path. *Teaching application:* When someone is agonizing over "how" to deliver bad news, the answer is fast and direct. The gentlest thing you can do is not prolong it.

**Nick Nanton Documentary Renegotiation**

> Voss screened his company's documentary "Tactical Empathy" in Vegas and told filmmaker Nick Nanton he loved it. Two days later, he realizes there's a major omission -- his team member Derek isn't in the film. He needs to reopen a closed conversation. Strategy: "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear." Two-line Sunday text. Nick immediately responds he's on a Zoom call, will call in 30 minutes. "He already knows he ain't going to like what he's going to hear. I'm prepping him for bad news." On the call: "Look, I know what I said. We got a problem." Immediately into problem-solving mode. Done in under ten minutes.
>
> Voss contrasts this with what would have happened normally: "Are you out of your mind? We've been working on this for a year. You didn't bring this up in a year. Not only that, you already told me two days ago... that you loved it."

*Teaching application:* "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear" -- two-line text, then straight to the problem. Never do the "how are you? how are the kids?" preamble before dropping bad news.
*Source:* huberman

**Oprah's Private Confrontations**

> Voss has learned from people behind the scenes that "Oprah has taken some highly volatile people to the woodshed over their misbehavior, and nobody knows about it because of the way she handles it." Her finishing line is always: "No matter what you decide, you have to understand that I will always love you and I will always support you."

*Teaching application:* You can deliver ultimatums while ending with unconditional respect. The last impression IS the lasting impression -- even tough confrontations can leave the person feeling valued.
