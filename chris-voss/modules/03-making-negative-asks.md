# Module 3: Making an Ask That Might Be Perceived Negatively

When you need to ask for something -- a raise, discount, upgrade, favor, exception -- and fear being perceived as greedy, presumptuous, demanding, or cheap, the solution is counterintuitive: say out loud every terrible thing they might think about you BEFORE you make the ask.

---

## The Accusations Audit [14/16] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

Voss calls this "probably the single most powerful strategy in the Black Swan method." It is the technique of preemptively naming every negative attribution the other side might hold about you -- spoken definitively, without hedging.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "All you've got to do is look them in the eye and call yourself every name that they are harboring about you." (Thought Economics)

> "The accusations audit is probably the single most powerful strategy in the Black Swan method." (Future Organization / Jacob Morgan)

> "Identifying a negative diminishes it every time. So if I'm going to make a big grab for the money, you're going to think I'm greedy, and I need to get that out of the way." (Lewis Howes)

> "Every single time someone has held back on Accusations Audits they felt were overkill, they got clobbered with exactly what they left out. There's no kill like overkill." (Black Swan Newsletter)

**What it means in practice:** Before making any ask that could trigger a negative reaction, list every negative thought the other side might have about you. Then say them all aloud -- definitively, not tentatively. "I'm going to seem greedy. I'm going to seem ungrateful. I'm going to seem like I don't value your time." This works because:
- Their imagination conjures something worse than reality; your actual ask becomes a relief
- It demonstrates radical self-awareness and honesty, building trust
- Neurologically, naming a negative emotion reduces its power every time (reduces cortisol, triggers oxytocin)
- It creates psychological safety -- if you're already naming their worst thoughts, they don't need to protect themselves

**Examples:**

1. **D.C. Subcontractor (+$1M profit) (Lewis Howes):** A multi-million dollar deal going bad. The subcontractor was furious at the general contractor. The female negotiator sat down and said: "I'm sure we seem like the big guy that doesn't care about you. I'm sure we seem like the big general contractors trying to take complete advantage of the sub and not appreciate how hard you're working for this and not care about your future at all." Result: "She turned that deal around. When she was done, she took an additional million in profit for herself and her company, and the other side liked them more."

2. **Internet Security Server Sales -- 3 instead of 1 (Thought Economics):** A sales professional kept getting refused when pushing a new server. Voss coached him to do an accusations audit -- spend two full days imagining every name the client would call him. "He said it made him feel dirty! And took him to a bad place -- but we told him to carry on and keep going. He needed to meditate just to deal with the reactions it gave him, but guess what -- it worked -- he didn't just sell them one server, he sold them three."

3. **"Why in God's name would you listen to a hostage negotiator?" (Knowledge Project):** Voss's own opening for presentations. He calls out what the audience is thinking: "Seems like listening to a hostage negotiator's a really dumb idea." People respond: "No it's not, maybe you might know something we don't know." Then: "Okay good, I got you to say it so I don't have to." And it sits in a lot more than if you say it yourself.

4. **New York Hotel 3-Step Suite Upgrade (Lewis Howes):** At a New York hotel, Voss walks up to the desk: (1) "Look, man, I'm getting ready to ruin your day" -- waits until they ask what's wrong. (2) "I'm sure I'm going to seem like another self-centered, unappreciative, demanding traveler who thinks the sun rises and sets on them." (3) "How much trouble am I going to get you in if I ask for a free upgrade to a suite?" The boss, standing nearby listening, jumped in: "Because you asked nicely, let me see what I could do." Got 4 nights in a suite.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Before ANY ask the user fears will be perceived negatively. Before requesting a raise, a discount, a favor, an exception. The instruction: make a list of every terrible thing they might think about you. Include everything -- even what feels excessive. Then say it all out loud before making the ask.

## Proactive Emotional Inoculation [10/16]

Preview something worse than reality so the actual ask becomes a relief.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "People are ridiculously resilient to pain. If warned and then that, you lower the boom. You're not going to like what I have to say. It's going to be heartbreaking. You're going to hate me. Hesitate no more than 3 seconds." (Huberman)

> "You will imagine something 10 times worse than what I have to say and what I have to say will actually be a relief." (High Performance Podcast)

> "If I call out a negative that's not there, it doesn't plant it, it actually inoculates you from it." (Huberman)

**What it means in practice:** When you say "You're not going to like what I'm getting ready to say," their survival brain imagines catastrophe. Their amygdala spins up the worst possible scenario. Then your actual request -- which is usually much milder than catastrophe -- arrives as relief. The gap between imagined worst-case and actual request is where goodwill lives. Additionally, naming a negative that doesn't actually exist doesn't plant it -- it vaccinates against it.

**Examples:**

1. **Brandon's Hotel "I'm Getting Ready to Ruin Your Day" (Lewis Howes):** Voss's son Brandon walks up to hotel staff and says: "I'm getting ready to make your day the most difficult day you ever worked here." Hotel staff -- who've seen "ritual sacrifice, goats in bags, every kind of crazy thing" -- immediately envision the worst. Then: "I'm just going to be another self-centered person looking for a free upgrade." Their response, with massive relief: "Oh my God, that's it? Let me give you this room. It's the presidential suite."

2. **Restaurant First-Date Inoculation (High Performance Podcast):** Walked past his preferred table to a far corner (COVID distancing). Told the hostess: "I am going to be the worst customer that has ever walked into this restaurant." Paused three breaths. Then: "We walked right by a table in the other room that I'd really love to sit at. Can we please go sit at this table?" She seated them immediately without even checking with management.

3. **Nick Nanton Documentary Renegotiation (Huberman):** Text message: "Is now a bad time to talk? I got something you don't want to hear." Two lines. Sunday. Nick immediately knows bad news is coming and preps before the conversation even starts. Resolution in under 10 minutes.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Before delivering any request the user thinks will be badly received. Before prices, bad news, big asks. The formula: warn that something bad is coming (3 seconds max), then deliver. Never cushion with irrelevant positives (the "mud sandwich"). Just: preview, pause, deliver.

## Emotional Currency Over Financial Currency [5/16]

Never solve with dollars what you can solve with recognition.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Emotional currency is not going to solve every deal. I just don't want to try to solve any deal with money when I could have solved it with emotional currency. I'm saving my money." (Lewis Howes)

> "Would you work for less than minimum wage to be Oprah's personal assistant? Yes!" (Thought Economics)

**What it means in practice:** Recognition, status, visibility, and feeling important cost nothing to give but satisfy deep psychological needs. Before offering financial concessions of any kind, exhaust emotional ones first. The Trump magazine cover story is the archetype: magazines traded a cover (which they needed to fill anyway -- costless) for dynamic interviews (priceless). Trump was "bought with emotional recognition."

**Examples:**

1. **Trump magazine covers (Lewis Howes):** Magazines put Trump on the cover -- something costless since they fill covers anyway. In return, he gave extensive, dynamic interviews and promoted the magazine. "They're trading something that costs them nothing. And he's giving them dynamic interviews." He was bought with emotional recognition, not money.

2. **"Fair is a criterion within an overall context" (Thought Economics):** "As soon as people start getting bent out of shape about a price tag, they're losing track of where the real value is." Value is contextual. A below-market price with extraordinary access, visibility, or relationship beats a higher price with nothing else.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is preparing to make a financial ask but the other side values recognition or status. When they can offer visibility, acknowledgment, or prestige instead of (or alongside) dollars. When they're solving every problem with money.

## The Two-Millimeter Shift: From Denial to Label [6/16]

The critical pivot that transforms self-sabotage into power.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "If your gut instinct gives you something you wanna deny, I don't want you to think I'm being too self-centered. The two-millimeter shift is going from the denial, which makes it worse, to the fairly calling it out, the label." (Big Interview)

> "You don't get rid of the elephant in the room by ignoring it or denying it's there. You diminish its impact by saying there's an elephant in the room." (High Performance Podcast)

**What it means in practice:** When your instinct says "don't let them think I'm X" -- the natural response is denial: "I don't want you to think I'm greedy." But denial reinforces the very thing you're denying. The brain hears "greedy" and anchors on it. The two-millimeter shift: instead of "I don't want you to think I'm greedy," say "I'm sure this is going to seem greedy." Denial reinforces the fear; acknowledgment deflates it. The shift is tiny in mechanics but massive in effect.

**Examples:**

1. **High Performance Podcast -- the elephant rule:** "You don't get rid of the elephant in the room by ignoring it or denying it's there." In preparation, ask yourself: "What would I normally deny?" Then say THAT thing out loud as a label instead.

2. **Big Interview -- salary negotiation context:** Before asking for more money: not "I don't want you to think I'm being too self-centered" but "I'm sure this is gonna seem very self-centered of me. It's gonna seem as if I'm only focused on my own goals."

3. **Future Organization -- hotel upgrade:** "What's the elephant in a room if I want something for free? That I'm cheap." He doesn't say "I'm not cheap" -- he says "I'm gonna look cheap, ungrateful, lazy." Not "I might" -- "I'm going to."

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user catches themselves wanting to say "I don't want you to think..." -- redirect them. That's the signal. Flip the denial into a definitive label. Use "I'm sure this is going to seem..." or "I'm probably going to look like..." -- never "I don't want you to think..."
