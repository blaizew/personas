### Module 9: Handling Conversations with Family, Partners, or Close Relationships

The paradox Voss returns to repeatedly: the same tools that defuse a bank robber become exponentially harder to use with your spouse, your sister, your child. Closeness makes empathy harder, not easier. Redundant behaviors trigger old wounds. The urge to fix, advise, and dispute is overwhelming. This module addresses the specific obstacles intimacy creates -- and how to overcome them.

---

#### Closeness Makes Empathy Harder [6/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "The closer you are to someone, sometimes it's really harder for you to see things from their perspective."
> *Source: diary-of-a-ceo*

> "My worst negotiations are hot button issues with people who are close to me. Redundant behaviors, past wounds, politics."
> *Source: future-organization*

**What it means in practice:** With strangers, you enter with curiosity because you genuinely don't know their world. With family, you THINK you know -- and that assumption blocks genuine listening. Close relationships activate every obstacle to empathy simultaneously: past grievances, redundant behavior patterns (they ALWAYS do this), political disagreements, and the sheer exhaustion of having had the same conversation before.

Voss himself admits this is where he struggles most. It's not that the tools stop working -- it's that the emotional barriers to deploying them are highest with people you love.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Diary-of-a-ceo:** Voss explicitly acknowledges difficulty unwinding from work mode at home. He describes scheduling a conversation with a family member he keeps "buying the wrong gifts for" -- even the expert needs to deliberately apply the tools.

2. **Future-organization:** Voss says his natural assertive tone is "always bad. Always, always, always bad" -- and it's worst with people close to him where his guard is down and his type defaults to the surface.

3. **Lewis-howes:** The broader principle -- "unexpressed feelings never die" -- compounds in close relationships where minor wounds have accumulated over years without being addressed.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone says "I know them too well" or "they always do this" or "we've been through this a hundred times." These are signals that assumption has replaced curiosity. Coach them to approach the conversation as if meeting this person for the first time -- what would they be curious about?

#### Non-Disputation as Love [4/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "I just wanted to make her feel heard, and to not dispute a thing that she said."
> *Source: ted-talk (client's email, quoted by Voss)*

The result, after an hour of absorbing an attack without counter-attacking:

> "Yesterday I attacked you, and he showed me nothing but love. Thank you for being my big brother."
> *Source: ted-talk (sister's email)*

**What it means in practice:** In close relationships, the most powerful thing you can do when someone attacks you is NOT dispute. Don't correct, don't defend, don't explain, don't counter-attack. Simply make them feel heard. An hour of absorbing an attack -- without a single counter-argument -- transforms the entire dynamic. The attack exhausts itself because it meets no resistance.

This is tactical empathy in its purest, hardest application: absorbing negativity from someone you love without defending yourself.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Tech Executive and His Drunk Sister (ted-talk):** A client who "uses the transformative skill of tactical empathy to cut million-dollar deals in the tech sector" applied it to his youngest sister -- the primary caregiver of their dying father, who'd had too much to drink at a family gathering. She attacked him for an hour. He disputed nothing. The next day: "Yesterday I attacked you, and he showed me nothing but love."

2. **High-performance:** Voss describes sounding-board listening -- "Stop giving advice and just let people be a sounding board" -- and notes that the biggest obstacle is the urge to fix or defend. In family contexts, this urge is strongest.

3. **Knowledge-project:** "I want people to feel like helping me or even offering to help me in ways I didn't even know they could." The principle applies in reverse with family: when you make someone feel heard without disputing, they voluntarily offer what you didn't ask for.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is bracing for a family confrontation and planning counter-arguments. The coaching is: don't dispute a single thing. Don't correct factual errors. Don't explain your side. Just listen, label, and let them talk until they're done. The transformation happens in the absence of resistance, not in the brilliance of your response.

#### Be a Sounding Board, Not an Advisor [5/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Stop giving advice and just let people-- just be a sounding board... All of us, the thoughts in our head, they make sense when they're in our head. And as soon as we start talking them out loud, just that mere act of saying stuff out loud, no matter how troubled you are, some of it is going to clear up."
> *Source: high-performance*

> "If you're a good sounding board, they can level themselves out in 20 minutes or less."
> *Source: high-performance*

**What it means in practice:** When your partner, child, or friend comes to you with a problem, resist the urge to solve it. Talking out loud IS the solution. Confusion clears up through vocalization -- not through your advice. If you're a good sounding board (meaning: you don't interrupt, don't advise, don't judge, just reflect and label), they'll self-correct in under 20 minutes. If it takes longer than 20 minutes, you're probably giving advice and should stop.

This connects to the guided discovery principle: "If they discover it, it's their idea and they're going to do it. If you offer it, you're giving them advice, and they got no emotional ownership."

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Crisis Hotline Call (high-performance):** A man calls the crisis intervention hotline. Voss says: "Sounds like you're going through a tough time." Twenty minutes later the man says: "Wow, I had no idea all that was inside me. I'm good now." All Voss did was serve as a sounding board -- no advice, no judgment, no solutions.

2. **Diary-of-a-ceo (guided discovery):** "If they discover something mutually beneficial versus if you offer it, if they discover it, it's their idea and they're going to do it. If you offer it, you're giving them advice, and they got no emotional ownership." Telling requires 19 repetitions; guided discovery requires once.

3. **Lewis-howes:** The plane seatmate who revealed more in one flight "than he's told anybody in 20 years" -- all through labels (it seems like, it sounds like, it looks like). No questions, no advice, just reflected understanding.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone says "my partner keeps coming to me with problems and I don't know what to tell them." The answer: stop telling them anything. Labels + silence + more labels. "Sounds like that's really weighing on you." Then wait. They'll talk themselves to clarity. Your job is to hold space, not solve.

#### "How" Questions for Children / Present-Stuck People [3/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "When we're late to school, how are you going to feel?... How did it feel last time when you walked in late? How embarrassing was that?"
> *Source: high-performance*

**What it means in practice:** Children (and many adults) are stuck in the present moment -- unable to project forward to consequences. "How" questions force mental time-travel from present impulse to future emotional reality. They don't moralize ("you SHOULD get ready faster") -- they make the person feel the future consequence NOW.

This is calibrated questions applied to parenting. Not "why aren't you ready?" (triggers defensiveness) but "how are you going to feel when we're late?" (forces them to live the consequence in advance).

**Examples across sources:**

1. **High-performance (parenting context):** The explicit application to getting a child ready for school. "How are you going to feel?" makes the child visualize the embarrassment of walking in late -- which is more motivating than any threat or lecture.

2. **Lewis-howes (Beverly Hills job description):** A coaching client's employer gave them a flawed job description. Instead of arguing why it won't work, Voss coaches: say "Do you want me to fail?" Their answer must be no -- and now they have to reconcile the design with that answer. Same principle: make them live the consequence.

3. **Jordan-165 (general principle):** "If I slow down, if I use questions to affect your thinking, if I shape your thinking so that you discover it -- you discover it faster and it's your idea." Applied to family: don't lecture, guide discovery through questions that make consequences vivid.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is struggling with a child, partner, or family member who "won't listen." The coaching: stop telling them what to do. Start asking how they'll feel when the consequence arrives. "How" questions are principally focused on implementation and force mental time-travel to emotional outcomes.

#### Unexpressed Feelings Never Die [5/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "There's an old saying, 'Unexpressed feelings never die.' And whether it's business or hostage negotiation, people harbor feelings... they will wait forever to pay somebody back and they will never let it go."
> *Source: jordan-678*

**What it means in practice:** In close relationships, minor wounds accumulate silently. The feeling doesn't dissipate because it wasn't voiced -- it calcifies. People will wait years -- decades -- to settle an emotional score they never voiced. Every unexpressed negative is a time bomb in the relationship.

The antidote: surface negatives proactively. Use labels to bring unspoken feelings into the open before they compound. In family relationships, this is harder because "we've always just let it go" -- but letting it go is a myth. It doesn't go anywhere.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Jordan-678:** Voss states this as a universal truth applicable equally to hostage situations and business partnerships. The mechanism is the same: unaddressed grievances don't fade -- they intensify.

2. **Landmark Forum / Bullying Revelation (lewis-howes):** A woman carried resentment about her cousin's bullying for 30 years. When she confronted the cousin, "The girl didn't even remember." Voss's takeaway: "How many people have I hurt that I didn't even know?... Every human being is gonna hurt you. Principally inadvertently."

3. **Black-swan-newsletter:** Negative emotions are "vampire zombies" -- "Stab them, cut their heads off, and set them on fire. Don't let them come back to haunt you." The only way to kill them is to name them out loud.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone describes tension with a family member that "came out of nowhere." It didn't come from nowhere -- it came from years of unexpressed feelings. Coach them to label what they suspect is underneath: "It seems like something from a long time ago is still bothering you." The label opens the door.

#### Stories

**Tech Executive and His Drunk Sister**

> "A friend, a colleague, a client, uses the transformative skill of tactical empathy to cut million-dollar deals in the tech sector. He's enormously successful. He's at a family gathering. His youngest sister's had too much to drink." She's the primary caregiver of their dying father, and "the stress on her is enormous." She starts in on him -- "and he realizes now it's his turn." He applies tactical empathy: "I just wanted to make her feel heard, and to not dispute a thing that she said." It went on for an hour.
>
> "The next day she sends him an email that says: 'Yesterday I attacked you, and he showed me nothing but love. Thank you for being my big brother.'"

*Teaching application:* Tactical empathy applied to family is harder and more important than in business. The technique: don't dispute, don't defend, absorb without resistance. An hour of non-disputation transformed an attack into gratitude. The person who can negotiate million-dollar tech deals found this harder.
*Source:* ted-talk

---

**Ex-Mrs. Voss "You Sound Angry"**

> Early in his training, Voss learned on the hotline that saying "you sound angry" to callers made anger dissipate "like magic." He tried it on his then-wife during a fundamental disagreement: "I look at her and I say, you sound angry... She blew up. She was like, I'm angry." The problem: "It wasn't just that she was angry. She was angry at me, and she held me responsible for it. And what I said stopped short of that." The correct approach: "It seems like I've been unreasonable. It seems like I've been unfair. It seems like I have never given you a chance. It seems like I haven't respected your autonomy."

*Teaching application:* Labels must account for YOUR role in causing the emotion. "You sound angry" is observation that can feel dismissive. "It seems like I've been unreasonable" is an accusations audit -- it includes your responsibility and gives them something to say "that's right" to. In close relationships, generic labels fail because the person's anger is usually about YOU specifically.
*Source:* talks-at-google

---

**Son's Football "That's Right"**

> Voss's son was moved from lineman (hit everything) to linebacker (dodge everything, only hit the ball carrier). His son wouldn't adapt. Every time the coach and Voss explained it, his son would say "you're right" -- and then do it wrong the next day. Finally Voss took him aside: "You think that dodging a block is unmanly. You feel like getting out of the way of somebody who's trying to hit you makes you a coward." Son got quiet, then said "that's right." Started ducking blocks the next day.
>
> Later told Voss: "I never would have been able to explain that for myself. Until you said it, I didn't know it was true. And never in a million years would I have thought doing it was acting like a coward. But it was absolutely what was driving me."

*Teaching application:* Articulating what someone CAN'T say about themselves creates instant behavioral change. The son couldn't access his own emotional block -- it took someone else naming it. In family relationships, this means going deeper than surface disagreement to the identity-level belief driving behavior.
*Source:* talks-at-google

**Landmark Forum / Bullying Revelation**

> Voss reveals he was bullied as a child -- "I had literally never told anybody about this." At Landmark Forum, he heard a woman share that her cousin bullied her at age 7 and she'd carried it 30 years. When the woman went to confront her cousin, "The girl didn't even remember." This triggered Voss's reflection: "How many people have I hurt that I didn't even know?... I have to have done that to somebody." He frames it as: "Every human being is gonna hurt you. Principally inadvertently."

*Teaching application:* Most emotional wounds are accidental and unknown to the person who inflicted them. This reframes family conflict: the person who hurt you probably doesn't know they did it. Labels can surface old wounds driving current behavior. Also -- the willingness to ask "who have I hurt without knowing?" is a prerequisite for genuine family repair.
*Source:* lewis-howes
