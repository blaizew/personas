### Module 20: Negotiating When You Have No Leverage

When you feel powerless -- the other side holds all the cards, you're the smaller party, you have nothing obvious to offer -- the natural response is despair or submission. Voss's position is unequivocal: leverage is perception, not reality, and the very engagement itself proves you have something they want. His entire career was built on negotiating from zero leverage -- you cannot offer a kidnapper anything they actually want except money you refuse to give.

---

#### "If Somebody's Talking to You, You've Got Something They Want" [6/16]

The foundational principle of this module: engagement itself is proof of leverage. If the other side is at the table, on the phone, or responding to your messages, you have something they need. The apparent powerlessness is an illusion manufactured by fear.

**Quote:** "If somebody's talking to you, you've got something they want, period. I always said there's always leverage." (talks-at-google)

**Quote:** "There's always leverage." (talks-at-google)

**Practical meaning:** Kidnapping is the ultimate test case. The hostage family appears to have zero leverage -- one buyer, the other side has the "product." But Voss frames kidnapping as a buyer's market: the kidnappers have a perishable asset with exactly one buyer. They cannot shop it around. They need you more than you think. The same principle applies to job interviews (they're spending time evaluating you because they need to fill the role), to procurement hardball (they wouldn't waste time intimidating a supplier they don't want), and to any situation where engagement is happening despite apparent power imbalance.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user says "they hold all the cards," when they feel they have nothing to offer, when they're considering capitulating because they perceive no alternatives. Challenge the assumption: "If you truly had nothing they wanted, why are they still talking to you?"

---

#### Paint a Vision of a Future Where They Live [6/16]

When you genuinely cannot concede anything -- no money, no car, no helicopter -- the only negotiation tool left is helping the counterpart see a future they want to inhabit. Make them visualize survival and prosperity; let their own survival instinct do the persuasion.

**Quote:** "How do you negotiate when you're not going to give them anything? Help them see a different vision of the future." (diary-of-a-ceo)

**Quote:** "What you really want them to see is a vision of future where they live, and then you're hoping the survival instinct kicks in." (diary-of-a-ceo)

**Quote:** "I call into a bank, bad guy picks up on the phone, and I'm gonna say, I want you to live. You know, I wanna see you survive this." (lex-fridman)

**Practical meaning:** In hostage barricade situations, Voss could not give the bad guy a car, release them from criminal liability, or make police disappear. His only tool was painting a picture of what happens when they come out peacefully -- treated with dignity, alive, talking to their family. In the Harlem high-rise standoff, six hours of repeatedly describing what surrender would look like (with zero verbal response from the fugitives) produced peaceful surrender. The mechanism: humans cannot resist a vivid picture of their own survival. In business, this means helping the other side see what their life looks like partnering with you, not just what the deal terms are.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user has nothing tangible to offer, when a negotiation seems deadlocked because there's no concession available, when someone needs to persuade from a position of genuine resource scarcity. The tool is temporal: shift the conversation from present demands to future possibilities.

#### The Illusion of Control [8/16] [SIGNATURE]

When you have no leverage, you cannot force outcomes. But you can engineer situations where the counterpart uses their own resources to solve your problems -- and feels empowered doing it. Calibrated "How" and "What" questions create this illusion.

**Quote:** "The secret to gaining the upper hand in a negotiation is giving the other side the illusion of control." (lewis-howes)

**Quote:** "You've engineered a situation in which your formerly recalcitrant counterpart is now using his mental and emotional resources to overcome your challenges." (book)

**Practical meaning:** "How am I supposed to do that?" is the signature deployment. The counterpart thinks they are in charge because they are doing the problem-solving. In reality, they are solving YOUR problem while feeling powerful and smart. This works precisely when you have no leverage -- because you are not trying to force anything. You are asking them to figure out how to give you what you need within their constraints. The less power you have, the more effective this technique becomes, because deference (which is natural when you have no power) is the ideal delivery mode.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user faces a demand they cannot meet, when they have no ability to force compliance, when they need the other side to generate solutions. The technique is most potent from a position of genuine weakness -- deference is authentic and therefore more persuasive.

#### "Never Be Mean to Someone Who Can Hurt You by Doing Nothing" [8/16]

Everyone has discretionary effort. The person at the bottom of the organizational chart can put your request on the top of the pile or the bottom. The concept of "no leverage" ignores the universal truth that every person you interact with controls something you want: speed, quality, priority, effort, enthusiasm.

**Quote:** "Never be mean to someone who can hurt you by doing nothing. And everybody understands the universal truth of that. Nearly everybody that you interact with on any level, doesn't matter what the interaction is, they could hurt you by doing nothing." (jordan-678)

**Quote:** "There's never a single conversation where you can't gain an edge and save yourself a lot of time, just with the application of a couple of these ideas." (jordan-678)

**Practical meaning:** Voss demonstrates this with the Sony music licensing example: a person who "just" takes orders decides whether your request goes to the top or bottom of the pile. Two weeks of delay versus 24-hour service is controlled entirely by how you made them feel. In the airline upgrade story, the agent gave an upgrade before seats were officially available -- pure discretionary effort triggered by emotional connection. This reframes "no leverage" entirely: you always have leverage because you can always influence how much effort someone puts into helping you.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is dealing with gatekeepers, clerks, administrative staff, or anyone who controls process but not decisions. When someone dismisses "low-level" contacts as unimportant. When the user is frustrated by bureaucratic delay and wants to push through.

#### "Seems Like You're Powerless Here" (Triggering Action) [4/16]

When someone claims they cannot help -- "that's our policy," "there's nothing I can do," "my hands are tied" -- labeling their powerlessness creates an identity challenge. Nobody wants to be powerless. The label triggers an almost involuntary demonstration of capability.

**Quote:** "If you can't budge at all, I'll say, all right, well look, seems like you're powerless here... Nobody wants to say yes to that." (lewis-howes)

**Practical meaning:** Voss uses this annually on credit card annual fees and in any gatekeeper scenario. The technique works because people have a fundamental need to feel capable and in control. When you label their inability with genuine sympathy (not sarcasm), they will search their system for any exception, any workaround, any creative solution to prove they are NOT powerless. The psychological mechanism is identity protection -- they will find a way to help specifically to refute the label.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user has hit a wall with customer service, when someone says "that's policy," when a negotiation partner claims they have no flexibility. Deploy as the absolute last move in a stalled conversation with a gatekeeper.

#### The Procurement Leverage Inversion [4/16]

When facing an aggressive, intimidating counterparty -- procurement departments, aggressive recruiters, hardball dealmakers -- their aggression itself is the signal that they want you. If they didn't need you, they would not waste time and energy trying to intimidate you.

**Quote:** "Procurement does not waste their time on suppliers they have no interest in, ever. So if procurement gets in a game with you, buckle up, let them call you names, don't get upset, calm down. They're only talking to you because they really want you." (jordan-165)

**Practical meaning:** This inverts the apparent power dynamic completely. The aggressive buyer's behavior -- their time investment, their emotional energy, their attention -- proves your leverage. They wouldn't bother being tough on someone they planned to dismiss. Voss also notes that procurement's quarter-end pressure is self-defeating: "The procurement guy who calls with 48 hours left in the quarter feels that's the only time the entire quarter he had leverage. 48 hours, his leverage is gone." Wait 72 hours and their position collapses.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user feels intimidated by an aggressive counterpart, when a procurement department or powerful buyer is applying pressure, when someone is about to fold under pressure that is itself evidence of their value. Reframe: "Would they invest this effort if they had better options?"

#### Deference as Power (From Below) [10/16]

Deference is most powerful when you clearly don't have to give it -- but when you genuinely lack power, deference is natural and therefore maximally authentic. The paradox: from a position of weakness, polite deference enables extraordinary assertiveness because it never registers as an attack.

**Quote:** "There's great power in deference and from a deferential approach, it's ridiculous how assertive you can get away with. The other side is never going to see it coming." (jordan-678)

**Practical meaning:** When you have no leverage, you cannot afford to be aggressive -- but you also cannot afford to be passive. Deference threads the needle: it looks like submission (which the powerful expect from the powerless) while enabling firm boundary-setting and assertive questioning. The billionaire in Voss's story uses deference on Trump not because he must, but because he chooses to -- and gains more influence than anyone who challenges Trump directly. From a position of genuine weakness, deference is even more effective because it is completely authentic.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is the smaller party and fears being steamrolled, when they think deference means capitulation, when they ask how to be assertive without appearing threatening to someone with more power.

#### Stories

**Kidnapping as Buyer's Market**
> Voss reframes the apparent powerlessness of hostage families: "Kidnapping in reality is a buyer's market." The hostage family has the cash; the kidnapper has a "product" (the hostage) with exactly one buyer. They cannot shop it around. They cannot sell to a competitor. The apparent powerlessness of the family is an illusion created by fear -- the family is actually the scarce resource (willing buyer) in the transaction.

*Teaching application:* When someone believes they have zero leverage, challenge the assumption by asking: who needs whom more? The person who appears powerful (holding the hostage, holding the job offer, holding the contract) often has a perishable asset with limited alternative buyers. Fear obscures this structural reality.
*Source:* jordan-165

**The Airline Upgrade (Coming After the Argument)**
> A student of Voss's was in line at an airline counter. The person ahead was angry and berating the gate agent. The student thought he was "screwed" because she'd be upset. Instead, when he reached the counter, he labeled: "Wow, it seems like they were pretty upset." She opened up. He continued with empathy, mirroring, and labeling. Result: "He ends up getting booked onto a flight and getting an upgrade before the seats were officially open." The previous angry customer -- who had objectively more "leverage" (louder, more demanding) -- got nothing.

*Teaching application:* Contrast amplifies the effect of empathy. Coming in after someone else's negativity is an ideal setup -- the gap between the demanding customer and the kind one magnifies your impact. Discretionary effort flows to pleasant people, not powerful people. The upgrade was given before seats were technically available -- proving that "no power" with kindness outperforms "aggressive power" without it.
*Source:* jordan-678

**Pepe Escobar's Escape (Don Pepe)**
> Jose "Pepe" Escobar was kidnapped in Ecuador. The FBI pushed for "how" questions against resistance from local police. "How do I know my husband's even alive? How am I supposed to pay if I don't know he's alive?" The forced interaction meant the kidnappers had to engage with Pepe regularly. His natural relationship-building ability was so strong that captors started calling him "Don Pepe" -- a sign of respect. 28 days in, at 2 AM in a rainstorm, Pepe escaped out a window. Voss's assessment: "Great negotiation gives the opportunity for good things to happen. And you don't get so focused on your outcome that you wouldn't take something better."

*Teaching application:* Right process from a position of zero leverage creates conditions for outcomes you never planned. The family had no power -- they couldn't pay, couldn't rescue, couldn't force anything. But the "how" questions forced internal collaboration within the captor group that humanized the hostage and relaxed security. The escape was never the plan; it was an emergent outcome of the right process applied without leverage.
*Source:* spycast, lewis-howes, jordan-678

**The Harlem High-Rise (6 Hours of Vision-Painting)**
> Voss negotiated at a 27th-floor apartment in Harlem with three fugitives. For 6 hours, with zero verbal response from inside, he repeatedly described what it would look like when they came out safely -- painted a vision of peaceful surrender. Then, without ever having said a word, the door opens and hands emerge exactly as described. When asked why she finally came out: "Well, you said you'd never go away, so we finally believed you and decided to come out."

*Teaching application:* Demonstrates that when you have no leverage (cannot force entry, cannot offer anything, cannot even confirm someone is listening), persistence in vision-painting works through sheer repetition. The only tool was describing a future they could inhabit. Silence from the other side does not mean they are not listening.
*Source:* high-performance
