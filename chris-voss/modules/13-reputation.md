### Building and Protecting Long-Term Reputation

Every negotiation is simultaneously a deal AND a reputation-building event. Voss treats reputation as the single most valuable long-term asset a negotiator possesses -- more important than any individual deal outcome.

---

#### Never Lie -- Integrity Is Your Currency [12/16] [EMPHATIC]

Voss's case against lying is tactical, not moral. He presents a three-layer argument that compounds in severity:

**Quote:** "Don't lie to anybody you're not going to kill... But even if you kill them, people they know are going to find out about it and you're going to pay for it anyway. Deception is a bad idea." (jordan-678)

**Quote:** "There's a really good chance the other side is a better liar than you are, they're gonna spot it right off the bat. Secondly, they could be luring you into a trap to see if you will lie. Thirdly, the chances are they're gonna find out that you lied to them eventually is really high." (lex-fridman)

**Quote:** "I would equate lying to dropping a nuclear bomb. No matter how righteous the reason for that is you're going to have to realize that there's going to be radioactive fallout that you have to deal with for a very long time to come." (jordan-678)

**The three layers:**
1. Detection risk -- they're probably a better liar and will spot yours
2. Trap risk -- the question may be a test of your integrity
3. Discovery cost -- they will find out eventually, and the reputational damage is permanent

**Practical meaning:** This is an absolute principle with no exceptions. Voss was asked at Harvard: "If a terrorist has a nuclear bomb and you could defuse it by lying, would you?" His answer: "No, because number one, he's probably a better liar than I am anyway. And lying to him is a trap that he's trying to lure me into." Even under the most extreme hypothetical, the tactical case against lying holds.

**Quote:** "My currency is integrity. And at some point in time, if you go like, you know where I'm coming from? My answer's gonna be like, look, I can agree on maybe where we're going, but if we're talking about, you know, am I on your side now?... I'm not interested in you taking it out of my hide." (lex-fridman)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is considering strategic omission or selective truth-telling
- User asks about "white lies" in business
- User wonders if transparency will cost them a deal
- User has been caught in a lie and needs to understand the fallout

#### Long-Term Greedy / "People Who Do Business with Me Prosper" [8/16]

**Quote:** "I want my reputation to be the people that do business with me prosper. If I have that reputation, then people will start lining up to do business with me." (big-interview)

**Quote:** "There's an old saying attributed to Goldman Sachs. Greedy, yes, but long-term greedy... your most valuable commodity is time. Everybody in here has one commodity that's exactly equal to Warren Buffett's... He's got 24 hours. We've got 24 hours." (talks-at-google)

**Practical meaning:** Design every deal so the other side wants to tell others about working with you. Short-term extraction destroys the flywheel. Time -- not money -- is the scarce resource, and deals that create enemies waste your future time on damage control rather than new opportunities.

**Quote:** "As long as you're not greedy short term, you're probably gonna do pretty well." (big-interview)

Voss actively engineers this in his own business. When negotiating with a Silicon Valley training partner: "Every time we start to sit down and begin to talk about compensation, she literally says to me the other day, 'I can sense that you're trying to make sure that we feel like we're getting paid enough.'" She noticed his intent before he stated it -- which IS the reputation forming in real time.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is tempted to extract maximum value from a one-time deal
- User is choosing between short-term profit and long-term relationship
- User is deciding how to structure partnership compensation

#### The Most Important Person Is Watching [5/16]

**Quote:** "The most important person watching me is not the person in the interaction, but somebody is watching how I conduct myself. And they're going to... the most powerful people sit back and study you for a long time before they trust you to put you in really phenomenal deals." (talks-at-google)

**Practical meaning:** Every interaction is observed by people deciding whether to trust you with bigger opportunities. The silent evaluators -- potential investors, future employers, referral sources -- are tracking your behavior long before they engage directly. Conduct yourself for the watchers, not just the counterpart.

This operates as a passive filter: the best opportunities come from people who watched you operate with integrity in smaller contexts. You never see the moment they decided to trust you -- but it was months or years of observation.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is tempted to behave differently because "nobody's watching"
- User feels an interaction is too small to matter
- User is in a reputation-building phase of their career

#### There's No Such Thing as a One-Off [7/16]

**Quote:** "If you hammer somebody in a negotiation, they're going to wait for the rest of their life to pay you back." (talks-at-google)

**Quote:** "There's no such thing as a one-off. They're going to be people that stay in your world one way or another." (talks-at-google)

**Quote:** "Hostage negotiators have repeat customers." (ted-talk, diary-of-a-ceo)

**Practical meaning:** Even in situations that appear one-time (buying a car, settling a claim, negotiating a contract with someone you'll never see again), the other party remembers. They talk. They post. They warn others. They wait for revenge.

**Quote:** "Unexpressed feelings never die. And whether it's business or hostage negotiation, people harbor feelings... they will wait forever to pay somebody back and they will never let it go." (jordan-678)

The principle extends to criminals: hostage takers return to barricade situations. A previous negotiator who lied resulted in lives being lost in subsequent encounters because the subject remembered. If this applies with terrorists, it applies everywhere.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User assumes a negotiation is one-off ("I'll never see them again")
- User is rationalizing aggressive tactics on a "small" deal
- User has been burned by someone who is retaliating for a past interaction

#### Aggravation Tax [3/16]

**Quote:** "In every transaction there's an aggravation tax... If you're not aggravating, you don't need to pay the aggravation tax. Let somebody else pay that aggravation tax, and you benefit from them actually paying that tax." (lewis-howes)

**Practical meaning:** Every price includes a hidden emotional premium extracted from dealing with difficult people. If you are pleasant, you are automatically negotiating from a lower baseline than the annoying customer who came before you. The gap between what a service provider charges a difficult customer versus a pleasant one can be 20-30%.

This connects directly to reputation: over time, your pleasantness compounds into a track record that produces preferential treatment, faster service, voluntary help, and goodwill that translates into real economic value.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is being difficult with vendors or service providers
- User doesn't see the connection between manner and outcomes
- User is frustrated with prices and wants to understand hidden dynamics

#### Stories

**Harvard Hard-Bargaining Reputation Destruction**
> When Voss joined Harvard's negotiation course, he used invisible hard bargaining techniques from kidnapping. "I'd learned some very hard bargaining techniques that I used to just really pound people with. And they didn't know that I did it to them. I did it in a very invisible way. The problem was word got out. So the first 2 or 3 times, I really slaughtered the other side. And then they started talking about it. And pretty soon, no one would talk to me. I'd sit down for a negotiation. They'd stare at me because they were afraid to say anything." An energy CEO had the same problem: a signed agreement sat on his desk because the counterpart CEO "is afraid to sign it because if you make a deal with me now, by definition, you've been beaten."
*Teaching application:* Even invisible techniques get exposed. Winning by domination has a half-life -- the monuments to aggressive success remain, but nobody will negotiate with you anymore. Deploy when someone is "winning" deals but noticing fewer opportunities over time.
*Source:* talks-at-google, high-performance

**The Deferential Billionaire and Trump**
> A billionaire in New York -- older, wealthier, more socially established than Trump -- uses deference when speaking to Trump. "He calls Trump 'president' on a regular basis and he's deferential to him." This man doesn't need anything from Trump -- no job, no deals, no money, no fame. "His life is on a higher-functioning level across the board." Yet he uses deference "because people love when somebody doesn't have to be deferential but is." The result: he has more influence over Trump than almost anyone else.
*Teaching application:* Deference is most powerful when you clearly don't need to give it. It creates maximum influence precisely because it's voluntary, not obligatory. This IS the reputation strategy in action -- consistent respect from a position of strength.
*Source:* knowledge-project

**Tom Girardi -- The Immovable Nice Guy**
> Tom Girardi, voted top trial attorney in California "so many times that when they put it on the front page, they say 'Again.'" Voss expected an "attack dog." Instead: "Tom walks into the class and he says, the key to negotiation is being nice and gentle." But Girardi is "an immovable, unrelenting opponent, and that's why he's so successful, and he's ridiculously nice about it." His signature: when you argue with him, he starts talking about "how you and I are going to be successful together 10 years from now." He never puts anything in emails and is relentlessly patient -- he knows if he stays nice long enough, the other side will accidentally reveal incriminating information. "He gets people saying stuff accidentally, and then he doesn't jump on you if you say something accidentally. He lets you say so many things accidentally."
*Teaching application:* Relentless niceness is not weakness -- it's the most devastating long-game strategy. Patience + warmth = inevitable information advantage and total dominance without creating enemies.
*Source:* lewis-howes

**Oprah's "In a Limo, Out in a Limo"**
> "Last impression is a lasting impression. The entertainment industry is infamous for in a limo, out in a taxi. With Oprah, you're in a limo, out in a limo." Oprah maintains consistent respect from first contact through final interaction. She's also taken "highly volatile people to the woodshed over their misbehavior, and nobody knows about it because of the way she handles it." Her finishing line after confrontations: "No matter what you decide, you have to understand that I will always love you and I will always support you."
*Teaching application:* Consistent respect at every stage -- including the ending -- is the ultimate reputation strategy. You can be demanding and direct while maintaining the warmth that preserves the relationship for the next interaction.
*Source:* high-performance
