### Module 19: Managing Your Own Emotions During Negotiation

When you feel anxious, angry, or overwhelmed before or during a negotiation, your emotional state becomes the primary obstacle to performance. Voss teaches that emotional self-regulation is not about suppression but about sequencing, redirection, and deliberate state-shifting -- using the same neurochemical tools on yourself that you use on counterparts.

---

#### Playfulness as High-Performance State [8/16] [EMPHATIC]

Voss treats positive mood not as a personality trait but as a cognitive performance enhancer with measurable effects. He cites research showing a 31% cognitive boost in a positive frame of mind, making playfulness an operational tool rather than a soft preference.

**Quote:** "I like to be playful in a negotiation because it makes me smarter. And when I'm playful and I'm smarter, then that automatically sort of infused in the other person's brain. They get smarter, they're in a better mood which means they're going to be better at thinking of ideas that benefit me." (jordan-678)

**Quote:** "Great negotiation is not exciting. It's astonishing." (huberman)

**Quote:** "Our brains work up to 31 percent more effectively when we're in a positive frame of mind. That's no small advance." (jordan-678)

**Practical meaning:** Playfulness is not the absence of seriousness -- it is the presence of cognitive superiority. A happy negotiator implements deals better, produces more creative solutions, and makes both sides smarter. The contrast with excitement is deliberate: effective negotiation looks calm and even boring from the outside, but produces outcomes that shock people.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user reports feeling tense before a negotiation, when they describe an adversarial dynamic developing, when they ask how to "get in the zone" -- direct them toward playfulness as the performance state, not intensity or aggression.

---

#### Genuine Curiosity as Emotional Control Hack [6/16]

Curiosity is Voss's fastest self-regulation technique. When negative emotion rises, redirecting attention to genuine curiosity about the other person's behavior creates what he calls "instant compartmentalization" -- negativity simply cannot coexist with authentic fascination.

**Quote:** "Genuine curiosity is a hack for emotional control. If I'm genuinely curious as to why you're crazy, that's one instant hack." (knowledge-project)

**Practical meaning:** This is not "pretend to be curious." It requires genuine wonder about what is driving the other person's behavior. When someone seems irrational, the curiosity question ("Why are they doing this? What am I missing?") both provides emotional regulation AND produces the investigative mindset that discovers Black Swans. The technique serves dual purposes: controlling your state while gathering intelligence.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is angry at a counterpart and can't think straight, when someone's behavior seems "crazy" or irrational, when a user feels triggered by an unreasonable demand. Redirect: "What would you need to believe for their behavior to make sense?"

---

#### The Rock-Paper-Scissors Emotion Sequence [3/16]

Voss teaches that emotional states cannot be jumped -- they must be sequenced through in order. You cannot go from fury to playfulness directly. You must pass through calm first. This provides a concrete operational framework for self-regulation.

**Quote:** "I think the emotions are kind of a rock-paper-scissors sequence. I don't think you can go from sadness to elation, directly... I think there's something to getting angry to pull you out of sadness. And I think if you're angry, you've got to go to calm next." (huberman)

**Practical meaning:** The sequence is: Sadness -> Anger -> Calm -> Positive/Playful. If you are angry, the next achievable state is calm (via the FM DJ voice on yourself). Only from calm can you shift to positive/playful. If you are sad, letting yourself get angry can be functional because anger has more energy. This is not about managing emotion away -- it's about navigating the required transitions.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user reports being emotional and needs to perform, when someone is trying to "just calm down" from fury and failing, when a user needs to move from a negative to a positive state for a critical conversation.

---

#### Fear Never Goes Away -- Choose What You Fear [4/16]

Voss's relationship with fear is not about eliminating it but about calibrating it. Fear is permanent human wiring. The question is not "how do I stop being afraid" but "what am I going to be afraid of?"

**Quote:** "Nobody ever gets away from it. You're wired into it. It really just matters what you're scared of. It's not that you're not scared, it's what you're scared of. I'm scared of not doing a good job. I'm scared I miss something. It took me a long time to realize I'm actually scared of being afraid." (jordan-165)

**Practical meaning:** Voss redirected his fear system to fear cowardice and incompetence rather than failure or rejection. His worst insult is "coward" -- that's by design. By choosing to fear inaction more than action, his fear pushes him forward rather than holding him back. This is not motivational advice -- it's the deliberate engineering of your fear response to serve performance.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is avoiding a difficult conversation because they're "not ready," when someone is procrastinating on high-stakes decisions due to anxiety, when the user asks how to become fearless (answer: you don't -- you recalibrate what you fear).

---

#### Prescribing the Symptom Reduces It [3/16]

A paradoxical intervention technique Voss developed from advising kidnapping families: telling people to embrace their fear makes it dissipate. The mechanism is that attempting to suppress an emotion amplifies it, while accepting it removes its power.

**Quote:** "I'm going to tell you in advance, I want you to be scared. It's going to help us in a negotiation." (jordan-165)

**Quote:** "If you have a negative emotion... instead of me trying to say eliminate fear, I say like it, it'll make it go away." (jordan-165)

**Quote:** "As soon as you stop trying to suppress it, it's one of the keys to make it go away." (jordan-165)

**Practical meaning:** This served dual tactical purposes in kidnapping: (1) The family's genuine-sounding fear relaxed kidnappers who believed they had the upper hand. (2) Paradoxically, giving themselves permission to be scared reduced the family's internal experience of fear. The psychological mechanism: suppression amplifies; acceptance diminishes. This applies directly to pre-negotiation anxiety -- naming it and welcoming it removes its grip.

**Coaching triggers:** When a user is trying desperately to suppress anxiety before a high-stakes moment, when someone is performing worse because they're fighting their own nervousness, when a user needs to be told "it's okay to be scared -- use it."

---

#### The Self-Calming Voice (Mirror Neurons on Yourself) [3/16]

The Late-Night FM DJ Voice is typically taught as an other-regulation tool. Voss also teaches it as a self-regulation tool -- using your own voice to trigger your own mirror neurons and calm yourself down.

**Quote:** "If you talk to yourself sometimes you're actually smarter if you talk out loud in a soothing calming voice -- that voice comes out of your mouth it goes through the air comes back through your ear and hits your mirror neurons and calms you down." (knowledge-project)

**Practical meaning:** Speaking to yourself in the FM DJ tone creates a neurochemical feedback loop: voice out -> air -> ear -> mirror neurons -> calming response. This is not visualization or affirmation -- it's using the mechanical properties of sound on your own nervous system. The same involuntary calming response you trigger in others, you can trigger in yourself.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user needs to calm down immediately before a conversation, when they report anxiety they can't manage through mental techniques alone, when they need a concrete physical action to regulate their state.

---

#### The Assertive Voice Is Counterproductive (Self-Awareness) [8/16]

Self-regulation includes awareness of your own natural vocal tendencies and their impact. Voss -- a self-identified assertive type -- teaches that the assertive voice feels powerful to the speaker but literally makes the counterpart dumber.

**Quote:** "The assertive voice is almost always counterproductive. It feels like getting hit in the face with a brick." (lex-fridman)

**Quote:** "Me making you dumber will always hurt me. Me making you feel attacked will always hurt me." (lex-fridman)

**Practical meaning:** Apple Watch data from Voss's training exercises showed a participant's heart rate jumping to 170 BPM -- fight-or-flight -- from merely hearing an assertive tone while sitting still. The assertive voice is self-defeating because a counterpart in fight-or-flight cannot generate creative solutions, cannot think clearly, and cannot collaborate. Self-regulation means catching your own assertive tendencies before they sabotage your outcomes.

**Coaching triggers:** When the user is naturally assertive and reports that conversations keep going sideways, when someone is told "you're being aggressive" but doesn't feel aggressive, when a user asks why their "direct" approach isn't working.

#### Stories

**The Misread Negotiation (Affective Cue Error)**
> Voss was in a negotiation and threw out a figure. "I saw him kind of look off to the side and look back and accepted my offer." Voss read this as the person having more money available and felt he had left something on the table. "I made the mistake of not saying to them the appropriate thing for me to say at the time would have been, 'seems like something just crossed your mind.'" After the fact, he learned he was wrong -- "They were stretched to the limit. The look of hesitation didn't mean that they were holding stuff back."

*Teaching application:* When you see a behavioral shift during negotiation, never assign meaning without checking. Emotional self-management includes managing the impulse to interpret ambiguous signals through your own emotional lens. The correct response to any behavioral shift is a neutral observation: "Seems like something just crossed your mind." This checks your interpretation without projecting meaning.
*Source:* huberman

**The Lyft Driver State Change**
> Voss gets into a Lyft. The driver is hostile -- won't help with bags, won't open the trunk, is "seething unhappiness." Voss is annoyed but decides this negativity will make the ride worse and longer. He asks: "What do you love about driving for Lyft?" The driver "proceeds to unload on me, on all his personal struggles, that I feel like a complete jerk for being angry at him, at everything that he's going through."

*Teaching application:* Even when you are annoyed, a genuine curiosity question transforms both your state and theirs. The "what do you love about..." question accesses something deeper than the presenting emotion in the other person while simultaneously shifting your own state from irritation to genuine interest. This is curiosity-as-emotional-control in a low-stakes setting -- daily practice for high-stakes performance.
*Source:* huberman

**"I Want You to Be Scared" (Kidnapping Families)**
> When advising a family during a kidnapping, Voss tells them upfront: "There's a really good chance we're going to do something that's going to scare the hell out of you." Then: "I'm going to tell you in advance, I want you to be scared. It's going to help us in a negotiation." The reasoning: if the family sounds scared, kidnappers think they have the upper hand and relax -- fewer threats, less urgency. Paradoxically, telling someone to embrace their fear makes the fear dissipate: "As soon as you stop trying to suppress it, it's one of the keys to make it go away."

*Teaching application:* The prescribing-the-symptom technique served dual purposes: it tactically relaxed the kidnappers (authentic fear reads as genuine submission) AND psychologically reduced the family's internal experience of fear. When coaching someone in pre-negotiation anxiety, prescribe the symptom: "I want you to be nervous. It means you care. Let it work for you."
*Source:* jordan-165

**The Chatbot Superiority Insight**
> About 5 years before the Lex Fridman interview, a company approached Voss about negotiation chatbots. "They said, in point of fact, we already spoke to the people that are teaching, quote, the Harvard methodology, and the rational approach to negotiation just doesn't work... But we're showing in around about 80% of the interactions a higher success outcome with these chatbots." The reason: "The chatbots were never in a bad mood. And you could reach out for chatbot in the middle of the night."

*Teaching application:* The primary advantage was not intelligence but emotional consistency. Validates the thesis that your own negative emotional state is the primary obstacle to good outcomes. "Negotiations go bad when people are in a negative frame of mind." Managing your own emotions is not optional soft-skill work -- it is the highest-leverage variable in the entire system.
*Source:* lex-fridman
