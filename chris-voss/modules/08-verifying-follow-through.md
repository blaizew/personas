### Module 8: Verifying Whether Someone Will Actually Follow Through

You got "yes." They agreed. They signed. They shook hands. Now the question Voss asks: will they actually DO it? His answer, backed by data from a major telecom: 50% of the time, they won't. This module is about testing whether a commitment is real before you invest in execution -- using calibrated questions, the Rule of Three, pronoun analysis, and vision diagnostics.

---

#### "Yes Is Nothing Without How" (evolved: "Yes Is Nothing, How Is Everything") [14/16] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Yes is nothing without how. And yes at its very best is only temporary aspiration, it's aspirational, it's usually counterfeit."
> *Source: lex-fridman*

> "I can give you fake yeses all day long and you're never gonna get what you wanted."
> *Source: knowledge-project*

> "When Never Split the Difference was first published, The Black Swan Group used to say, 'Yes is nothing without how.' Since then, we've updated our approach. Now, we teach that yes is nothing, and how is everything."
> *Source: black-swan-newsletter*

**What it means in practice:** An agreement without implementation detail is worthless. Every "yes" must be followed by "how" to test whether the yes is real. The "how" question forces the counterpart to think through the operational steps of execution -- and if they can't or won't, you've exposed a fake commitment before it costs you time and resources.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **50% Implementation Failure at a Major Telecom (knowledge-project, jordan-678, spycast):** Voss's company was competing for a negotiation contract with a major telecommunications company. They discovered that "fully 50% of the deals they sign never get implemented. Half." Their signatures were meaningless. The problem wasn't making deals -- it was deals that never got executed because nobody asked "how."

2. **The Drug Dealer Proof of Life (spycast, lewis-howes):** "Hey, dog. How do I know she's alive?" -- and "there's this long silence, and a drug dealer on the other end of the phone says, 'I'll put her on the phone.'" The "how" question instantly shifted who had the upper hand.

3. **Ecuador Kidnapping (jordan-678, spycast):** The wife kept asking "How do I know my husband's even alive?" This forced the kidnappers into repeated internal conferences, changing the entire power dynamic. The captors started calling him "Don Pepe" -- respect emerging from the process of having to repeatedly prove he was okay.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Any time someone celebrates getting agreement -- a signed contract, a verbal yes, a handshake. The coaching question is: "Great, you got yes. Now what's the how? Did they describe what implementation looks like? Did they walk through the steps?" If not, the yes may be counterfeit.

#### The Rule of Three (Triple Agreement) [5/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "It's very hard to fake agreement convincingly three times."
> *Source: book (Never Split the Difference)*

**What it means in practice:** Get agreement three times via three different methods in one conversation:
1. Direct statement of commitment ("We'll have this done by Friday")
2. A label or summary that triggers "that's right" ("So it sounds like you've got the team aligned and the resources allocated")
3. A calibrated "how" question about implementation ("How are you going to handle the handoff to the operations team?")

If they can't produce all three -- if they falter on the implementation question, if they give "you're right" instead of "that's right," if they deflect from specifics -- you've identified non-commitment before it becomes a failed execution.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Book framework (book):** The exact three-step structure is laid out as a formal verification protocol. Each step tests a different layer of commitment -- verbal, emotional, and operational.

2. **Huberman interview:** Voss explains that the brain cannot fake genuine agreement three different ways in succession without revealing the deception through tonal shifts, pronoun changes, or vagueness.

3. **Black-swan-newsletter:** The upgraded philosophy -- "Yes is nothing, how is everything" -- makes the Rule of Three even more pointed: the how question IS the real commitment test. Steps 1 and 2 are warmups; step 3 is the diagnostic.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone reports getting easy agreement. Especially when the other side said yes quickly, enthusiastically, or without questions. Easy yeses are the most dangerous -- they often signal someone who wants to end the conversation, not someone who's genuinely committed.

#### Vision Drives Decision (Implementation Diagnostic) [7/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Vision drives decision. You've thought it through in advance. What does letting the hostages go look like? If you have no intention of ever releasing the hostage? If I follow through, then you're not going to be able to answer the question and you're probably going to throw it back on me really quickly."
> *Source: huberman*

**What it means in practice:** Ask counterparts what implementation looks like. If they can describe it clearly -- the steps, the sequence, the logistics -- they've envisioned it and are likely to follow through. If they deflect, vague-out, or throw the question back on you, they have no plan to comply. People who haven't thought through the vision of follow-through won't follow through.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Hostage context (huberman):** "What does letting the hostages go look like?" If a hostage-taker can describe the process of release in detail, they've genuinely contemplated it. If they can't -- if they change the subject or get angry -- they never planned to release anyone.

2. **Business application (high-performance):** "Vision drives decision" as a two-word framework. People don't decide based on logic or self-interest alone -- they decide based on whether they can SEE the outcome. If they can't paint the picture, the decision hasn't been made.

3. **Tom Girardi's method (lex-fridman, lewis-howes):** "Every single time you go to pick a point in the future where we're both happy 10 years, 20 years from now, and let's work our way back." When the counterpart can describe that future in detail, you know they've committed. When they can't, they haven't.

**When to deploy in coaching:** After getting agreement, ask: "What does this look like when it's working?" or "Walk me through how the first week goes." Their ability to describe concrete steps is the diagnostic. Vagueness = non-commitment.

#### Warning Signs of Non-Commitment [6/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Somebody says 'you're right' to you, what they're really asking you to do is shut up and go away."
> *Source: talks-at-google*

> "I'll try" is "the classic American lie."
> *Source: diary-of-a-ceo*

**What it means in practice:** Two phrases signal you haven't achieved genuine buy-in:

1. **"You're right"** = disengagement, not agreement. They're tired of the conversation and want it to end. Contrast with "That's right," which signals genuine epiphany and neurochemical bonding.

2. **"I'll try"** = planning to fail. Different from "I don't think it's gonna happen, but I'll check" -- which is honest about the unlikelihood while offering genuine advocacy. "I'll try" is the polite way of saying "this isn't going to happen and I know it."

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Son's Football Position (talks-at-google):** Voss's son kept saying "you're right" about switching from lineman to linebacker -- and then doing it wrong the next day. "You're right" = intellectual acknowledgment without behavioral change. The breakthrough came only when his son said "that's right."

2. **The "I'll Try" Diagnosis (diary-of-a-ceo):** Voss explicitly contrasts "I'll try" (lie) with "I don't think it's gonna happen, but I'll check" (honest non-commitment). His alternative in the live demo: "I basically said, I don't think it's gonna happen. But I'll check, because I'm trying to shift us out of an adversarial into a collaborative conversation... I don't want to mislead you."

3. **Book framework:** The book identifies "counterfeit yes" as one of three types of yes (commitment, confirmation, counterfeit). Most yeses in negotiation are counterfeit -- said to end the conversation or shop terms to competitors.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone reports "they agreed!" -- ask what exact words they used. If the words were "you're right" or "I'll try" or "sure, sounds good," flag that these are non-commitment signals. Coach them to push for "that's right" or specific implementation language.

#### Pronoun Usage as Commitment Indicator [5/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "The most powerful negotiators in business will always use plural pronouns because they're trying to hide their influence on their side."
> *Source: jordan-678*

**What it means in practice:** Listen to pronoun usage as a diagnostic:

- **Heavy "I/me/my"** = low importance in the decision chain. They're projecting authority they don't have. ("I can totally make this happen" from someone who can't.)
- **Avoidance of first-person ("we," "they," "the team")** = genuine decision-maker hiding behind the group to avoid being pinned down.
- **Can't say "I will do X"** with a first-person pronoun = they may not own the implementation. If commitment language stays abstract ("it'll get done," "the team will handle it"), push for personal ownership.

The Pinocchio Effect extends this: liars use more words, more third-person pronouns, and more complex sentences. They distance themselves from their statements. Truth is economical and uses first-person naturally.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Chase Manhattan Bank Robber (jordan-678, jordan-165, diary-of-a-ceo):** The mastermind used plural pronouns obsessively -- "There are all these other people with me" / "these other guys they're so much more dangerous" / "let me put you on hold because THEY're going to overhear." He was the architect but appeared least powerful.

2. **Business application (jordan-678):** "The harder they work to make themselves seem powerless, the more influential they are." In business, anyone constantly deflecting to bosses, committees, or partners is likely the actual decision-maker avoiding commitment.

3. **Book framework:** "Liars use more words than truth-tellers and more third-person pronouns. They employ complex sentences to persuade suspicious listeners."

**When to deploy in coaching:** After getting a commitment, review the language used. Did they say "I will" or "we'll see" or "the team should be able to"? Personal pronouns + specific action = genuine ownership. Vague team language = diluted accountability.

**50% Implementation Failure (Telecom)**

> Voss's company was competing for a negotiation contract with a major telecommunications company. Through the process, they discovered that "fully 50% of the deals they sign never get implemented. Half." Their signatures were meaningless. They were "killing themselves on implementation... destroying their profit over bad implementation or even not even implementing at all."

*Teaching application:* Half of signed deals never execute because nobody asked "how." The real cost isn't failed negotiations -- it's deals that get signed and then die in implementation.
*Sources:* knowledge-project, jordan-678, spycast

---

**Venezuela FARC Hostage Exchange**

> Voss's FBI unit worked a case in Venezuela where the FARC held a hostage. They agreed on an exchange point at a river crossing that was a significant distance from where the hostage was being held. The logic: "They're not going to drag the hostage all the way to this river crossing if they're not going to let them go. It's just too much effort." Once there: "Even if they're ambivalent, once they get there, if they've gone through all the effort to get to the meeting location and the hostage is there, we've now just increased the chances significantly."
>
> The final step: "The very last thing we'd always have the family get the bad guys to say at last, not first, but last, was we'd actually get a verbal promise to let them go again at the end, because we've been talking to them long enough. At this point in time, we got a pretty good idea of what they sound like when they're lying and what they tell in the truth."

*Teaching application:* (1) Investment creates commitment -- make the other side invest in the outcome physically. (2) Get verbal commitments LAST, not first, because by then you know their truth-telling baseline. (3) Behavioral investment (physically dragging someone to a location) is more reliable than verbal commitment alone.
*Source:* huberman

**The Barricade Negotiator Who's Too Good**

> Voss is listening to a barricade situation where the negotiator and the subject are having such a great conversation that the subject doesn't want it to end. Voss turns to the commander: "He isn't coming out as long as he gets to talk to Jordan... He love Jordan. He's having a great time."

*Teaching application:* Rapport is not the goal -- resolution is. Good conversation does not equal genuine commitment to action. Sometimes the person agrees with everything because they're enjoying the interaction, not because they plan to follow through.
