# Module 1: Getting Buy-In from Resistant People

When someone is resisting, dismissing, or giving empty agreement without follow-through, the goal is not to argue harder -- it is to make them feel so deeply understood that collaboration becomes neurochemically inevitable.

---

## "That's Right" vs. "You're Right" [14/16] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

The two most important words in negotiation are "that's right" -- and the two most dangerous are "you're right." They sound similar but produce opposite outcomes.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "When somebody feels like what you've said is completely their position, they say that's right. Not you're right, but that's right." (Lex Fridman, 37:38)

> "When you feel completely understood by me, you bond to me." (Lex Fridman, 38:10)

> "The other way you know you're wrong is when you're trying to explain something to somebody and they look at you and say, you're right. Somebody says you're right to you, what they're really asking you to do is shut up and go away." (Talks at Google)

**What it means in practice:** "That's right" signals a genuine epiphany -- a moment where the counterpart recognizes that you have articulated their position more clearly than they could articulate it themselves. This triggers a neurochemical cascade: oxytocin (bonding) and serotonin (satisfaction). "You're right" signals the opposite -- disengagement disguised as agreement. Get "that's right" by summarizing their world -- the facts PLUS how they see the facts -- until they affirm voluntarily.

**Examples:**

1. **Philippines/Schilling kidnapping (Talks at Google):** A $10 million ransom demand. Negotiator Benji summarized the terrorist leader Sabaya's worldview until Sabaya said "that's right." Result: "$10 million to 0 in one conversation, space of about 5 minutes." Two weeks later, Sabaya called Benji: "I don't know what you said to me on the phone. I was going to kill Jeffrey. He kept me from doing it. They should promote you." Even when they know what you did, they're still OK with it.

2. **Son's football position change (Talks at Google):** Voss's son was moved from lineman to linebacker but kept hitting instead of dodging. Every time the coach explained, his son said "you're right" -- and did it wrong the next day. Voss articulated what his son couldn't: "You think that dodging a block is unmanly. You feel like getting out of the way of somebody who's trying to hit you makes you a coward." Son got quiet, then said "that's right." Started ducking blocks the next day. Later told Voss: "I never would have been able to explain that for myself. Until you said it, I didn't know it was true."

3. **Black Swan Newsletter (Ghost Reengagement Sequence):** After getting a ghoster to respond with "Have you given up on X?", immediately deliver a Summary of their perspective -- past, present, future -- until they say "that's right." Only then proceed.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user reports that someone keeps saying they agree but nothing changes. When a conversation is stuck in circular argument. When the user has been making logical arguments that aren't landing. Direct them to stop arguing and start summarizing the other person's world until they hear "that's right."

## Tactical Empathy / Labeling Emotions [16/16] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

The foundation of the entire Black Swan method -- present in every source, central to every technique.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Not agreeing or even liking the other side. Don't even gotta like them, don't gotta agree with them. Just straight understanding where they're coming from and articulating it, which requires no agreement whatsoever." (Lex Fridman, 10:13)

> "My experience as a layman, is that empathy's not got a downside. That you don't need me to act like I'm on your side for us to make a great deal." (Lex Fridman, 16:00)

> "We defined tactical empathy as simply taking an inventory of the perspective of the person you're talking to -- especially the parts that we don't like, and then telling them what it is. Describing it back to them calmly. No denials, no disagreements." (TEDx talk)

**What it means in practice:** Labels are statements beginning with "It seems like...", "It sounds like...", or "It looks like..." that identify and articulate the other person's emotional state or perspective. The mechanism is neurochemical: labeling an emotion reduces cortisol (stress hormone) and triggers oxytocin (bonding). It is involuntary -- the counterpart cannot prevent the calming effect. Specificity matters enormously. "You seem upset" can backfire. "It seems like you're afraid that if you come out, there will be consequences" builds trust because it demonstrates genuine understanding.

**Examples:**

1. **Egyptian Muslims testifying (Lex Fridman, Huberman):** Voss needed Arab Muslims to testify against a Muslim cleric. He sat with them and said: "You believe that there's been a succession of American governments for the last 200 years that are anti-Islamic." They shook their heads -- "yeah." He never said he agreed. Never gave his point of view. "I was so unafraid of their beliefs that I was willing to just state them and not disagree or contradict." Every single one testified.

2. **Three Fugitives Barricade (Book):** Three fugitives in an apartment, SWAT in the hallway. Voss addressed their specific emotional states: "It seems like you're afraid that if you come out, there will be consequences from the people you owe." Six hours of precision labeling -- peaceful surrender without a shot.

3. **Huberman -- proactive inoculation:** "If I call out a negative that's not there, it doesn't plant it, it actually inoculates you from it." Labeling emotions that haven't surfaced yet prevents them from emerging rather than creating them.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Always. This is the universal starting point. When the user is preparing for any resistant conversation, guide them to list the emotions the other person is likely feeling and construct specific labels. Remind them: empathy requires no agreement, no liking, no sympathy.

## Calibrated "How" and "What" Questions [16/16] [SIGNATURE]

The tool that shifts the problem-solving burden onto the counterpart while giving them the illusion of control.

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "You've engineered a situation in which your formerly recalcitrant counterpart is now using his mental and emotional resources to overcome your challenges." (Book)

> "Yes is nothing without how. And yes at its very best is only temporary aspiration, it's aspirational, it's usually counterfeit." (Lex Fridman, 26:38)

> "The how question causes a unification on the other side." (Jordan Harbinger 678, 00:11:22)

**What it means in practice:** Questions beginning with "how" and "what" force slow thinking (Kahneman's System 2) in the counterpart. They shift cognitive burden without being confrontational. Never use "why" (triggers defensiveness). The meta-question is always "What information am I trying to extract?" -- the specific question is just the delivery vehicle. Follow every "yes" with "how" to test whether it's genuine.

**Examples:**

1. **Ecuador kidnapping (Jordan 678, Spycast):** "How do we know he's alive?" forced the kidnapper's negotiator to physically travel back to the jungle and hold conferences with the group. "We'd never forced the other side into an internal collaboration that favored us ever before."

2. **Live hostage roleplay (Diary of a CEO):** Bartlett demands a car in 60 seconds. Voss responds with "How am I supposed to do that?" After two minutes, Bartlett asks "How long do you need?" -- extending his own deadline without Voss ever saying no. Bartlett's internal monologue: "All the questions were making me ponder... making me abandon my focus."

3. **50% implementation failure (Knowledge Project, Jordan 678):** At a major telecom, fully 50% of signed deals never got implemented. The cause: nobody followed "yes" with "how." "I can give you fake yeses all day long and you're never gonna get what you wanted."

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user has gotten verbal agreement but suspects non-commitment. When they need to say no without saying no. When they need the other person to own the solution rather than resist an imposed one.

## Guided Discovery Over Advice-Giving [8/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "If they discover it, it's their idea and they're going to do it. If you offer it, you're giving them advice, and they got no emotional ownership." (Diary of a CEO)

> "If I want to get a point across to you in a didactic exchange where you're just listening, you're just passive, you're not engaged. I got to tell you 19 times... If I slow down, if I use questions to affect your thinking, if I shape your thinking so that you discover it -- you discover it faster and it's your idea." (Jordan Harbinger 165, 00:40:54)

**What it means in practice:** Telling requires 19 repetitions for retention. Guided discovery requires one -- and the person believes it's their idea. Use calibrated questions, labels, and silence to lead the counterpart toward the conclusion you want them to reach. They adopt it faster and execute with conviction because it carries emotional ownership.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is frustrated that their advice isn't being taken. When they keep explaining the same thing. When they need buy-in for implementation, not just agreement.

## Vision of the Future / Work Backward [10/16]

**Core idea in Voss's words:**

> "Let's look at 10 years from now where we could both be in a phenomenal place together. Now let's work our way back from there." (Lex Fridman, 17:22, attributed to Tom Girardi)

> "I want you to live. You know, I wanna see you survive this... Let's pick a point in the future that we're both good with and then we work our way back." (Lex Fridman)

> "Vision drives decision." (High Performance Podcast)

**What it means in practice:** People endure anything if the future portends what they want. Shift the temporal frame from present conflict to future collaboration. Paint what the world looks like when this works out for both parties. Applied from billion-dollar lawsuits (Tom Girardi's signature move) to hostage barricades ("I want you to live").

**Examples:**

1. **Harlem high-rise standoff (High Performance Podcast):** For 6 hours, Voss painted a vision of safe surrender to an apartment where no one responded. A sniper finally reported a curtain moving. Five minutes later, three fugitives surrendered silently in exactly the manner described. When asked why: "Well, you said you'd never go away, so we finally believed you and decided to come out."

2. **Diary of a CEO -- no-leverage negotiation:** "How do you negotiate when you're not going to give them anything? You help them see a different vision of the future... what you really want them to see is a vision of future where they live, and then you're hoping the survival instinct kicks in."

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the conversation is stuck in present-state adversarial framing. When the user and their counterpart are arguing about who's right rather than where they're going together.

## Stories

**Philippines/Schilling "That's Right" ($10M to $0)** [14/16] [SIGNATURE]
> One of the turning points was getting the terrorists to say "that's right" on the phone. Changed everything. There was a $10 million ransom demand on the table, going from $10 million to 0 in one conversation, space of about 5 minutes. The bad guy was a murdering, raping, head-chopping bad guy named Sabaya. Two weeks later, Sabaya called Benji back: "Hey, have you been promoted yet? I don't know what you said to me on the phone. I was going to kill Jeffrey. He kept me from doing it. They should promote you." Even when they know, they're still OK with it.
*Teaching application:* When negotiations are deadlocked, stop conceding and start summarizing. Get "that's right" to transform the dynamic. It works even on the most extreme adversaries.
*Source:* Talks at Google, Lex Fridman, Book

**Son's Football Position Change** [4/16]
> His son wouldn't adapt from lineman to linebacker. Every time the coach explained, his son would say "you're right" -- and do it wrong the next day. Finally Voss articulated what his son couldn't say: "You think that dodging a block is unmanly. You feel like getting out of the way of somebody who's trying to hit you makes you a coward." Son got quiet, then said "that's right." Started ducking blocks the next day. Later: "I never would have been able to explain that for myself. Until you said it, I didn't know it was true. And never in a million years would I have thought doing it was acting like a coward. But it was absolutely what was driving me."
*Teaching application:* When someone intellectually agrees ("you're right") but won't change behavior, the block is emotional and unarticulated. Articulate what they cannot say about themselves.
*Source:* Talks at Google

**Harlem High-Rise Standoff (6 Hours)** [3/16]
> For 6 hours, Voss talked to what everyone assumed was an empty apartment. He repeatedly described what it would look like when they came out safely -- painting a vision. Then a sniper reports a curtain moving. Without ever saying a word, the door opens and hands emerge exactly as described. When asked why she didn't respond: "We were hoping you would go away." When asked why she came out: "Well, you said you'd never go away, so we finally believed you and decided to come out."
*Teaching application:* Silence from the other side doesn't mean they're not listening. Persistence + vision-painting works even without any verbal response.
*Source:* High Performance Podcast

**Egyptian Muslims Testifying** [5/16]
> "I would say to them, you believe that there's been a succession of American governments for the last 200 years that are anti-Islamic. And they'd shake their head and go, yeah. And that'd be the start of the conversation. That's empathy. I never said I agreed. I never said I disagreed. But I showed them that I wasn't afraid of their beliefs. I was so unafraid of them that I was willing to just state them and not disagree or contradict." Every single one testified.
*Teaching application:* Empathy without agreement builds trust across deep divides. You never have to argue someone into cooperating -- you just need to demonstrate fearless understanding of their worldview.
*Source:* Lex Fridman, Huberman
