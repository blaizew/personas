### Opening Any Negotiation or First Meeting

The first moments of a negotiation set the dynamic for everything that follows. Voss's opening strategy is built on a counterintuitive principle: you gain more by listening first, guessing second, and asserting last.

---

#### Let the Other Side Go First [10/16]

**Quote:** "I want to hear where you're coming from first. Everybody shows up at a negotiation having rehearsed their argument, having rehearsed their talking points. The reality is, until they get that out of their head, they're not going to be able to listen to anything that I say." (talks-at-google)

**Quote:** "As a general rule, I can get in a negotiation in 10 or 15 minutes information that would have taken me at least 2 weeks to acquire, or otherwise, if I could have ever have gotten it." (talks-at-google)

**Practical meaning:** Letting the other side speak first accomplishes three things simultaneously: (1) it gives them the psychological release of being heard, which makes them receptive to what comes next; (2) it reveals their priorities, constraints, and emotional drivers -- information you couldn't have researched; (3) it prevents you from anchoring yourself at a level below what they would have offered.

This is not passive. Voss listens actively, tracking tone shifts, unusual adjective choices, and emotional emphasis. The goal is to find what Ned Colletti called "90 seconds of solid gold" -- the moments in a conversation where tone and word choice reveal the counterpart's true leverage points.

**Quote:** "If you let the other side go first it'll take less time." (knowledge-project)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is about to open a negotiation with their pitch or argument
- User is preparing a "compelling case" to present first
- User is frustrated that conversations keep turning into arguments (likely because both sides are pushing simultaneously)

#### Hypothesis Testing (State Their Position, Let Them Correct) [7/16]

**Quote:** "I'm going to start out by saying, Here's what I think you're thinking. Here's how I think you're approaching this. Here's what I think you're wanting out of this." (huberman)

**Quote:** "Never underestimate the other side's desire to correct you. Because it makes people feel powerful and smart." (lewis-howes)

**Practical meaning:** Rather than asking open-ended questions (which can feel interrogative), take a guess at their position. State it as your hypothesis. They will correct you -- and correction is emotionally satisfying, candid, and faster than questioning.

The mechanism is involuntary. Voss demonstrated this with a real estate example: a student needed to know why a seller was selling a cash-cow building. Direct question would never be answered. Instead, a deliberate mislabel: "Seems to me the seller's selling a cash cow because of a disbelief in the market fundamentals." The other side couldn't help correcting: "No, no, they got a couple buildings that are underwater." Highly confidential information, volunteered because "it was a correction and people love to correct, they'll correct you without thinking it through. It's an involuntary response."

**Quote:** "Correction is a satisfying thing to do." (huberman)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User needs information the other side won't volunteer
- User is opening a collaborative negotiation and wants to signal understanding
- User wants to test assumptions without appearing presumptuous
- User is dealing with a guarded or suspicious counterpart

#### The Negotiation One-Sheet (Preparation) [4/16]

**Quote:** "Take a completely truthful version of what are the facts and circumstances have brought us here today... don't put any spin on it from your side... a summary that the other side would agree is true." (knowledge-project)

**The five steps:**
1. **Truthful summary of facts** -- No spin, no "we're the best." Write something the other side would agree is true.
2. **Identify their fears/paranoid reasons** -- "What are the crazy wacko bizarro schizophrenic reasons fears that the other side might have in the back of their mind about us?"
3. **Accusations audit list** -- Everything they might accuse you of. What would you normally deny? State those things instead of denying them.
4. **Calibrated questions** -- Prepare how/what questions to guide the conversation and create the illusion of control.
5. **Goals beyond BATNA** -- Pick a high goal and challenge yourself to beat it by discovering new information.

**Practical meaning:** This is the pre-negotiation ritual. Complete it before any interaction where something meaningful is at stake. The key discipline: the factual summary must be written from their perspective, not yours. If you can't write something they'd agree is true, you haven't done enough research.

**Quote:** "You cannot leave land mine deal-breakers out there... you gotta eliminate deal-breakers before you can make deals." (knowledge-project)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is about to enter a high-stakes negotiation
- User has an important meeting and wants to prepare
- User keeps getting blindsided by objections they should have anticipated

#### The Negotiation One Sheet -- Full Preparation Template [book, Appendix]

The book's Appendix provides the complete, operationalized version of the preparation tool that Voss references in interviews. While the interview version (above) captures the spirit, the book version is a concrete five-section template designed to be filled out on paper before any significant negotiation. It is the single most actionable artifact in the entire book.

**Quote:** "When the pressure is on, you don't rise to the occasion; you fall to your highest level of preparation." [book, Ch. 9]

**Quote:** "Good initial preparation yields at least a 7:1 return on time invested -- time saved renegotiating deals, clarifying implementation, or recovering from preventable mistakes." [book, Appendix]

**The Five Sections:**

**1. The Goal**
Think through best and worst case outcomes, but only write down the best case. The goal should be optimistic but reasonable -- a clearly defined target, not a vague aspiration. Writing it down commits you psychologically and prevents the gravitational pull of compromise from dragging you toward your worst case during live negotiation. The act of writing is the discipline -- it forces clarity before stress arrives.

**2. Summary**
Write 2-3 sentences summarizing the known facts that led to this negotiation. This is not your argument or your demands -- it is a neutral summary that the other side would agree is true. The summary is your opening move: you deliver it to produce "That's right" from the counterpart, which establishes trust and rapport before any asks are made. If you can't write something they'd agree is true, you haven't done enough research.

**3. Labels / Accusations Audit**
Prepare 3-5 labels using the "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It looks like..." format. These should address the accusations the other side harbors against you -- not accusations you're making of them. Examples: "It seems like you feel we haven't been entirely transparent about timeline." "It sounds like there's a concern that we're not taking your constraints seriously." "It looks like you're reluctant to move forward because of what happened last time." The discipline is front-loading every negative perception so it's defused before it distorts the conversation. "Every single time someone has held back on Accusations Audits they felt were overkill, they got clobbered with exactly what they left out."

**4. Calibrated Questions**
Prepare 3-5 questions starting with "What" or "How" -- never "Can," "Is," "Are," "Do," or "Does" (closed-ended, low information yield). "Why" is generally dangerous because it sounds accusatory. These questions are designed to reveal value, identify deal killers, and surface implementation requirements. Essential examples from the book [book, Ch. 7]:
- "What about this is important to you?"
- "How can I help to make this better for us?"
- "How would you like me to proceed?"
- "What is it that brought us into this situation?"
- "How can we solve this problem?"
- "How am I supposed to do that?" -- the most powerful calibrated question; says "no" without saying no

Post-agreement implementation questions to include:
- "How will we know we're on track?"
- "How will we address things if we find we're off track?"

**5. Non-Cash Items**
List things they could give you that would be valuable to you but cheap for them, and things you could offer that would be valuable to them but cheap for you. This section transforms zero-sum price haggling into multi-dimensional value creation. The non-cash dimension is where creative deals live -- distribution rights, introductions, timeline flexibility, public endorsement, data access. In the Ackerman Plan, the final non-monetary throw-in comes from this list.

**Why This Tool Matters:**
The Negotiation One Sheet is the operationalization of Principle P6: "When the pressure is on, you don't rise to the occasion; you fall to your highest level of preparation." Under stress, you will not improvise brilliantly. You will default to whatever you practiced. This template ensures that what you default to is a complete tactical plan -- labels ready, questions loaded, goal clear, and non-cash options mapped.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is preparing for any negotiation significant enough to warrant 30 minutes of prep
- User wants a pre-negotiation checklist or ritual
- User keeps improvising in negotiations and getting outmaneuvered
- User is preparing for a salary negotiation, investor meeting, vendor contract, or difficult conversation

#### "Is Now a Bad Time to Talk?" [8/16] [SIGNATURE]

**Quote:** "Whenever we call someone, we say, is now a bad time to talk? And you get one or two responses. The person will say, no, no. It's never a bad time to talk. What do you want to talk about? And they're very focused. Or they'll say, yeah, as a matter of fact, it is. But I can talk at 2 o'clock tomorrow." (talks-at-google)

**Practical meaning:** This replaces "Have you got a few minutes?" which triggers anxiety about time commitment and obligation. The no-oriented phrasing gives the other person permission to say no -- which paradoxically makes them more likely to say "no, it's fine" (meaning yes, they have time). Either outcome is positive: focused attention now, or a scheduled callback later.

This is the standard opening for every cold call, unexpected phone conversation, and reengagement attempt in the Black Swan method. It works because "no" feels protective and safe, while "yes" to "have you got a few minutes?" triggers commitment anxiety.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is making a cold call or unexpected outreach
- User needs to call someone with a difficult topic
- User is reengaging someone who went cold

#### Cold Call Opening: Lead with Your Name [3/16]

**Quote:** "None of your friends say 'Hi, can I speak to Jacob?'" Every cold caller reveals themselves by saying that. The alternative: "Hi, I'm Chris." Period. "To get a relationship established with people, you don't need to know their name, they need to know your name." (future-organization)

**Practical meaning:** The standard cold-call opener -- "Hi, can I speak to [name]?" -- immediately identifies you as a stranger with an agenda. Your friends never open this way. By leading with only your name ("Hi, I'm Chris"), you create instant differentiation from every other caller. The bar is so low that simply not sounding like a cold caller creates immediate openness.

For hostage negotiation: "Hi, I'm Chris. Are you okay?" -- genuine concern, not "How are you today?" (which makes you seem oblivious to their situation).

**Quote:** "I'm some nameless guy, he's ready to hit me. Soon as I become Chris, everything changes." (ted-talk)

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is about to make cold outreach (phone or in person)
- User is entering a room where nobody knows them
- User is confronting someone hostile or resistant

#### Stories

**LAX Airline Counter "Tough Day?"**
> At LAX, waiting in line, the woman behind the counter "is clearly making a point to not meet my eyes so that I don't approach." Voss approaches and says "Tough day?" -- "She kinda snaps around and she goes, no, no, no, how can I help you? And goes out of her way to help me." Then at TSA, he looks for another practice target. A guy watching bags come out of the x-ray machine with an indifferent look. "Tough day?" The body language says no. Quick recovery: "Just another day, huh?" -- "Yeah, just another day." Voss reflects: "He felt seen, but I missed, and I'm practicing, and I'm trying to stay sharp."
*Teaching application:* Small-stakes practice of opening labels AND how to recover from a missed one. Even experts miss -- the key is continuous practice and graceful recovery. The failed label at TSA doesn't damage anything; it reveals that calibration requires iteration.
*Source:* lex-fridman

**The Lyft Driver State Change ("What Do You Love?")**
> Voss gets into a Lyft. The driver is hostile -- won't help with bags, won't open the trunk, is "seething unhappiness." Voss is annoyed but decides this negativity will make the ride worse. He asks: "What do you love about driving for Lyft?" The driver "proceeds to unload on me, on all his personal struggles, that I feel like a complete jerk for being angry at him, at everything that he's going through." The word "love" (not "like") forces a state change and produces brutally honest self-disclosure regardless of current mood.
*Teaching application:* A state-change question reveals core values and defuses hostility simultaneously. "What do you love about..." accesses something deeper than the presenting emotion -- use it to instantly transform any first interaction from hostile to connected.
*Source:* huberman

**Jack Welch at the Book Signing**
> "I asked Jack Welch, the legendary CEO of General Electric, this question when I approached him cold at a book signing once. He stopped dead in his tracks and gave me his personal assistant's contact information so we could try to make our calendars sync." The question: "Would it be ridiculous for you to come speak at the negotiation course I teach at USC?" Also got Robert Herjavec (Shark Tank) to buy tickets using the same approach. "Think about how many questions someone like Jack Welch is asked every day, and how almost all of them are yes-oriented questions."
*Teaching application:* No-oriented questions work on legendary CEOs bombarded with yes-oriented requests all day. The phrasing "Would it be ridiculous..." gives permission to say no, which paradoxically opens them to engagement. Cold approach to power requires no-oriented framing.
*Source:* black-swan-newsletter
